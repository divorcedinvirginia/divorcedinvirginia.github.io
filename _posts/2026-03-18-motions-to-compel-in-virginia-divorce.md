Discovery in a contested Virginia divorce is built on a presumption of good-faith participation: both sides issue requests, both sides respond, and the process moves forward. That presumption is violated in a significant number of cases.

Sometimes the non-compliance is passive — responses arrive late, documents are incomplete, or interrogatory answers are so vague as to be useless. Sometimes it is active — a party stonewalls, refuses outright, or produces records that are obviously sanitized. Either way, the mechanism for forcing compliance is the same: a motion to compel.

Understanding how motions to compel work, what triggers them, what the process looks like, and what consequences follow when a court grants one is essential knowledge for anyone in contested divorce litigation.

---

## What a Motion to Compel Is

A motion to compel is a formal request to the court asking a judge or commissioner to order the other party to comply with a specific discovery obligation. It is filed when:

- A party fails to respond to interrogatories or requests for production within the required time period (21 days in Virginia)
- A party responds with objections that are improper, boilerplate, or unsupported
- A party produces documents that are obviously incomplete or that omit records they are known to possess
- A party provides interrogatory answers that are evasive, non-responsive, or so vague as to be meaningless
- A party fails to appear for a properly noticed deposition
- A third-party subpoena recipient fails to comply

The motion asks the court to compel the non-complying party to do what they were already obligated to do — and, in most cases, to award the moving party the attorney's fees they incurred in bringing the motion.

---

## Before You Can File: The Meet-and-Confer Requirement

Virginia courts expect the parties to make a genuine effort to resolve discovery disputes on their own before involving the court. Before filing a motion to compel, your attorney must typically:

1. **Send a deficiency letter** identifying specifically what is missing, inadequate, or improperly objected to, and requesting supplementation by a stated deadline
2. **Follow up by phone or email** if the deadline passes without response
3. **Document these efforts** in detail, because the court will want to see that you tried to resolve the dispute without judicial intervention

This process is called meet and confer. It is not merely a formality — courts take it seriously. A motion to compel filed without evidence of a genuine attempt to resolve the dispute first can be denied or returned for further conferral, adding delay and cost.

In practice, meet and confer in divorce litigation often looks like this: your attorney sends a letter identifying specific deficiencies. The opposing attorney responds with a letter defending the responses or requesting additional time. Your attorney responds to that. After two or three rounds of letters — each of which is billed to you — the dispute either resolves or it does not. If it does not, the motion is filed.

The meet-and-confer correspondence itself is important. It creates the record that demonstrates the other side's bad faith, which the court considers when deciding whether to award fees.

## What the Motion Contains

A motion to compel in a Virginia divorce case typically includes:

**A factual background section** explaining the history of the discovery requests: when they were served, what was requested, when the deadline passed, what (if anything) was produced, and the nature of the deficiencies.

**The specific requests at issue** — usually quoted verbatim, along with the response (or non-response) received.

**Legal argument** explaining why each objection is improper, why each non-response violates Virginia's discovery rules, and why the court should order production.

**Documentation of meet-and-confer efforts** — copies of the deficiency letters and any correspondence that followed.

**A request for attorney's fees** under Virginia Rule of Court 4:12, which permits the court to award fees against a party who failed to comply with discovery without substantial justification.

**A proposed order** for the court to sign, specifying exactly what the non-complying party must produce, by when, and what fees are awarded.

## Common Scenarios That Lead to Motions to Compel

### Complete Non-Response

The responding party simply does not answer interrogatories or respond to document requests by the deadline. No request for extension, no partial production, nothing.

This is the strongest basis for a motion to compel and the scenario most likely to result in a fee award. Courts have little patience for complete non-response.

### Boilerplate Objections

The responding party answers every request with the same recycled objections: *"Overly broad, unduly burdensome, not reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence, and seeks information protected by attorney-client privilege."*

Courts in Virginia have grown increasingly skeptical of boilerplate objections. Under Virginia Rule of Court 4:1, objections must be specific — a party who objects must state the basis for the objection with particularity. A one-size-fits-all objection to an entire set of document requests does not meet this standard and is vulnerable to a motion to compel.

### Partial Production With Strategic Omissions

The responding party produces some documents — enough to say they complied — while omitting the ones most likely to be damaging. This is harder to prove than complete non-response, because the non-producing party can claim they simply do not have the missing records.

This is where forensic work becomes important. If bank statements show transfers to an account that has not appeared in the production, if a credit card statement references a bank account that has not been disclosed, or if tax returns identify income sources that have not been documented, your attorney can use those leads to demand the missing records specifically — and to make the case that the omission was deliberate.

### Evasive Interrogatory Answers

An interrogatory asks: *"Identify all financial accounts in your name or in which you have any interest, including account numbers, financial institutions, and current balances as of [date]."*

The answer comes back: *"Respondent has numerous accounts. Respondent will supplement this response as additional information becomes available."*

This is not a legally adequate answer, and the court will not be sympathetic to it if the pattern continues. A motion to compel asking for complete, verified supplementation is the appropriate response.

### Claiming Documents Do Not Exist When They Clearly Do

If your spouse claims in discovery that a particular account, asset, or document does not exist, and you have independent evidence that it does — a tax return that lists income from a business not disclosed, a bank statement that references an external transfer, a deed recorded in public records — the motion to compel should include that evidence.

A party who falsely denies the existence of documents in discovery is not just non-compliant — they are potentially committing perjury, since discovery responses are signed under oath. Courts treat this seriously.

### Deposition No-Shows

A party who is properly noticed for a deposition and fails to appear without prior arrangement or good cause has created a serious discovery violation. The motion to compel in this scenario asks the court to order the deposition to proceed by a specific date and to award fees for the wasted preparation time and any court reporter cancellation fees.

Repeated deposition no-shows, or a pattern of last-minute cancellations, can result in sanctions beyond fees — including adverse inferences or, in extreme cases, default.

## The Hearing on a Motion to Compel

Once filed, the motion is scheduled for a hearing before a judge or General District Court commissioner, depending on your jurisdiction and the issues involved. Both attorneys appear and argue their positions.

The non-complying party's attorney will typically argue one or more of the following:

- The requests were overly broad or burdensome and the objections were justified
- Production is in process and additional time is needed
- The documents sought do not exist or are not in the client's possession
- The meet-and-confer process was not completed in good faith by the moving party

Your attorney will counter with the specific record of non-compliance, the correspondence demonstrating good-faith efforts to resolve the dispute, and legal authority establishing that the objections were improper or the responses were inadequate.

**What the court can order:**

- That all responsive documents be produced by a specific date (often 10–14 days)
- That specific interrogatory responses be supplemented with complete, verified answers
- That a deposition proceed by a specified date
- That attorney's fees be paid by the non-complying party to the moving party
- That certain facts be deemed established — an adverse inference sanction, meaning the court will assume the undisclosed information was harmful to the non-producing party
- That specific claims or defenses be stricken if the party's non-compliance has prejudiced the other side's ability to present their case

The most common outcome of a successful motion to compel is an order requiring production within a specific window and an award of the reasonable attorney's fees incurred in bringing the motion. More severe sanctions are reserved for repeated or egregious violations.

## Attorney's Fees and the Substantial Justification Standard

Under Virginia Rule of Court 4:12, when a motion to compel is granted, the court **shall** award the moving party the reasonable expenses incurred in making the motion, including attorney's fees — unless the non-complying party's failure to respond was "substantially justified" or an award would be unjust under the circumstances.

"Substantial justification" is a meaningful standard. A party that had a legitimate legal reason for objecting — even if the court ultimately disagrees with that reason — may avoid a fee award. A party that simply ignored deadlines, gave boilerplate objections with no legitimate basis, or was clearly playing games with the process will not meet this standard.

In practice, fee awards on motions to compel range from a few hundred to several thousand dollars, depending on how much work the motion required and what the court determines is reasonable. They are meaningful but rarely fully compensatory for the actual disruption caused by discovery obstruction.

**Importantly:** Fee awards on individual motions to compel are different from a broader fee award for litigation misconduct. A party who engages in a sustained pattern of discovery obstruction — multiple motions, repeated non-compliance, clear bad faith — may face a much more significant fee award at the conclusion of the case under the court's inherent authority to sanction misconduct.

## When Motions to Compel Are Not Enough: Escalating Sanctions

A single motion to compel and a modest fee award rarely change the behavior of a party determined to obstruct discovery. For cases involving repeated, willful non-compliance, the escalating tools available to the court become increasingly significant.

### Monetary Sanctions Beyond Fee Awards

The court can impose monetary sanctions — beyond just the cost of the motion — as a penalty for egregious discovery conduct. These are distinct from fee awards and are intended to deter future misconduct, not merely compensate the other side.

### Adverse Inference Instructions

If a party destroys, conceals, or fails to produce evidence, the court can instruct the factfinder (the judge, in a bench trial) to draw an adverse inference — to assume that the missing evidence, if produced, would have been harmful to the non-producing party.

In a divorce case, an adverse inference in a financial context is powerful. If your spouse refuses to produce business records, an adverse inference instruction tells the court: *"You may assume the records your spouse refused to produce showed income greater than what they disclosed."* This can shift the entire financial picture of the case.

### Striking Pleadings

In cases of extreme, willful non-compliance, the court can strike all or part of the non-complying party's pleadings — meaning their affirmative claims or defenses on specific issues are dismissed. A party who refuses to produce financial records, for example, might have their claim to specific property stricken as a sanction.

### Default

In the most extreme cases, a party who systematically refuses to participate in discovery at all — failing to respond to any requests, failing to appear for depositions, defying court orders — can have a default entered against them, resulting in the case being resolved entirely in the other party's favor.

Default is rare in divorce cases because the non-complying party's attorney will almost always intervene before it reaches that point. But it exists, and courts have invoked it in cases of truly egregious conduct.

### Contempt of Court

Defying a court order to produce documents — as opposed to simply failing to respond to a discovery request — is contempt of court. A party held in contempt can face fines, and in Virginia, incarceration. The combination of a court order (issued on a successful motion to compel) and continued non-compliance creates a direct pathway to contempt proceedings.

Contempt is a serious tool, and most parties comply after a court order is entered. For those who do not, the consequences are severe enough that continued obstruction becomes untenable.

## The Cost of Bringing Motions to Compel

Every motion to compel has a price that must be paid upfront by the moving party, recovered only if the court awards fees — and even then, only partially.

**Typical costs to bring a motion to compel:**

- Attorney time drafting the motion and supporting memorandum: **$1,500–$4,000** depending on the complexity and the number of issues addressed
- Attorney time preparing for and attending the hearing: **$500–$2,000**
- Court filing fees: **$25–$100** depending on jurisdiction
- Meet-and-confer correspondence leading up to the motion: often **$500–$2,000** in attorney time that predates the motion itself

Total cost per motion: **$2,500–$8,000** in realistic terms, with fee recovery of some portion if the motion is granted.

In cases with a systematically obstructive opposing party, multiple motions to compel are common. The cumulative cost — even with partial fee recovery — can reach **$15,000–$40,000** in additional litigation expense beyond what the case would otherwise have cost.

This is one reason experienced attorneys urge their clients to pursue settlement when the picture becomes clear enough to negotiate intelligently. Discovery obstruction extends the case, increases costs for both parties, and rarely results in the obstructing party keeping the information they were trying to protect — because courts, having seen the pattern, draw their own conclusions.

## What to Do If You Are Facing Discovery Obstruction

If you are the party whose requests are being stonewalled, the practical steps are:

1. **Document everything from day one.** Keep every letter, every email, and every record of every deadline that was missed. Your motion will be stronger with a clear, well-documented timeline.

2. **Give your attorney clear information about what you know exists.** If you know there is an account your spouse has not disclosed, tell your attorney why you know that and what evidence you have. This makes the motion more specific and harder to defend against.

3. **Be patient with the meet-and-confer process — but not indefinitely.** The process exists for a reason and courts expect it. But if it becomes clear that further correspondence is only buying more delay, tell your attorney you are ready to move forward with the motion.

4. **Understand that fees may not fully compensate you.** Even with a fee award, you will spend more than you recover. Frame the motion strategically — use it to get the documents and to create a record of the other party's conduct for the final case, not primarily as a way to be made financially whole.

5. **Consider whether the pattern of obstruction itself is evidence.** A spouse who is willing to go through multiple motions to compel, defy court orders, and risk sanctions to keep documents hidden is telling the court something. Make sure your attorney is presenting that narrative clearly.

---

Discovery obstruction is a deliberate choice with consequences that tend to compound over time. The more aggressively a party fights disclosure, the more the court notices — and the less credibility that party retains when it matters most.
