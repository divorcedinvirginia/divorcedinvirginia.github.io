Many separating couples try to handle custody informally in the early stages of a divorce. They talk it over, agree on a schedule, and assume that mutual good faith will carry things through until the divorce is finalized. For some families, this works. For others — often without warning — it does not.

The other parent starts returning the children late. They cancel visits with short notice or no notice. They announce unilateral changes to the schedule. They refuse to return the children at the agreed time. They take the children for extended periods without your consent and point to the informal arrangement as proof that this is normal.

This post is about what to do when that happens — and why the absence of a formal court order is the core of the problem.

---

## The Fundamental Problem: An Agreement Is Not an Order

This is the most important thing to understand in this situation. An informal custody arrangement — even one that both parents agreed to in writing, by text, or by email — is not a court order. It is a private agreement between two people.

Private agreements between divorcing spouses are not enforceable in the way court orders are. You cannot call the police and ask them to enforce a text message exchange about pickup times. You cannot file a contempt motion based on a violation of something a court never ordered. You have no formal legal mechanism to compel compliance with an informal schedule.

What you do have is evidence — of the original agreement, of the violations, and of a pattern of conduct that a court will be asked to evaluate. Building and preserving that evidence is your most important job right now.

---

## Document Every Violation — Starting Today

If the other parent is not following the agreed schedule, begin a written log immediately. For every incident, record:

- **The date and time** of the scheduled exchange or event
- **What was agreed** — the specific schedule that was in place, with as much specificity as possible
- **What actually happened** — the other parent arrived two hours late, did not return the children at all, texted at 6 p.m. to say pickup was cancelled, announced they were keeping the children an extra week, etc.
- **The children's reaction**, if relevant — were they upset, confused, distressed by the change?
- **Any communication that occurred** — save every text, email, and voicemail related to the schedule

Do not rely on your memory. Courts deal with competing accounts of events that happened months ago, and detailed contemporaneous records are far more credible than a general description of "they were always late and kept changing things." Your log with specific dates, times, and details is the difference between a credible record and a narrative the other parent can dispute.

Keep all written communications. Do not delete texts even if they are upsetting to read. Forward emails to your own address or take screenshots and back them up somewhere your spouse cannot access.

---

## Communicate in Writing — And Keep It Factual

When the other parent deviates from the schedule, respond in writing. Not with an emotional reaction — with a clear, documented statement of what was agreed and what happened.

If they say they are keeping the children an extra night: *"Our agreement was that you would return the children Sunday at 6 p.m. I'm confirming I'll be available at that time for the exchange. Please let me know if there is something specific going on."*

If they cancel a visit: *"I'm noting that you've cancelled today's visit. This is the third time this month a scheduled visit has been cancelled. I'll plan to have the children available at the next scheduled time."*

If they unilaterally announce a change: *"I haven't agreed to a change in the schedule. I'm happy to discuss adjustments through our attorneys, but I'm not in a position to agree to changes unilaterally."*

These messages do two things: they create a written record of the other parent's deviation from the agreement, and they demonstrate that you responded calmly and appropriately rather than reactively. Both matter when a judge reads the exchange.

Do not match the other parent's tone if they are hostile or aggressive. Do not threaten what you will "do to them" in court. Do not use the children as leverage. Keep every message something you would be comfortable reading aloud to a judge.

## What the Other Parent Is Actually Building

If the other parent is arbitrarily changing the schedule or keeping the children beyond the agreed time, they may be doing it carelessly — or they may be doing it deliberately. Either way, it is worth understanding what the legal effect of their conduct can be.

### They May Be Trying to Establish a New Status Quo

Virginia courts give heavy weight to the existing custody arrangement when making permanent decisions. A parent who gradually expands their time with the children — by keeping them longer, by engineering schedule changes that leave the children primarily in their care, by using the lack of a court order to accumulate time — can then point to that arrangement as the established status quo.

If this pattern goes on long enough, a court asked to enter a formal custody order may start from the current arrangement rather than the original informal agreement. "The children have been living primarily with me for the past eight months" is a powerful argument, and it is built one missed exchange at a time.

### They May Be Reducing Your Contact Deliberately

A parent who is building a custody case may be working to minimize your visible role in the children's lives — by cutting short your parenting time, by scheduling the children's activities during your time without coordination, or by refusing exchanges in a way that makes your involvement appear sporadic.

When a court later reviews who was the more actively involved parent during the separation period, the parenting time log matters. If your actual time with the children is being restricted, that restriction needs to be documented and addressed promptly.

### They May Be Testing Whether You Will Act

Some parents push boundaries in the early stages of a separation to see what they can get away with — how much the other parent will tolerate before seeking legal intervention. If you accept the pattern without consequence, it often escalates.

## When to Involve Your Attorney

The moment you recognize a pattern — not a single incident, but a pattern of schedule violations, arbitrary changes, or extended unauthorized retention of the children — contact your attorney.

Your attorney needs to know because:

- A strongly worded letter from your attorney to the other parent's attorney sometimes produces compliance that your own messages cannot
- Your attorney can evaluate whether the situation warrants filing for a formal temporary custody order
- Your attorney can advise you on what is and is not appropriate to do unilaterally
- The pattern you have been documenting is evidence your attorney needs to have

Do not wait until you are at a breaking point. By the time most people call their attorney about informal schedule violations, the pattern has been going on for weeks or months and the other parent has accumulated a significant advantage.

## The Only Real Solution: Get a Court Order

This bears repeating clearly: the only durable solution to an informal schedule not being followed is to get a court order.

An informal agreement, no matter how clearly documented, no matter how unambiguous the text messages, cannot be enforced by the court. What the court can enforce is an order it has entered — a pendente lite custody order, a consent order signed by both parties, or a final custody order in the divorce decree.

Once a court order is in place:

- Violations are enforceable through contempt proceedings
- Police can assist with enforcement in some circumstances
- Repeated violations become part of the court record and affect the violating parent's credibility at trial
- A parent who repeatedly violates court orders faces increasingly serious consequences: fines, attorney's fee awards, adverse custody rulings, and in extreme cases, incarceration

### How to Get a Temporary Custody Order

Your attorney can file a motion for a pendente lite custody order at any point during the divorce proceedings. The motion asks the court to enter a formal temporary custody arrangement that will govern the parties until a final order is entered at trial or by agreement.

The hearing will typically involve:
- Evidence about the current custody arrangement and who has been providing primary care
- Evidence about any violations of the informal agreement
- Each parent's proposed schedule
- The best interests of the children

Your documentation — the log of violations, the written communications, the record of the pattern — becomes evidence at this hearing. A well-documented pattern of one parent ignoring agreements and arbitrarily retaining the children is relevant both to the temporary order the court enters and to how the court evaluates that parent's reliability and respect for the co-parenting relationship.

### Consent Orders as an Alternative to a Contested Hearing

If the dispute about the schedule is not rooted in a deep strategic disagreement, both parties can agree to enter a consent order with the court. A consent order is a formal court order that both parties sign agreeing to its terms. Once entered by a judge, it has the same legal force as a contested order.

A consent order allows you to formalize the agreed schedule — including the one the other parent has been violating — in a way that gives it legal teeth. If you can reach agreement on the terms through your attorneys without a contested hearing, a consent order is typically faster and cheaper than litigation.

## What You Can and Cannot Do Without a Court Order

Understanding the limits of your options without a court order prevents you from taking actions that harm your case.

### What You Can Do

- **Document everything** — keep the log, save the communications, note the pattern
- **Respond in writing** to every deviation from the agreed schedule
- **Withhold agreement to schedule changes** — you are not obligated to agree to unilateral changes just because your spouse announces them
- **Show up for every scheduled exchange** — even if you expect the children will not be there. Document that you were there. This demonstrates your consistency and willingness to follow the agreement.
- **Consult and direct your attorney** — including asking your attorney to contact opposing counsel about the pattern
- **File for a formal temporary custody order** — this is your primary legal tool

### What You Cannot Do

- **Withhold the children in retaliation.** If the other parent is not returning the children at the agreed time, you cannot respond by refusing to return them at the next exchange. Two parents both violating the informal agreement destroys your credibility and leaves the court with a picture of two people who cannot cooperate, which may result in a more restrictive order than either wants.

- **Physically prevent exchanges.** Blocking an exchange — physically or by removing the children to an undisclosed location — can be treated by a court as interference with the other parent's custody rights, even without a formal order. Do not do this.

- **Deny the other parent all contact.** Unless there is a genuine safety emergency, unilaterally cutting off the other parent's access to the children will be viewed extremely negatively by a court. The standard in Virginia is that both parents' relationships with the children should be fostered and protected unless there is a specific, documented reason not to.

- **Take the children across state lines for an extended period without notice or agreement.** Even without a custody order, doing this can trigger jurisdictional complications and will be viewed as interference with the other parent's time. Virginia may lose jurisdiction if the children are out of state for six months. Do not create that problem for yourself.

- **Call the police expecting them to enforce an informal agreement.** Law enforcement cannot and will not enforce a private arrangement between divorcing spouses. They can respond to emergencies, document incidents, and in some circumstances assist with the preservation of peace during exchanges — but they cannot order a parent to hand over the children based on a text message schedule.

## Showing Up Even When You Know They Won't

This deserves its own section because it is counterintuitive and many people do not do it.

If you know — or strongly suspect — that the other parent will not produce the children at the scheduled exchange, **show up anyway.**

Document that you were there. Take a timestamped photograph of yourself at the exchange location. Send a text at the moment you arrive: *"I'm at [location] for the 6 p.m. exchange."* If the children are not produced, send a follow-up: *"It is now 6:15 p.m. The children have not been brought for the scheduled exchange. I am leaving. Please contact me to arrange an alternative time."*

This creates an unambiguous record that you were present, you were ready, and the other parent failed to comply. It is the kind of evidence that speaks clearly in a custody hearing. A pattern of the other parent not producing the children combined with a pattern of you reliably showing up tells a court exactly what it needs to know about who is facilitating the children's relationship with both parents.

## If the Children Are Being Withheld Completely

If the other parent has taken the children and is refusing to return them at all — not just running late, not just changing the schedule, but actively withholding the children — the situation crosses from a scheduling dispute into an emergency.

Contact your attorney immediately for emergency relief. Depending on the circumstances, options include:

- **Emergency pendente lite motion** for temporary custody, filed on an expedited basis
- **Writ of habeas corpus** — a legal proceeding to compel the immediate production of the children before a court
- **Involvement of law enforcement** if there is a safety concern or if the withholding meets the threshold for custodial interference under Virginia law

Virginia Code § 18.2-49.1 makes it a criminal offense to take a child in violation of a custody order or with the intent to deprive the other parent of custody. Without a court order, the criminal threshold is higher, but prolonged unauthorized withholding of children — particularly if accompanied by relocation — can still have criminal implications.

Do not wait. The longer a child is in one parent's exclusive care without a court order, the harder it becomes to undo that arrangement.

---

The gap between "we agreed" and "a court ordered" is where more custody problems originate than anywhere else in Virginia divorce law. The faster you close that gap with a formal court order, the more protected you and your children are — regardless of how cooperative the other parent seems to be today.
