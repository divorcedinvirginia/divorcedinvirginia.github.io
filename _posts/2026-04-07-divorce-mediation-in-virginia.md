Mediation is not arbitration, and it is not a court hearing. It is a structured negotiation, facilitated by a neutral third party, in which you and your spouse attempt to reach agreements that can resolve some or all of the issues in your divorce without a judge deciding them for you.

Most contested Virginia divorces involve some form of mediation, either by choice or by court order. Many cases that appear headed for trial settle in mediation. Many cases that do not settle in mediation still make enough progress to narrow the issues and reduce the cost of what follows.

Understanding what mediation actually is — and what it is not — is essential for getting anything useful out of it.

---

## What Mediation Is and Is Not

### What It Is

Mediation is a voluntary, confidential negotiation process in which a trained mediator helps you and your spouse identify issues, understand each other's positions, and explore whether agreement is possible. The mediator is not a judge. They cannot order anything. They cannot make decisions for you. They can only facilitate the conversation and help move it forward.

If you reach an agreement in mediation, that agreement is typically reduced to writing and, once incorporated into a separation agreement or court order, becomes legally binding. If you do not reach an agreement, the case proceeds as it otherwise would — to additional hearings, a guardian ad litem if children are involved, or ultimately trial.

Nothing said in mediation can be used against you in court. Virginia law protects mediation communications from disclosure (Virginia Code § 8.01-581.22). This confidentiality is what makes genuine negotiation possible — you can explore options, make offers, and acknowledge weaknesses in your position without those statements becoming evidence.

### What It Is Not

**Mediation is not therapy.** The mediator is not there to help either party process their feelings about the marriage or the divorce. Some mediators are skilled at de-escalating emotional conflict when it arises, but the purpose of the session is to move toward agreement, not toward healing.

**Mediation is not a fact-finding process.** The mediator is not conducting an investigation, evaluating credibility, or determining who is telling the truth. They are facilitating negotiation between two parties who will each present their own version of the facts.

**Mediation is not free legal advice.** The mediator cannot advise either party on their legal rights or tell you whether a proposed agreement is fair under Virginia law. That is your attorney's job — and attending mediation without having consulted with an attorney first is one of the most common and costly mistakes people make.

**Mediation does not require compromise on everything.** You can reach agreement on some issues — spousal support, for example — while leaving others — the division of a specific asset, a custody schedule detail — unresolved for court. Partial agreements are common and valuable.

## How Mediation Is Initiated

Mediation in a Virginia divorce can be initiated in several ways:

**By agreement of the parties.** Either party can propose mediation at any point in the case. Many attorneys recommend it early, before positions have hardened and before the cost of litigation has already consumed significant resources.

**By court order.** Virginia courts have authority to refer cases to mediation, and many judges do so routinely — particularly in cases involving children. A court-ordered mediation does not mean you are required to agree to anything. It means you are required to attend and make a good-faith effort to negotiate.

**Through the collaborative divorce process.** Some Virginia divorces are structured as collaborative cases from the outset, with mediation built into the process rather than added when litigation stalls.

The mediator can be selected by agreement of the parties — often through a list provided by the court or through referral from attorneys — or appointed by the court.

## Who Mediates Virginia Divorces

Virginia mediators in family law cases typically come from one of several backgrounds: attorneys who have shifted focus to mediation, mental health professionals with family mediation training, and professional mediators who specialize in family disputes. The combination of legal knowledge and conflict-resolution skill varies significantly among mediators.

**Attorney-mediators** tend to be more comfortable with the legal complexity of property division, spousal support calculations, and the legal implications of proposed terms. They are generally well-suited for cases where the legal issues are the primary obstacle.

**Mental health professional mediators** tend to be more skilled at managing high emotional conflict and are often preferred in cases where the primary obstacle is the parties' inability to communicate productively rather than the legal complexity of the issues.

Neither type is inherently superior — the right mediator for a given case depends on the nature of the dispute and the dynamics of the parties. Your attorney can usually make a useful recommendation.

## What to Expect: The Format

### Before the Session

Most mediators will conduct some form of pre-mediation intake — a phone call or written questionnaire to understand the issues in the case, the parties' general positions, and any safety concerns. If domestic violence has been part of the marriage, mediation may not be appropriate, or the mediator may structure the session to account for the power imbalance. Be honest in the intake process about any circumstances that might affect your ability to negotiate freely.

**Prepare a summary of the issues.** Before mediation, you should have a clear picture of what is actually in dispute — what assets exist, what their approximate values are, what the custody and parenting time questions are, whether spousal support is at issue, and what each party has said they want. If you do not have this clarity going in, the early portion of the session will be consumed establishing it rather than negotiating.

**Consult with your attorney before you go.** This is non-negotiable. You need to understand your legal rights before you sit down to negotiate them away or preserve them. What does Virginia's equitable distribution law actually entitle you to? What would a court likely order on spousal support given the length of the marriage and the income disparity? What custody arrangement has the strongest support in the evidence? Without answers to these questions — from your attorney, not from the mediator — you cannot evaluate whether a proposed agreement is reasonable.

**Know your bottom line.** Mediation works best when you enter it knowing what you actually need versus what you would prefer. If you have not thought through which issues are genuinely non-negotiable and which have room for movement, you are likely to either give away more than you should or hold firm on the wrong things.

### The Session Itself

Mediation sessions in Virginia divorce cases typically run between three and eight hours, with full-day sessions common for cases involving multiple contested issues. Some cases require multiple sessions scheduled days or weeks apart. Complex cases — those involving significant assets, business interests, or deeply contested custody — may require several full days of mediation spread over weeks.

Most Virginia divorce mediations use one of two structural formats:

**Joint sessions.** Both parties and their attorneys (if represented) are in the same room with the mediator. The mediator facilitates discussion, keeps the conversation productive, and manages the exchange of proposals. Joint sessions can be efficient and allow each party to hear the other's reasoning directly. They can also become unproductive quickly if the emotional conflict between the parties is high.

**Caucus format (separate rooms).** The parties and their attorneys are in separate rooms, and the mediator moves between them, carrying proposals and information. This format is common in high-conflict cases where joint sessions would quickly deteriorate. It is slower, because the mediator is the sole channel of communication, but it removes the direct confrontational element that derails many joint sessions.

Some mediators begin with a brief joint session to establish ground rules and frame the issues, then move to caucuses. Others conduct the entire session in caucuses. The mediator will typically determine the format based on the intake information and adjust as the session proceeds.

### The Role of Attorneys in Mediation

Whether attorneys attend mediation varies. In some cases, both parties attend with their attorneys present throughout. In others, parties attend without attorneys but have consulted with them beforehand and can call them during breaks. Some mediators prefer attorney-free sessions to reduce the adversarial dynamic.

If your attorney is not present during mediation, you should have a clear understanding before you go of the parameters within which you are authorized to agree — what you can accept on each issue without needing to consult further. Do not feel pressured to sign anything at the end of a mediation session without having your attorney review it, even if the mediator presents this as a normal expectation.

## What You Can Expect to Accomplish

The issues that mediation can address in a Virginia divorce include:

**Property division.** Who gets the house, or whether it is sold and proceeds divided. How retirement accounts are divided. The disposition of vehicles, investment accounts, personal property, business interests, and debt.

**Spousal support.** Whether spousal support will be paid, in what amount, for how long, and under what circumstances it terminates.

**Child custody and legal decision-making.** Whether custody will be sole or joint, how significant decisions about the children will be made, and what the parenting time schedule will look like week to week, during holidays, and over school breaks.

**Child support.** Virginia child support is calculated by statute using both parents' incomes and certain expenses, but there are areas of discretion — particularly around extraordinary expenses — that can be negotiated.

**Procedural issues.** When the divorce will be finalized, how the process will move forward if mediation does not resolve all issues.

Not every case resolves entirely in mediation. A common and productive outcome is a partial settlement — agreement on some issues (property, for example) while leaving others (a specific custody dispute) for the court to resolve. A partial settlement that removes several issues from litigation can significantly reduce cost and duration even if full resolution is not achieved.

## Common Pitfalls

### Going In Without Legal Advice

The single most common and costly mistake in mediation is attending without a clear understanding of your legal rights. Mediation is a negotiation, and negotiating without knowing your alternatives — what a court would likely order if no agreement is reached — means you have no rational basis for evaluating any proposal. People who settle in mediation without legal advice often discover afterward that they agreed to terms that a court would have awarded them more favorably.

### Letting Emotion Drive Positions

High-conflict divorces involve genuine grievances, genuine anger, and genuine pain. All of that is real and valid. It is also a poor basis for negotiating a financial and parenting arrangement you will live with for years.

The most common form of this in mediation is what experienced mediators call **positional bargaining** — taking a position not because it reflects what you actually need, but because it signals something about your anger, your willingness to fight, or your refusal to give your spouse what they want. Positional bargaining is expensive, because it produces impasse rather than agreement, and the cost of the impasse — continued litigation — ultimately falls on both parties.

### Treating Every Issue as Equally Important

Not every issue in a divorce is equally significant. Some people enter mediation prepared to fight equally hard over furniture as over retirement accounts, over a $200 difference in child support as over the primary residence designation. This is a failure to prioritize.

Before mediation, work through what actually matters most to you — financially, practically, in terms of your children's wellbeing — and accept that you will likely need to give ground somewhere. Knowing where you can afford to give ground, and where you cannot, is one of the most useful things you can bring into the room.

### Agreeing to Something You Do Not Understand

Mediation produces proposals quickly, and the pressure of the room — particularly after hours of negotiation — creates a momentum toward closure. That momentum can push people toward signing off on terms they have not fully understood or thought through.

Do not agree to anything in mediation that you do not understand. If you need time to run a number past your attorney, ask for it. If you are unsure what a proposed term means legally, ask for clarification. A term that sounds reasonable in the room and turns out to be harmful once you have had time to think about it is not a good settlement — it is a problem that may take more litigation to fix.

### Using Mediation as a Discovery Tool

Mediation is not a substitute for formal discovery. Some parties attend mediation hoping to extract information from the other side — financial disclosures, admissions about assets — that they have been unable to obtain through normal channels. Mediators are generally wise to this dynamic and will manage it. More importantly, it is an inefficient use of mediation time and tends to produce defensiveness in the other party rather than useful information.

## Techniques Mediators Use

Understanding how mediators move negotiations forward helps you participate more effectively and recognize when a technique is being used on you.

### Reframing

Mediators frequently reframe how issues are described — translating positional statements (*I will never agree to that*) into interest-based ones (*it sounds like what matters most to you here is...*). Reframing reduces the emotional charge of a position and opens space for discussion of the underlying concern.

When a mediator reframes your position, they are not misrepresenting it. They are trying to identify what you actually need so that the negotiation can move toward options that address it. Engage with the reframe rather than resisting it.

### Reality Testing

A mediator will often test the parties' positions against what is actually likely to happen if no agreement is reached. This is sometimes called the BATNA conversation — *Best Alternative To a Negotiated Agreement.* What will a court actually order on this issue? How long will litigation take? What will it cost?

This technique can feel like pressure, and it is — but it is legitimate pressure based on accurate information. If your attorney has told you that your position on a given issue is unlikely to prevail in court, a mediator's reality testing is confirming what you already know. Listen to it.

### Anchoring and Counter-Offers

Mediators manage the exchange of numbers and proposals. They will often ask a party to make the first offer on a given issue — which anchors the negotiation around that number — and then carry counter-offers between rooms, managing the incremental movement toward a middle.

Being aware of the anchoring effect is useful. If you make the opening offer on a financial issue, anchor it toward your desired outcome rather than toward what you think will be accepted. The negotiation will move toward the middle regardless.

### The Hypothetical

Mediators frequently use hypotheticals to explore whether an agreement might be possible without either party formally committing to it: *If your spouse were to agree to X, would you be open to considering Y?* The hypothetical allows parties to test options without the risk of having their willingness to compromise held against them.

Engage with hypotheticals genuinely. They are one of the most productive tools a mediator has, and they are protected by the confidentiality of the mediation process.

### Identifying Common Ground

In high-conflict cases where the parties seem to disagree about everything, mediators work to identify areas of actual agreement — often starting with the children — to build momentum. If both parents agree that the children's stability is the priority, that shared value can be used to evaluate proposed parenting arrangements against something both parties have already endorsed.

### Bracketing

Bracketing is a technique used to jump-start stalled financial negotiations. The mediator proposes that both parties agree to accept a resolution within a defined range — neither party commits to the specific number, only to the range — and then the negotiation within that range becomes more tractable. For example: *Both parties agree that the resolution on this issue will be between $X and $Y. Now let's work toward a specific number in that range.*

Bracketing signals that both parties are genuinely in a zone of potential agreement, which often creates momentum that direct offers and counter-offers cannot.

## After Mediation

If mediation produces a full or partial agreement, the mediator will typically prepare a written summary or memorandum of understanding that captures the agreed terms. This document is not yet a legally binding agreement — it is a record of what the parties agreed to in the room.

Your attorney will take that memorandum and draft it into a formal separation agreement or incorporate it into a court order. Read that draft carefully. Occasionally, language in a formal agreement captures a mediated term differently than you understood it. Raise any discrepancy with your attorney before signing anything.

If mediation does not produce agreement, the case continues as it would have. The confidentiality of the mediation means that nothing said in the room can be used in court. The failure to settle in mediation is not evidence of bad faith and does not affect how a court will view your case.

---

Mediation works best when both parties enter it informed, prepared, and genuinely open to the possibility of resolution — not as a performance of willingness to settle, but as a genuine attempt to reach an agreement that is better for both of them than having a judge decide. That posture, combined with good legal preparation and realistic expectations about what the alternative looks like, gives mediation the best chance of producing an outcome both parties can live with.
