A contested divorce is hard on everyone. But children experience it differently than adults do — and what parents say to them, in the heat of the moment or with the best of intentions, can cause lasting harm. It can also cause lasting damage to a custody case.

This post covers how to have honest, age-appropriate conversations with your children about the divorce, what to absolutely avoid, and how to talk about the other parent in a way that protects your kids — and your legal position.

---

## What Children Actually Need to Hear

Children of all ages need three things above everything else during a divorce:

1. **Reassurance that they are loved by both parents and that will not change**
2. **Clarity that the divorce is not their fault**
3. **Predictability about what their daily life will look like**

Everything else — the legal details, the reasons for the divorce, who did what to whom — is not something your children need to know, regardless of how old they are. The impulse to share "the truth" with your kids is understandable. It is almost always a mistake.

### The Initial Conversation

If possible, both parents should be present for the first conversation about the divorce. This is difficult in a contested situation, especially if the separation was sudden or acrimonious. But a unified initial message — delivered calmly, without blame, focused on the children's daily lives — is far better for children than two competing versions of events.

If a joint conversation is not possible, each parent should tell the children the same core facts:

- Mom and Dad have decided to live in separate homes
- This is a grown-up decision and has nothing to do with anything the children did
- Both parents love the children and will continue to be their parents
- The children will still go to the same school, see the same friends, and [fill in specific continuity points]

Do not say more than this in the first conversation. Children need time to absorb even this much.

### Tailor the Conversation to the Child's Age

**Young children (under 7)** need simple, concrete information. They do not understand legal concepts or adult relationship dynamics. Focus entirely on the practical: where they will sleep, when they will see each parent, that both parents will come to their birthday. Avoid abstract explanations.

**School-age children (8–12)** will have more questions and may already sense that something is wrong. Answer their direct questions honestly but briefly. If they ask why you are getting divorced, an answer like *"We have some grown-up problems we haven't been able to work out, and we've decided it's better to live apart"* is truthful without being harmful. Do not volunteer details they did not ask for.

**Teenagers** are capable of understanding more and will often press for specifics. They deserve honest answers to direct questions — but honest does not mean comprehensive, and it does not mean using them as a sounding board for your grievances. A teenager asking *"Did Dad cheat on you?"* deserves a truthful answer if the answer is yes, but it does not deserve a detailed account of the affair or an invitation to take sides.

## What Not to Do — and Why It Matters in Court

### Do Not Discuss the Divorce Case With Your Children

This is one of the most common — and most damaging — mistakes parents make in contested divorces. The temptation is real: your children are present, they are asking questions, and you are frustrated and frightened. But the details of your case — what you are asking for in court, what your spouse's attorney has argued, what a commissioner recommended — are not for your children.

Children repeat what they hear. They repeat it to the other parent, to teachers, to guardians ad litem, and to custody evaluators. A child who can recite the details of a spousal support dispute or describe one parent's legal strategy in a custody hearing has been exposed to things they should never have heard — and a Virginia judge will hold that against the parent who did the exposing.

### Do Not Ask Children to Choose Sides

This is parental alienation in its most direct form. Asking a child — directly or subtly — to align with one parent against the other causes psychological harm and is treated very seriously by Virginia courts.

Subtle forms of this include:

- *"Don't you want to live with me?"*
- *"You know I'm the one who really takes care of you."*
- *"Your father doesn't really care about spending time with you — that's why he's never home."*
- *"Ask your mom why she's doing this to our family."*

None of these statements are neutral. All of them put the child in an impossible position and signal to the court that you are willing to harm your children to gain an advantage.

### Do Not Make Promises You Cannot Keep

Children in the early stages of a contested divorce are anxious about change. The instinct to reassure them — *"Don't worry, you'll always live here"* or *"I'll make sure you keep your room"* — is understandable. But in a contested custody case, you do not know what the outcome will be. Promises about custody arrangements, living situations, schools, or holidays that you cannot guarantee will only deepen the child's distress when circumstances change.

Promise what you know to be true: *"I will always be your parent. I will always love you. I will always be there for you."* These promises are ones you can keep.

### Do Not Record Conversations With Your Children

This deserves its own section, because it is more common than most people realize — and more damaging.

In a contested custody case, parents sometimes record conversations with their children hoping to capture something the child says about the other parent that can be used as evidence. This practice is harmful to children and almost always backfires legally.

**Why recording children is harmful:**

Children who know or suspect they are being recorded feel pressure to perform. They tell the recording parent what they think that parent wants to hear. This is not honesty — it is a child trying to manage a frightening situation by pleasing the adult they are with. The recording does not capture the child's genuine feelings; it captures a child under pressure.

Repeated recording of a child for litigation purposes can rise to the level of emotional abuse. Courts have appointed guardians ad litem specifically because a parent was using children this way.

**Why it backfires legally:**

Virginia courts are deeply skeptical of recorded statements from children obtained by a parent during contested custody litigation. Judges and commissioners understand the dynamics at play. A recording of a child saying they want to live with you, obtained in your living room while the divorce is active, carries very little evidentiary weight — and the act of making the recording can make you look manipulative and willing to put your legal interests above your child's wellbeing.

**Virginia's wiretapping law:**

Virginia Code § 19.2-62 generally prohibits recording conversations without the consent of at least one party. A parent recording a conversation with a minor child occupies a gray area — courts have reached different conclusions about whether a parent can consent on behalf of a child. Do not assume it is legal. Do not do it without explicit guidance from your attorney.

If you have genuine concerns about something your child has disclosed about the other parent's conduct, the proper channel is to report it to your attorney, who can request a guardian ad litem or custody evaluation through the court.

### Do Not Use Children as Information Gatherers

Asking your children — in any form — about what is happening at the other parent's home is harmful and constitutes covert parental alienation.

This includes:

- *"What did Dad say about the hearing?"*
- *"Is your mom dating anyone?"*
- *"What did they have for dinner? Who was there?"*
- *"Did your dad say anything about the house?"*

Children who are asked these questions feel like spies. They feel torn between their parents and guilty regardless of what they say. Repeated questioning of this kind causes anxiety, sleep problems, and behavioral changes in children — and it is the kind of behavior that a guardian ad litem will identify and report to the court.

If you need information about what is happening in your spouse's home, ask your attorney about appropriate discovery methods.

## How to Talk About the Other Parent

How you speak about your spouse to your children is one of the most consequential things you will do during the entire divorce process. Courts in Virginia watch for this carefully. Guardians ad litem specifically ask children how each parent speaks about the other. Custody evaluators consider it in their reports.

### The Standard to Hold Yourself To

The standard is not neutrality — it is active protection of your children's relationship with their other parent.

Children have a psychological need to see themselves as the product of two people worth loving. When you demean, criticize, or undermine the other parent in front of your children, you are not just attacking your spouse — you are attacking a part of your child's identity. Children who are repeatedly told that one parent is bad, selfish, dishonest, or dangerous internalize that message in ways that cause lasting harm.

### What to Say Instead

When your children ask about the other parent — why they left, why the divorce is happening, whether they are a good person — your answers should be honest but protective:

**If your child asks why you are getting divorced:**
*"Sometimes adults have problems they aren't able to fix, even when they try hard. It's a grown-up situation, and it has nothing to do with you."*

**If your child expresses anger at the other parent:**
*"It makes sense that you feel that way. This is a really hard time for everyone. Your dad loves you very much, even though things are hard right now."*

**If your child asks if the other parent is a bad person:**
*"Your mom has made some choices I disagree with, but she loves you, and that's what matters most between the two of you."*

**If your child returns from the other home and seems upset:**
*"I can see you're having a hard time. Do you want to talk about it? I'm here."* — and then listen without probing for information about the other parent.

### When the Other Parent Has Genuinely Done Something Wrong

This is the hardest situation. If the other parent has been abusive, has committed a crime, has abandoned the family, or has done something the children may eventually find out about, the pressure to share "the truth" is intense.

The guiding principle is: **children need to be protected from harm, not from reality — but timing matters.**

If there is a genuine safety concern (abuse, substance use, dangerous behavior around the children), report it through proper legal channels immediately. Do not editorialize about it with the children — report it to your attorney and let the court process handle it.

For other wrongdoing — infidelity, financial misconduct, legal problems — your children do not need to know during the divorce. They may come to understand more when they are older. But a contested custody case is not the right time or context for that conversation, and you are not the right person to deliver it unilaterally while litigation is pending.

### Talking About Difficult Changes Honestly

There will be real changes in your children's lives — moves, new schools, changes in finances, less time with one parent. Children deserve honest, age-appropriate acknowledgment of these changes.

What they do not deserve is an accompanying narrative about whose fault those changes are.

*"We're going to move to a smaller place for a while. It won't be as big as this house, but we're going to make it ours."* is honest and forward-looking.

*"We have to move because your dad isn't paying what he should"* is honest and damaging.

The first version gives your child something they can work with. The second makes your child feel responsible for managing a conflict that is not theirs to manage.

## What to Do When Children Are Struggling

Even with the best parenting, children in contested divorces struggle. Signs to watch for:

- Regression to younger behaviors (bedwetting, thumb-sucking, clinginess) in young children
- Declining grades or school avoidance
- Withdrawal from friends and activities
- Increased aggression or defiance
- Sleep disturbances, nightmares, or complaints of stomachaches
- Expressions of hopelessness or statements like "I wish I wasn't here"

**Seek professional support early.** A child therapist who specializes in family transitions can give your children a safe place to process what they are experiencing without the complication of parental loyalty conflicts. Courts look favorably on parents who proactively seek therapeutic support for their children — it demonstrates that you are putting the children's wellbeing first.

Do not wait for the other parent's agreement to seek therapy for your children if they are showing signs of distress. Talk to your pediatrician and consult your attorney if access to therapy becomes a contested issue.

---

The way you parent through a contested divorce — especially how you talk to your children and about their other parent — is one of the clearest indicators courts use to determine who will best serve the children's interests long-term. It is also, more importantly, one of the most significant factors in whether your children come through this experience intact.

The short-term relief of venting to your children, recording their conversations, or enlisting them as allies is not worth the long-term cost to them — or to your case.
