Your eight-year-old climbs into the car after a weekend at the other parent's home and says, unprompted: *"Dad says you're a liar."* Or: *"Mom hates you. She told me."* Or: *"Dad says you're the reason our family fell apart."*

The experience is visceral. It is enraging. And it is one of the most consequential moments in a high-conflict divorce, because what you do in the next sixty seconds sets the tone for everything that follows — for your child, for your co-parenting relationship, and for how this moment eventually shows up in your custody case.

Almost every natural instinct you have in that moment is wrong. This post is about what to do instead.

---

## What Is Actually Happening

Before responding, understand what is in front of you.

Your child has just been placed in an impossible position. They have been asked — consciously or not — to carry a message between two adults who are in conflict. Children who carry these messages feel the weight of them. They often do not fully understand what they are repeating. They are watching your face very carefully to see how you receive it, and they are silently braced for the fallout.

The message itself may be:

**Accurate.** The other parent said exactly what the child reported, and the child is repeating it faithfully. This is parental alienation — one parent using a child as a vehicle for hostility toward the other parent — and it is harmful to the child regardless of how it affects you.

**Partially accurate.** The other parent said something negative, and the child is interpreting or summarizing it in the most dramatic terms they have available. What was said may have been more subtle, but the child absorbed the emotional content and is expressing it directly.

**Filtered through the child's emotional state.** Children who are struggling sometimes attribute feelings to a parent that reflect their own anxiety. A child who is worried that their parents hate each other may express that fear as fact: *"Dad hates you."*

**A test.** Older children sometimes say provocative things to gauge how a parent will react. A child who has learned that certain statements produce emotional reactions in a parent may be exploring that dynamic.

What is almost never happening: the child is making it up for no reason. Something prompted this statement. Your job is to respond in a way that addresses the child's experience — not the content of the message.

## The First Sixty Seconds: What to Say

Your response in the immediate moment is the most important part. Everything you say will be replayed in the child's mind and very possibly reported back to the other parent.

**Do not react to the content of the message.** Do not confirm it, deny it, or engage with whether it is true. The accuracy of whether the other parent hates you is completely irrelevant to how you should respond to your child.

**Acknowledge your child's experience, not the message:**

*"Thank you for telling me that. That sounds like it was hard to hear. Are you okay?"*

*"I can see that bothered you. How are you feeling about it?"*

*"That's a heavy thing to carry around. I'm glad you told me."*

These responses do several things simultaneously: they make the child feel heard, they redirect from the content of the message to the child's emotional experience, and they signal that you are not going to explode — which is what the child was braced for.

**Do not ask follow-up questions about what else the other parent said.** Not *"What did Dad say exactly?"* Not *"Was anyone else there?"* Not *"Has Dad said other things like that?"* These questions turn your child into an information source and interrogate them about the other parent's home. It puts them directly in the middle.

If your child volunteers more information, listen without probing. If they seem to want to talk, let them — briefly, and without asking questions that steer the conversation toward the other parent's conduct.

## What to Say About the Other Parent

This is where parents most often cause damage. The temptation to correct the record — to tell your child that you do not deserve to be talked about that way, that the other parent is wrong, or that they are the one who caused the family's problems — is almost overwhelming.

Resist it entirely.

### The Standard

The standard for talking about the other parent to your children has been mentioned in several posts on this site, but it bears repeating here in the sharpest possible terms: **your job is to actively protect your child's relationship with the other parent, including when that parent is behaving badly.**

This is not about protecting the other parent. It is about protecting your child. A child who hears both parents speak disparagingly about each other — or who hears one parent respond to the other's attacks by launching their own — is a child who cannot feel safe in either home. They feel responsible. They feel caught. They feel that loving one parent is a betrayal of the other.

### What to Actually Say

When your child tells you the other parent hates you, here are responses that are honest, protective, and child-focused:

*"I don't believe your dad hates me. Grown-ups sometimes say things when they're upset that they don't really mean. What I know for sure is that your dad loves you very much."*

*"Mom and I have had a really hard time, and sometimes people say things out of hurt. That's not about you, and it's not something you need to worry about."*

*"I'm sorry you had to hear that. That's grown-up stuff, and it's not fair that it got put on you. You don't need to carry it."*

*"Whatever is happening between me and Dad, that's between us. Your relationship with him is yours, and it doesn't have anything to do with how he or I feel about each other."*

Notice what these responses have in common: they acknowledge the child's experience, they neither confirm nor escalate the other parent's statement, they gently introduce the idea that adult conflict does not define the child's relationship with either parent, and they relieve the child of the burden of the message they were made to carry.

### What Not to Say

- *"Well, I'm not surprised. Your dad has always been like this."*
- *"Your mom doesn't hate me — she hates what I know about her."*
- *"That's because your dad is angry that I'm getting more time with you in court."*
- *"I don't hate your dad — I just wish he would be honest for once."*
- *"You should tell your dad that if he has something to say to me, he should say it himself."*
- *"Did they say anything else?"*

Every one of these responses puts your child deeper into the conflict. Some of them are subtle — *"I don't hate your dad"* sounds neutral but it keeps the child's attention on the question of whether the parents hate each other. Some of them are obvious. All of them are damaging.

## After the Conversation: What to Do With the Information

You now have information that your child was exposed to negative statements about you at the other parent's home. How you handle that information matters.

### Document It

Write down, as soon as you can after the conversation, exactly what your child said and the context in which they said it. Note the date, what you were doing when the child disclosed it, the child's exact words as closely as you can recall them, and how the child appeared emotionally. Do not embellish or interpret — record what was said and observed.

This documentation is not for you to use as emotional ammunition. It is part of a pattern record that your attorney and, potentially, a guardian ad litem needs to see if this conduct is ongoing.

### Do Not Contact the Other Parent About This Directly

Do not call, text, or email the other parent to confront them about what your child reported. Here is why:

The other parent will deny it. The conversation will escalate. The escalation will produce communications that reflect poorly on you. The child, who may have already told the other parent what they told you, will feel responsible for the conflict that follows. None of this helps your child or your case.

If you need to address this with the other parent, do it through attorneys. Your attorney can send a letter noting the pattern and referencing the specific incident without involving you in a direct confrontation.

If there is a co-parenting app in use, you can make a brief, factual note in the communication log: *"[Child's name] reported on [date] that they heard [general characterization of what was said]. I'd ask that adult feelings about our situation not be shared with the children."* Keep it factual and non-accusatory. The record is made. You have stated your concern. Move on.

### Raise It With Your Attorney

If this is a pattern — your child regularly comes home with negative statements about you that originated with the other parent — tell your attorney. Documented patterns of this kind are directly relevant to the best-interests custody analysis. They may support:

- A request for a guardian ad litem to be appointed to independently assess the children's experience in both homes
- A custody evaluation if the pattern is serious and persistent
- A modification motion if the alienating conduct is severe enough to warrant a change in the parenting arrangement
- An argument at trial about each parent's willingness to support the children's relationship with the other parent

A single incident of your child reporting a negative statement may not be actionable. A documented pattern spanning months — with specific dates, what was said, the child's emotional state, and evidence of the impact on the child — is a different matter.

### Consider Whether Your Child Needs Therapeutic Support

A child who is being used to carry hostile messages between parents is under a form of emotional pressure that they should not be managing alone. If your child is regularly coming home with accounts of negative statements about you, or if they seem burdened, anxious, or caught between their parents, individual therapy provides them with a safe space to process these experiences without the complication of loyalty conflicts.

A therapist who works with children in divorced families can also identify whether the pattern your child is describing has the characteristics of systematic parental alienation — and can provide a professional assessment that is far more credible in a custody proceeding than your own account of what your child told you.

## What Communication With the Other Parent Should Look Like

The incident your child has just described makes clear — if it was not already — that direct emotional communication between you and the other parent is not working. The question is how to communicate about the children going forward in a way that does not produce more of the same.

### Keep All Communication Written

Everything between you and the other parent should be in writing — text, email, or co-parenting app. No phone calls about anything substantive. No in-person conversations that are not at formal exchanges with witnesses present.

Written communication creates accountability. People who are inclined to say things in the heat of a verbal conversation are somewhat less likely to type those same things into a message that both parties know is a permanent record.

### Use a Co-Parenting App

If you are not already using a co-parenting app — OurFamilyWizard, TalkingParents, or Coparently — this is the moment to propose it, through your attorney if necessary. These platforms:

- Timestamp every message in a way that cannot be altered
- Archive the complete communication history for court use
- Reduce the emotional charge of individual messages because both parties know the record is being kept
- Signal to the court that you are committed to structured, accountable co-parenting

A co-parenting app does not prevent the other parent from saying damaging things to your children in their own home. But it controls the channel through which they communicate with you, and it creates a record of how both of you behave in co-parenting communication.

### Use the Business Rule

Every message you send to the other parent about the children should pass a single test: *Could I send this message to a difficult business colleague without it being considered unprofessional?*

Subject: custody schedule, children's medical appointment, school conference logistics. Tone: neutral, specific, factual. Content: what you need to communicate, nothing else.

Not: your grievances about what they said to the children. Not: your legal position on custody. Not: your feelings about the marriage or the divorce. All of that belongs in conversations with your attorney or your therapist — not in co-parenting communication.

### Set a Response Window and Stick to It

High-conflict co-parenting communication often involves one parent sending urgent, emotional messages at all hours and expecting immediate responses. Establish a personal rule: you will check and respond to co-parenting messages at set times — once in the morning and once in the evening, for example — and you will not respond outside those windows unless the matter involves a genuine emergency.

This does two things: it removes the emotional reactivity of real-time message exchanges, and it prevents the other parent from managing your emotional state through the timing and content of their messages.

An emergency is: the child is injured, the child needs medical attention, there is a safety concern. An emergency is not: a demand for an immediate response about next month's schedule, a message sent at 11 p.m. about a grievance, or any message that begins with an accusation.

### Never Use the Children as the Communication Channel

After an incident like the one described in this post, it may feel tempting to respond through the same channel the message arrived in — to send a message back through your child. *"Tell your dad that I said..."*

Do not. Ever. For any reason.

Children who are used as communication channels between parents are being asked to manage adult conflict. They feel responsible for delivering messages accurately. They feel anxious about the response their message will produce. They are put in the position of choosing between loyalty to one parent and loyalty to the other every time they open their mouth.

The moment you ask your child to carry a message to the other parent — no matter how innocuous the message seems — you have placed your child in the middle of the conflict in a way that causes harm. Courts recognize this immediately. Guardians ad litem are trained to look for it. It does not help your case, and it hurts your child.

---

The moment when your child tells you the other parent hates you is the moment your co-parenting character is tested most directly. The child in front of you is watching to see whether you will put them first — whether you are the safe place they can bring the hard things without the hard things getting harder. Being that place, in that moment, is the most important thing you can do. Everything else can wait.
