Divorce is one of the most disruptive events in a child's life. Even when parents handle it well, children experience grief, confusion, loyalty conflicts, and anxiety that they often lack the language or the safe space to express. Therapy gives them that space.

Many parents wait too long to seek therapy for their children — often until the distress is severe or a school counselor raises a concern. Others avoid it because they fear it will be used against them in court, or because the other parent opposes it. This post addresses all of those concerns directly, along with practical guidance on finding the right therapist and navigating the process when parents disagree.

---

## Why Therapy During Divorce Is Different

Children in therapy during a divorce are not in ordinary therapy. They are processing an active, ongoing stressor — not a past event — while simultaneously being asked to manage loyalty conflicts between the two most important people in their lives. The quality and approach of the therapist matters enormously in this context.

A good child therapist in a divorce situation:
- Maintains clear neutrality between the parents
- Does not become a conduit for information between the household and the court
- Focuses on the child's emotional processing, not on fact-gathering
- Communicates with both parents, not just the one who initiated therapy
- Knows when to involve a guardian ad litem or refer to a custody evaluator

A therapist who aligns with one parent, shares session content with one parent but not the other, or who allows themselves to be used as a litigation tool is not serving the child — and can cause significant harm to both the child and the legal case.

## When to Seek Therapy: Signs Your Child Needs Support Now

Do not wait for a crisis. The earlier children have access to therapeutic support during a divorce, the better their outcomes tend to be. That said, certain signs indicate that the need is urgent rather than preventive.

### Signs That Warrant Immediate Attention

- Statements suggesting the child does not want to be alive, or that others would be better off without them — take these seriously every time, without exception
- Significant regression: a seven-year-old who has been toilet-trained suddenly is not, or a nine-year-old begins sleeping with a stuffed animal they abandoned years ago
- Refusal to eat, sleep disturbances that are severe or persistent, or significant unexplained physical complaints (stomachaches, headaches) with no medical cause
- Sudden, dramatic behavioral change at school — fighting, complete withdrawal, a sharp drop in grades over a short period
- Expressions of hopelessness, statements like "nothing matters" or "I don't care about anything anymore"
- Disclosure of anything that suggests abuse or witnessing violence

If your child makes any statement suggesting self-harm, contact your pediatrician or a mental health crisis line immediately and do not wait for the court process to catch up.

### Signs That Suggest Therapy Should Begin Soon

- Persistent sadness, crying, or emotional flatness that does not lift after the initial shock of the separation
- Increased anxiety — excessive worry about ordinary things, separation anxiety, reluctance to go to school
- Changes in friendships or withdrawal from activities the child previously enjoyed
- Expressing loyalty conflicts directly: *"I feel bad when I have fun at Dad's"* or *"I don't want to make Mom sad by saying I like it at Dad's house"*
- Acting out at one home but not the other, in ways that suggest the child is processing something they cannot articulate
- Asking frequent, repetitive questions about the divorce that suggest they are not finding reassurance

### Preventive Therapy: When There Are No Obvious Signs

You do not need to wait for distress signals. If you are entering a contested divorce, if the separation was sudden or acrimonious, or if there has been conflict in the home that the children have witnessed, starting therapy early is a reasonable and protective choice.

Framing it correctly matters. Therapy is not for "broken" kids. It is a tool — like a coach for the emotional side of a hard situation. Tell your child: *"We're going through a big change, and I want to make sure you have someone to talk to who is just for you — not me, not Dad, just you."*

## What Type of Therapist to Look For

### Credentials

Look for a licensed mental health professional with specific experience in child and adolescent therapy. In Virginia, relevant licenses include:

- **Licensed Professional Counselor (LPC)** — most common in private practice
- **Licensed Clinical Social Worker (LCSW)** — often has broader systems training, including family dynamics
- **Licensed Marriage and Family Therapist (LMFT)** — specifically trained in family systems, often well-suited for divorce contexts
- **Licensed Clinical Psychologist (Ph.D. or Psy.D.)** — can also conduct formal psychological evaluations if needed

A child psychiatrist (M.D.) is appropriate when medication may be part of the treatment picture — generally for more severe presentations like clinical depression, anxiety disorders, or ADHD that is being destabilized by the divorce stress.

### Experience With Divorce and Custody Situations

Ask directly: *"Do you have experience working with children of divorce, particularly in contested custody situations?"* This is a specific skill set. A therapist who primarily works with adults, or who has never navigated the dynamics of co-parenting conflict, is not the right fit.

A therapist experienced in divorce contexts will:
- Know how to structure communication with both parents without taking sides
- Understand what a guardian ad litem does and how therapy intersects with the legal process
- Have clear policies about what they will and will not testify to if subpoenaed
- Know the difference between their role as a treating therapist and the role of a forensic custody evaluator (these are distinct and should not be combined)

### Age-Appropriate Modalities

Different therapeutic approaches work better at different developmental stages:

- **Play therapy** is highly effective for children under 10 who cannot yet fully articulate their feelings verbally. Children express and process through play, and a trained play therapist knows how to facilitate that.
- **Cognitive-behavioral therapy (CBT)** works well for school-age children and teenagers who can engage verbally and who are experiencing anxiety or depressive symptoms.
- **Art therapy** and **sand tray therapy** offer non-verbal processing options for children across ages.
- **Family therapy** — with both parents present — can be useful once the acute conflict has stabilized, but is generally not appropriate in the early stages of a contested divorce.

### Practical Matters

- Verify that the therapist accepts your insurance or offers a sliding scale fee
- Confirm their cancellation policy and scheduling availability — consistency matters in child therapy
- Ask how they handle communication with parents: will they meet with each parent separately? Will they share session content? Under what circumstances?
- Ask their policy on subpoenas and court involvement — will they provide records? Will they testify? In what circumstances?

## How to Find a Therapist

**Start with your pediatrician.** Pediatricians often maintain referral lists for child therapists they trust and who have experience with family transitions. A pediatrician's referral also creates a medical record of the child's need for support, which can be relevant if the other parent contests therapy.

**Other sources:**
- Psychology Today's therapist finder (psychologytoday.com) — filter by age group, specialty (divorce, family conflict), and insurance
- Your employer's Employee Assistance Program (EAP) — often provides a limited number of free sessions
- Your child's school counselor — cannot provide therapy but can refer and often knows local practitioners
- The Virginia Counselors Association (vcacounselor.org)
- Your attorney — family law attorneys regularly work with therapists in custody cases and often have referrals

**What to ask in an initial phone call:**
- What is your experience working with children in contested custody situations?
- How do you structure communication with two parents who are not cooperating?
- What is your policy if one parent subpoenas your records?
- Do you work with play therapy / CBT / [relevant modality for your child's age]?
- What are your fees and do you take [your insurance]?

## Navigating Therapy When the Other Parent Objects

This is one of the most common flashpoints in contested custody cases, and it requires careful handling.

### Why Parents Resist Therapy for Their Children

The other parent may resist therapy for several reasons:

- They fear the therapist will become a witness against them
- They believe the requesting parent is using therapy to manufacture evidence
- They are in denial about the children's distress
- They want to control the narrative about the children's wellbeing
- They have philosophical or religious objections to mental health treatment

Some of these concerns are understandable. Some are not. None of them override a child's genuine need for support.

### What You Can Do Without the Other Parent's Agreement

In Virginia, if you have joint legal custody — which is the default in most cases — both parents have the right to participate in major decisions about the children's health, education, and welfare, including therapy.

This means that technically, starting therapy without the other parent's knowledge or agreement can create conflict and may be raised as an issue in litigation. However:

- **Emergency situations override this.** If your child is in acute distress or has made statements suggesting self-harm, you do not need the other parent's agreement to seek immediate help.
- **Pediatrician referrals carry weight.** If a physician recommends therapy, the other parent's refusal is harder to sustain legally.
- **You can seek a court order.** If therapy is clearly in the children's best interest and the other parent is unreasonably refusing, your attorney can file a motion asking the court to authorize therapy and to direct both parents to cooperate.

### Protecting the Therapy Relationship

Once therapy begins, your job as a parent is to support it — not to use it.

Do not:
- Ask your child what they talked about in therapy
- Ask the therapist what your child said about the other parent
- Attempt to use the therapist as a witness in your custody case
- Share the therapist's name or contact information with your attorney for litigation purposes without the therapist's knowledge

Do:
- Attend any parent sessions the therapist recommends
- Follow through on any recommendations the therapist makes (school support, schedule adjustments, referrals to other services)
- Communicate with the therapist about significant events (a contentious court date, a schedule change, a difficult conversation the child witnessed)
- Ensure the other parent has equal access to the therapist for parent sessions

A therapist who is used as a litigation tool will typically withdraw from treating the child. Losing a therapeutic relationship mid-case is destabilizing for the child and damages your credibility with the court.

## Therapy and the Legal Case

### What Therapists Can and Cannot Be Compelled to Share

In Virginia, communications between a therapist and a patient are protected by privilege. However, child therapy in a custody case occupies complicated legal terrain:

- **The child is the patient**, not the parent — so technically the child holds the privilege. Courts may appoint a guardian ad litem to make decisions about privilege on the child's behalf.
- **Mandatory reporting obligations** override privilege. If a therapist learns of abuse, neglect, or a credible threat of harm, they are required by Virginia law to report it regardless of privilege.
- **Courts can order disclosure** of therapy records in some custody cases, particularly when the child's mental health is directly at issue.
- **Treating therapists are different from forensic evaluators.** A treating therapist's role is to help the child — not to evaluate the parents or make custody recommendations. Courts generally give less weight to a treating therapist's opinions about parental fitness than to a formally appointed custody evaluator's.

### How Seeking Therapy Affects Your Case

Seeking therapy for your children almost always reflects positively on you. It demonstrates:
- That you are attentive to your children's emotional needs
- That you are putting their wellbeing ahead of your own convenience or the cost of an additional appointment
- That you recognize the divorce is hard on them and are doing something constructive about it

A parent who refuses to seek therapy for visibly struggling children, or who blocks the other parent from doing so, tends to look either oblivious or self-interested — neither of which is a good look in a custody case.

## After the Divorce: When to Continue and When to Conclude

Therapy does not automatically end when the divorce is finalized. The post-divorce transition — new living arrangements, possible school changes, adjusting to two-household life, and eventual new relationships in the parents' lives — can be as stressful as the divorce itself.

Let the therapist guide the timing of termination. A child who has processed the acute phase and developed healthy coping strategies may not need ongoing weekly sessions, but benefit from periodic check-ins around significant transitions (a parent moving, a new stepparent, a custody modification).

The goal of therapy is not for your child to be fine with what happened to their family. It is for them to have the tools to move forward with their relationships, their sense of self, and their capacity for trust intact. That is worth whatever it costs — in time, money, and effort — to make it happen.
