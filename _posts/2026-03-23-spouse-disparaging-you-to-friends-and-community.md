Divorce is painful enough without discovering that your spouse is campaigning against you in your social circle. But it happens constantly, and it takes many forms: a mutual friend pulls you aside to say your spouse has been telling people you were abusive, unfaithful, or financially irresponsible. Neighbors stop making eye contact. Your children's school community seems to have heard a version of events. Social media posts frame you as the villain. People who you thought were friends have quietly taken sides.

This experience is one of the most demoralizing parts of a contested divorce. It also has real consequences — for your relationships, for your children, and potentially for your legal case. Understanding those consequences, and responding strategically rather than emotionally, makes a significant difference.

---

## What Is Actually Happening

When a spouse disparages the other parent in the community, it is rarely random. In most cases it serves one or more of the following purposes:

**Controlling the narrative.** The first person to tell the story often shapes how people understand it. A spouse who gets to mutual friends, family members, and community contacts early — before you have had a chance to process what is happening, let alone present your own perspective — has a significant advantage in the court of public opinion.

**Building a support network.** Divorce is socially isolating. Your spouse may be working to consolidate the mutual social network on their side, ensuring that the people you might otherwise turn to for support have already formed a negative impression of you.

**Creating witnesses.** Friends and community members who have heard your spouse's account of the marriage may later be called as witnesses, asked to submit declarations, or consulted by a guardian ad litem. A spouse who has been telling the same story to the same people for months has effectively pre-briefed their witness pool.

**Pressuring you to settle.** Social isolation and reputational damage are forms of leverage. If you are being ostracized from your community, losing friendships, and managing the fallout of your spouse's narrative on top of everything else the divorce involves, the pressure to make it stop by settling on unfavorable terms can be significant.

**Projecting.** In high-conflict divorces, the spouse who is most aggressively disparaging the other is sometimes the one whose conduct is most problematic. Publicly establishing the other party as the bad actor can be a pre-emptive attempt to discredit whatever that party might later say about them.

## The Legal Dimension

### Does Disparagement Affect Your Case?

It depends on what is being said, to whom, and whether it reaches the children.

**Disparagement that stays between adults in the community** — friends, neighbors, acquaintances — is generally painful but not directly actionable in the divorce proceedings. Courts do not typically wade into the question of what one spouse said to mutual friends unless it crosses into defamation, harassment, or interference with a legal proceeding.

**Disparagement that reaches the children** is a different matter entirely. A parent who tells the community negative things about the other parent almost always tells (or allows to reach) the children as well. If the children hear from friends, relatives, or community members that one parent has been characterized as abusive, dishonest, or dangerous — because the other parent has been spreading that characterization — it is part of the alienation picture that Virginia courts take seriously.

**Disparagement that constitutes defamation** — false statements of fact, not mere opinions — can potentially support a civil claim separate from the divorce. This is a high bar and a separate legal matter, but it exists.

**Disparagement that reaches your employer or professional community** raises its own set of concerns, particularly if it affects your income or employment — which directly affects the financial resolution of the divorce.

**Disparagement involving the guardian ad litem or custody evaluator** — attempting to influence a court-appointed neutral by feeding them a one-sided narrative through community contacts — is a form of misconduct that courts respond to seriously when it is identified.

### Mutual Non-Disparagement Clauses

Many separation agreements and custody orders include mutual non-disparagement clauses: provisions that prohibit both parties from making negative statements about the other to third parties, particularly in the presence of the children. If such a clause is in your agreement or order, violations are enforceable through contempt proceedings.

If you do not yet have a court order or separation agreement, propose a mutual non-disparagement clause through your attorney. Proposing it demonstrates that you are committed to co-parenting appropriately. If your spouse refuses to agree to it, that refusal is itself informative — and potentially relevant to how a court views the situation.

## What to Do

### Document What You Are Hearing

When you learn that your spouse has said something specific to a third party, write it down immediately: the date you heard it, who told you, what they said your spouse said, and any context around when and where the original statement was made.

This documentation serves two purposes. First, it builds a record of a pattern if the conduct continues. Second, it identifies potential witnesses — people who heard the statements directly and can describe them.

Note: what someone tells you that your spouse said to them is hearsay. A court cannot simply take your word for it. The value of your documentation is in identifying the actual witnesses — the people who heard the statements directly — not in creating a record of what you were told.

### Identify and Preserve Witness Statements

If mutual friends are willing to tell you what was said, some of them may be willing to provide a written statement or even testify about it. Ask your attorney before approaching anyone about providing a formal statement — the way you solicit witness information matters, and doing it incorrectly can create its own problems.

Written communications in which your spouse made negative statements — texts, emails, social media posts — are more useful than witness accounts of oral statements because they are direct, unambiguous evidence. If you have access to any such communications, preserve them immediately.

### Respond to the Community Thoughtfully — Not Defensively

How you respond to your spouse's narrative in the community matters almost as much as the content of that narrative. People who are closely watching a divorce — and many will be — form judgments not just based on what they hear about each party but on how each party conducts themselves.

A defensive, extensive rebuttal to every claim your spouse has made — a counternarrative delivered to every mutual contact — comes across as desperate, angry, and consumed by the conflict. It also puts your friends and acquaintances in an uncomfortable position and often backfires.

A measured, brief response is almost always more effective. When someone tells you what your spouse has been saying, you do not have to rebut it in detail. Something like: *"I appreciate you telling me. There are two sides to this, and I'm not going to speak negatively about [spouse's name]. Things are difficult right now, but I'm focused on getting through this and being there for the kids."*

This response accomplishes several things simultaneously: it declines to engage in a mud fight, it signals maturity and restraint, it plants the idea that your spouse's account may be one-sided, and it puts your children at the center of your priorities — which is where they belong.

### Decide Which Friendships Are Worth Preserving

A contested divorce reveals, with painful clarity, who in your social network will remain genuinely neutral and who will not. Some mutual friends will position themselves firmly with your spouse. Some will try to maintain relationships with both of you but find it increasingly difficult. Some will withdraw from both of you entirely rather than navigate the conflict.

Accept that some of these friendships will not survive the divorce. Attempting to fight for them by campaigning against your spouse, providing counter-evidence, or pressuring people to take your side tends to accelerate their departure rather than prevent it.

Focus your energy on the friendships with people who are genuinely supportive, who do not require you to perform for them, and who are not also serving as your spouse's confidants. Be cautious about how much you share with anyone who maintains regular contact with your spouse — even well-intentioned people relay information.

### Build Your Own Support Network

The best antidote to reputational damage in a shared social circle is a robust individual support network that is not entangled with your spouse at all. This may mean reconnecting with friends from before the marriage, investing in relationships that are yours alone, or building new connections through work, community involvement, or activities you have taken up during or after the separation.

People who know you independently — not as half of your former marriage — form their own impressions of you based on direct experience. Those impressions are not affected by what your spouse says to mutual contacts.

### Protect Your Professional Reputation Separately

If your spouse's disparagement is reaching your professional community — colleagues, clients, professional associations, your employer — treat this as a separate and urgent concern. Reputational damage in a professional context can affect your income, which affects the entire financial resolution of your divorce.

If the statements your spouse is making about you to professional contacts are false and damaging, your attorney should know about this immediately. The remedies may include a formal cease-and-desist letter, a defamation claim, or — if the conduct is sufficiently connected to the divorce proceedings — an argument for enhanced attorney's fees or other sanctions.

## What Not to Do

### Do Not Respond in Kind

The most common and most damaging response to a spouse's disparagement campaign is to launch one in return. It feels justified. It is almost universally harmful.

A counter-campaign against your spouse — telling your version of events to mutual friends, posting your side of the story publicly, texting people to correct the record — does not restore your reputation. It creates an impression of two warring adults who have lost perspective, both of whom are saying negative things about each other. The community does not know who to believe. They often end up withdrawing from both of you.

More importantly, anything you say about your spouse in the community can be characterized as evidence of poor co-parenting judgment in your divorce case. The mutual friend you vented to last month may have passed along what you said. The text you sent correcting the record might be produced in discovery.

The standard for your own conduct is the same one you would want a judge to apply when reviewing it: *Is this something I would be comfortable explaining in court?*

### Do Not Involve the Children in the Narrative War

Children who attend the same schools, sports programs, and community activities as their parents hear what the community is saying. They will eventually encounter your spouse's version of events through friends, classmates, or other parents.

Do not weaponize this. Do not use the community reaction as evidence that your children should distrust the other parent. Do not tell your children what their parent has been saying about you in hopes that they will take your side. Do not coach them on what to say to teachers, coaches, or community members who might ask.

Children who are drawn into a reputational battle between their parents are being harmed. Courts know this, and they hold it against the parent who drew them in.

### Do Not Post on Social Media

This cannot be overstated. Social media posts during a contested divorce — expressing your side of the story, posting indirect references to the situation ("so grateful for the people who actually know me"), sharing content that obviously relates to the conflict — are among the most reliably damaging things a party can do to their own case.

Everything you post is potentially discoverable. Screenshots circulate without your control. Your spouse's attorney is watching your accounts. A post you made to 200 people in a moment of frustration can be printed and handed to a judge.

If you cannot stop posting entirely, impose a strict personal rule: nothing about the divorce, your spouse, relationships, finances, or anything that could be read as connected to the case. Nothing.

### Do Not Use Mutual Friends as Intelligence Sources

Mutual friends who are sharing information about what your spouse is saying may have good intentions — or they may be passing information in both directions. Either way, using them as sources of intelligence about your spouse's conduct and strategy puts them in a terrible position and often produces unreliable information.

Treat information from mutual friends as useful context — worth documenting if it describes specific statements — but do not rely on it as strategy and do not share your own strategy in return.

## When the Conduct Crosses Into Harassment or Defamation

There is a point at which a spouse's disparagement campaign crosses from painful social conduct into legally actionable behavior.

**Defamation** requires a false statement of fact (not an opinion), communicated to a third party, that causes damage. Statements like *"my spouse cheated on me"* (if true) or *"my spouse is a terrible person"* (an opinion) are not defamation. Statements like *"my spouse embezzled money from their employer"* (if false) or *"my spouse abused our children"* (if false) potentially are.

Virginia recognizes both defamation per se — statements that are defamatory on their face, such as false accusations of criminal conduct or sexual misconduct — and defamation per quod, which requires proof of actual damages.

**Harassment** occurs when repeated contact or communication — including through third parties — rises to the level of conduct that would cause a reasonable person substantial emotional distress.

**Interference with a legal proceeding** — attempting to influence witnesses, tamper with evidence, or improperly contact court-appointed neutrals — is a more serious matter with direct consequences in the divorce case itself.

If your spouse's conduct has reached any of these thresholds, raise it with your attorney immediately. The remedies may include injunctive relief (a court order prohibiting the conduct), monetary damages in a separate civil action, or sanctions in the divorce proceedings.

## The Long Game

The reputational damage that a spouse inflicts in a community during a divorce often heals faster than it seems like it will in the moment. Social networks that fracture during a divorce often restructure themselves over time. People who heard a one-sided account often update their understanding when they see how each person actually lives their life in the years that follow.

The person who emerges from the divorce with their integrity intact — who did not engage in a counter-campaign, who treated people with dignity, who focused on their children and their own forward movement — almost always recovers socially faster than the person who burned every available relationship in the fight.

The best response to someone telling a damaging story about you in your community is to give the community something different to observe directly.
