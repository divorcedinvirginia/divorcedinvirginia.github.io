One of the most violating experiences during a divorce is coming home to find that your spouse has been in the house without your knowledge and that things are missing. Whether it is financial documents, electronics, jewelry, sentimental items, or furniture, the sense of violation is real — and in a contested divorce, so are the legal consequences.

What you can do depends significantly on the specific circumstances: whether you or your spouse has legal right to be in the home, whether a court order governs the situation, and what was taken. This post covers all of those scenarios.

---

## First: Understand the Legal Baseline

Unless a court has ordered otherwise, both spouses have a legal right to be in the marital home during a divorce — regardless of whose name is on the deed or lease. Marriage confers rights of access and possession that do not automatically end at separation.

This is a fact that surprises many people. Your spouse moving out does not extinguish their legal right to re-enter the home. A separation agreement or court order can change this, but a separation alone does not.

This also means that changing the locks on your spouse without a court order is legally precarious. Some Virginia courts have held that doing so can constitute constructive interference with your spouse's property rights, and it can be used against you in the divorce proceedings. Do not change the locks unless:

- You have a court order granting you exclusive use and possession of the home
- You have a protective order that restricts your spouse's access
- Your attorney has specifically advised you that it is appropriate in your circumstances

If you do not yet have a court order, your immediate legal options are more limited than you might expect — but there are still clear steps you should take.

## If There Is No Court Order Yet

### Document Everything Immediately

The moment you realize your spouse has been in the home and removed items, begin creating a record.

**Photograph every room.** Do it immediately — before you move anything, before you clean up, and before the situation changes further. Photograph the spaces where items are missing, the entry points that were used, and any evidence of how access was gained (a window that was unlocked, a side door that was forced, a key pad with a changed code that your spouse apparently still knew).

**Make a written inventory of what is missing.** Be as specific as possible: describe each item, its approximate value, when you last saw it, and where it was located. For high-value items — jewelry, art, electronics, collectibles — gather any purchase records, appraisals, insurance documents, or photographs you have. The more specific your inventory, the stronger your legal position.

**Note the date and approximate time.** If you can narrow down when the entry occurred — based on when you last saw the items, neighbor observations, or security camera footage — document that.

**Check your security systems.** If you have a doorbell camera, security system, or smart lock with an activity log, review and screenshot the records immediately. These often overwrite after a short period. Access logs showing when your spouse entered the home are powerful evidence.

**Check with neighbors.** A neighbor who saw your spouse's car in the driveway at a specific time, or who saw items being loaded, is a potential witness. Note their observations in writing.

### Contact Your Attorney the Same Day

This is not a situation to wait on. Call your attorney the day you discover the unauthorized entry and removal. Your attorney needs to know because:

- You may need to file an emergency motion immediately
- The taking of marital property without agreement can constitute dissipation — a factor courts consider in equitable distribution
- Your attorney can send a preservation letter or cease and desist demanding that your spouse stop and return items
- If the entry itself was improper, your attorney can seek sanctions

Do not call your spouse directly about this. Do not send an angry text. Do not post about it. Any unilateral emotional reaction you take — confronting your spouse, threatening to change the locks, removing items from your spouse's new residence in retaliation — will be used against you in court.

### Request an Emergency Pendente Lite Hearing

If your spouse is systematically removing marital property, you can ask the court for emergency pendente lite relief: an order granting you exclusive use and possession of the home and an injunction prohibiting your spouse from returning to the property or removing any additional items.

Emergency motions are reserved for situations with genuine urgency — ongoing or repeated conduct, imminent threat of further removal, or removal of items that are particularly significant (financial documents, irreplaceable personal property, items needed for the children's daily life). If the removal was a single incident and no further access is anticipated, a standard motion on a regular hearing schedule may be more appropriate.

An emergency injunction against asset dissipation can be issued ex parte — meaning without advance notice to your spouse — if the circumstances justify it. The standard is high: you must show that giving advance notice would give your spouse the opportunity to cause further irreparable harm before the court can act.

### Consider a Protective Order if Safety Is a Factor

If your spouse's entry into the home involves any element of intimidation, threats, or physical confrontation — or if there is a history of domestic violence — a protective order may be appropriate in addition to the divorce-related remedies. A protective order is a criminal court matter (separate from your divorce proceedings) and can prohibit your spouse from entering the home and from contacting you entirely.

Do not hesitate to seek a protective order if you feel unsafe. The threshold for a preliminary protective order in Virginia is not high — a reasonable fear of harm is sufficient.

## If There Is a Court Order Governing the Home

If a court has already entered a pendente lite order granting you exclusive use and possession of the marital home, the situation changes significantly. Your spouse's unauthorized entry is not just a civil dispute — it is a potential violation of a court order.

### Document the Violation the Same Way

The same documentation steps apply: photograph everything, inventory what is missing, check security system records, and note the time and method of entry. The difference is that this documentation now supports not just a motion for additional relief, but a contempt proceeding.

### File a Motion for Rule to Show Cause

When a party violates a court order, the remedy is a motion for rule to show cause — a motion asking the court to order your spouse to appear and explain why they should not be held in contempt for violating the order.

Contempt in a Virginia divorce case can result in:

- Fines
- Payment of your attorney's fees for bringing the contempt proceeding
- Incarceration, in cases of willful and egregious violation
- An adverse inference in the underlying divorce case — the court may take the violation into account in deciding property division, custody, or other matters

A spouse who violates an exclusive possession order by entering the home and removing property has taken an action that courts take seriously. The contempt record also becomes part of the broader narrative about that spouse's willingness to abide by court orders — which matters in every subsequent proceeding.

### Call the Police If Appropriate

If your spouse enters the home in violation of a court order granting you exclusive possession, you can call the police. Law enforcement officers can respond and, depending on the specific language of the court order and the nature of the conduct, may be able to take action — including requiring your spouse to leave and, in some circumstances, citing or arresting them for the violation.

Bring a copy of the court order with you when you contact law enforcement. Officers are more able to act when they can see the specific terms of the order your spouse has violated.

If a protective order is in place and your spouse has violated it by entering the home, the police response is clearer: violation of a protective order is a criminal offense in Virginia.

## What Happens to the Items That Were Taken

Whether or not you get the specific items back depends on what they were and what the court orders.

### Personal vs. Marital Property

Courts distinguish between personal property (items that belonged to one spouse before the marriage, or that were received as a gift or inheritance by one spouse during the marriage) and marital property (everything else acquired during the marriage).

Your spouse has an arguable right to remove their own personal property from the marital home — items that were theirs before the marriage or that are clearly theirs alone. They do not have a unilateral right to remove marital property — property that belongs to both of you — and certainly not without your knowledge or agreement.

If the items removed were marital property, the court will account for them in the equitable distribution. Your spouse's unauthorized removal of marital property and your detailed inventory of what was taken will be relevant evidence.

### Court Can Order Items Returned

If marital property has been removed, your attorney can seek a court order requiring your spouse to return specific items. Courts have authority to order the return of property and to impose sanctions if the order is not complied with.

However, getting a court order is not the same as getting the property back. If your spouse has sold items, damaged them, or refuses to produce them, the court may instead assign a dollar value and credit you that amount in the property division. Keep your inventory and any documentation of value (purchase records, appraisals, comparable sales) so the court can make that calculation.

### Financial Documents and Records

If your spouse has removed financial documents — tax returns, account statements, investment records, business records — this has specific legal implications beyond the property issue. Financial documents in the marital home are subject to discovery, and removing them can constitute spoliation — the destruction or concealment of evidence.

Report the removal of financial documents to your attorney immediately. Your attorney can:

- Issue a litigation hold demand, formally putting your spouse and their attorney on notice that the documents must be preserved
- Seek sanctions if documents are concealed or destroyed
- Subpoena the originating institutions directly to obtain copies of records that were removed

The fact that your spouse removed financial documents may itself be evidence of an attempt to hide information from the discovery process.

## Preventing Future Unauthorized Access

Once you have addressed the immediate situation, take steps to make future unauthorized access harder — within the bounds of what your court order (or absence of one) permits.

### Change the Locks — But Only If You Can

If you have a court order granting you exclusive possession of the home, change the locks immediately. This is entirely appropriate and any competent attorney will advise you to do it.

If you do not have such an order, consult your attorney before changing the locks. In some circumstances, changing the locks without a court order can be characterized as locking out your spouse from their own home — which can create legal complications and antagonize a court that views it as unilateral conduct.

### Secure Garage Codes and Smart Access

Your spouse may not be using a physical key. Smart locks, garage door openers, keypad entries, and shared smart home systems can all provide access without a traditional key. Change codes on every digital entry point if you have court authority to do so. Revoke shared access to smart home apps. If your vehicles are in the garage, check that the garage door codes your spouse knows have been changed.

### Inform Your Security Monitoring Company

If you have a monitored security system, contact the provider and remove your spouse as an authorized contact. Your spouse may have the ability to arm or disarm the system, receive alerts, or — in some cases — disable monitoring remotely. Removing their access protects both the physical security of the home and the integrity of your access logs as evidence.

### Install a Doorbell Camera If You Do Not Have One

Doorbell cameras with activity logging provide timestamped evidence of who approaches or enters the home. They are inexpensive, easy to install, and have become standard evidence in divorce proceedings where unauthorized access is an issue. If your spouse knows a camera is present, it may deter future entries. If it does not, you will have a clear record.

### Ask a Trusted Neighbor to Keep Watch

A neighbor who is willing to note your spouse's visits — with dates, times, what was observed, and whether items were visibly being removed — is a potential witness. You do not need to ask them to confront your spouse or to do anything beyond observing and noting what they see from their own property.

## What Not to Do

The emotional response to discovering that your spouse has been in your home and taken things is powerful. The actions driven by that emotion are almost universally counterproductive in a divorce case.

**Do not retaliate by going to your spouse's residence and removing items.** Two wrongs do not cancel each other out in court. You will simply be adding a second unauthorized taking to the record, and you will lose credibility on the issue of your spouse's conduct.

**Do not confront your spouse in person.** A confrontation about the taking — especially one that becomes heated — creates new evidence against you: statements made in anger, physical contact that could be characterized as threatening, or conduct that gives your spouse grounds to seek a protective order against you.

**Do not send threatening messages.** A text that says *"I know you took the jewelry and if you don't bring it back I'm going to make your life hell in this divorce"* is exactly the kind of message that ends up in a motion in front of a judge.

**Do not dispose of your spouse's remaining property in retaliation.** Throwing out, selling, or damaging items that belong to your spouse is dissipation of marital property and potentially conversion — a civil wrong for which you can be held liable.

**Do not move out of the home in response.** If you leave the home because you are upset about the entry and taking, and your spouse then moves back in, you may have significantly complicated your claim to exclusive possession and your custody status quo. Stay put and use the legal process.

---

Unauthorized entry and removal of property during a divorce is a serious matter — both as a personal violation and as a legal issue. The right response is documentation, prompt communication with your attorney, and systematic use of the legal tools available. Reactive, emotional responses feel satisfying in the moment and are almost always damaging to your case.
