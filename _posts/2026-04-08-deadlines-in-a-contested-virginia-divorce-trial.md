A contested Virginia divorce that proceeds to trial is not a single event — it is a process that unfolds over months or years, structured around a series of deadlines that govern what can happen, when, and in what order. These deadlines are set by statute, by court rule, by local circuit court practice, and by the scheduling order the judge enters in your specific case.

Missing a deadline in a contested divorce is not a minor administrative inconvenience. Depending on which deadline is missed and by how long, the consequences can include exclusion of evidence, loss of discovery rights, sanctions, adverse inferences, and in serious cases, dismissal of claims or defenses. Understanding the deadline structure of a contested Virginia divorce is one of the most important things a party can do to protect their position throughout the case.

---

## The Scheduling Order: The Governing Document

Once a contested divorce is filed and both parties are represented, the court will typically enter a **scheduling order** — a case-specific document that sets the deadlines for all phases of the litigation. Every scheduling order is different, but most cover the same core phases:

- Discovery cutoff (the date by which all discovery must be completed)
- Expert witness designation deadline
- Deadline for dispositive motions
- Pretrial conference date
- Trial date

The scheduling order is not a suggestion. It is a court order, and violations are treated accordingly. If you need an extension on any scheduling order deadline, your attorney must move for it in advance — ideally before the deadline passes — and demonstrate good cause. Courts are not uniformly sympathetic to extension requests, particularly when the requesting party is responsible for the delay.

Keep a copy of the scheduling order. Know every date on it. Your attorney should be calendar-managing these deadlines, but you should understand them as well.

## Pre-Filing Deadlines

### The Separation Period

Before a contested Virginia divorce can be granted on no-fault grounds, the parties must be separated for a defined period:

- **One year** if the parties have minor children
- **Six months** if the parties have no minor children and have a signed separation agreement

Virginia Code § 20-91 governs. The separation period begins on the date the parties physically separate with the intent of at least one party that the separation be permanent. The date of separation is often itself a contested factual issue.

The separation period is not a deadline in the traditional sense — it is a precondition. A divorce on no-fault grounds cannot be finalized until this period has elapsed. If the case involves fault grounds (adultery, cruelty, desertion), the fault ground may be available immediately, but no-fault grounds still require the separation period.

### Filing Deadline After Grounds Arise

Virginia does not impose a statute of limitations on divorce itself, but **adultery as a ground for divorce** does carry a time-limited bar: Virginia Code § 20-94 provides that adultery cannot be used as a ground if the parties have cohabited after discovering the adultery. Resuming the marital relationship after discovering an affair effectively waives the adultery ground.

## Service of Process Deadline

After the divorce complaint is filed, the defendant spouse must be served. Virginia Rule 3:5 requires that service be completed within **one year** of the filing of the complaint. If service is not accomplished within that period, the court may dismiss the case. In practice, a defendant who is aware of the filing will typically be served promptly, but in cases where a spouse is evading service, tracking the one-year deadline is important.

---

## Responsive Pleading Deadline

Once served, the defendant spouse has **21 days** to file an answer (or other responsive pleading) if served within Virginia (Virginia Rule 3:8). If served outside Virginia, the period is **30 days**. If served by publication, the period is **50 days** from the first date of publication.

Failing to answer within this period leaves the defendant in default, which can allow the plaintiff to obtain a default judgment. In practice, most represented defendants file timely answers, but the deadline matters in cases where the defendant is unrepresented or attempting to delay.

## Discovery Deadlines

Discovery is the process by which each party obtains information from the other — financial records, communications, business documents, parenting records — and it is governed by a set of interlocking deadlines that are among the most consequential in the case.

### Interrogatories, Requests for Production, and Requests for Admission

Under the Virginia Rules of Civil Procedure:

- A party has **21 days** to respond to interrogatories (written questions) — Rule 4:8
- A party has **21 days** to respond to requests for production of documents — Rule 4:9
- A party has **21 days** to respond to requests for admission — Rule 4:11

These 21-day deadlines run from the date of service, not the date of receipt. Missing them is consequential: failure to respond to requests for admission within the deadline means the matters are **deemed admitted** — a potentially devastating result in cases where the requests were crafted strategically.

Extensions to discovery response deadlines are common and usually obtained by agreement between counsel, but they must be documented in writing. Do not assume an oral agreement to extend a discovery deadline is enforceable.

### Supplementation of Discovery Responses

A party who has responded to discovery has an ongoing obligation to supplement those responses if they learn that a prior response was incomplete or inaccurate — Rule 4:1(e). There is no specific deadline for supplementation other than the general requirement that it be done "seasonably" — meaning promptly after the party learns of the need to supplement. Failure to supplement can result in exclusion of the undisclosed information at trial.

### The Discovery Cutoff

The scheduling order will set a date by which all discovery must be **completed** — not just propounded. This means that if you want to take a deposition, the deposition must be concluded, not merely scheduled, by the discovery cutoff. If you want documents produced before trial, the request must be served, the response period must run, and any follow-up must be completed before the cutoff.

The practical consequence of the discovery cutoff is that you should work backward from it. If you have 21 days to receive a response to a document request, and you need time to review those documents before the cutoff, you need to serve the request at least 21 days — plus review time — before the cutoff. Late discovery requests that cannot be completed before the cutoff will typically be rejected by the opposing party and, if contested, by the court.

### Motions to Compel

If a party fails to respond to discovery or provides inadequate responses, the requesting party must file a **motion to compel** within a reasonable time. Virginia courts generally expect motions to compel to be filed promptly after the response deadline passes — not weeks later. The court will not look favorably on a party who waited months to complain about a discovery deficiency and then raised it at the eve of trial.

## Expert Witness Deadlines

If either party intends to call an expert witness — a vocational evaluator, a business appraiser, a real estate appraiser, a forensic accountant, a child psychologist — the scheduling order will include a deadline by which that expert must be **designated** and their opinions disclosed.

Expert designations typically require:
- The identity of the expert
- The subject matter of their testimony
- A summary of their opinions and the basis for them
- Their qualifications and any publications
- Their fee schedule

Failure to designate an expert before the deadline generally results in **preclusion** — the expert cannot testify at trial. Courts enforce expert designation deadlines strictly, because the purpose of the deadline is to give the other party time to retain a rebuttal expert and conduct the expert's deposition before trial.

If you anticipate needing an expert — and in most contested Virginia divorces involving significant assets, a business, or contested custody, you will — the expert must be retained and their analysis substantially completed before the designation deadline. Waiting until the deadline arrives to begin working with the expert is one of the most common and costly mistakes in divorce litigation.

### Expert Depositions

After an expert is designated, the opposing party has the right to depose them. Expert depositions must also be completed before the discovery cutoff. This compresses the timeline further: the designation deadline must be early enough to allow time for the expert deposition before the cutoff date.

## Custody-Related Deadlines

### Guardian ad Litem Appointment

If the court appoints a guardian ad litem (GAL) for the children — which is common in contested custody cases — the GAL will typically be given a deadline to complete their investigation and file a report before the trial. The GAL's investigation involves interviews with the children, each parent, teachers, therapists, and other relevant people, and the report is one of the most significant documents in a contested custody trial.

The GAL deadline is set by the court's appointment order. Both parties should be aware of it because the GAL's investigation requires cooperation — failing to respond to the GAL's requests or making access to the children difficult can harm your position in the custody case.

### Home Study Deadlines

In some custody cases, the court orders a home study — a formal evaluation of each party's home environment. Home studies are conducted by licensed social workers and take time to schedule, complete, and report. If a home study has been ordered, confirm the deadline for its completion and ensure that your household is available and prepared for the evaluation.

## Pretrial Conference and Pretrial Disclosures

### Pretrial Conference

The scheduling order will set a date for a **pretrial conference** — a meeting between the judge (or court's designee) and the parties' attorneys to discuss the status of the case, the issues remaining for trial, and procedural matters. The pretrial conference typically occurs several weeks before trial.

At or before the pretrial conference, the court may require the parties to submit:
- A joint pretrial statement identifying contested and uncontested facts
- Lists of proposed trial exhibits
- Lists of proposed witnesses
- Stipulations on issues the parties have agreed to

These submissions have their own deadlines, typically set by local court rule or by the pretrial conference order.

### Exhibit and Witness Lists

Most Virginia circuit courts require that exhibit and witness lists be filed and exchanged before trial — often **10 to 14 days** in advance, depending on the local rule. Exhibits or witnesses not on the list may be excluded at trial. This deadline requires that you and your attorney have identified every document you intend to offer into evidence and every witness you intend to call, organized and disclosed, well before the trial date.

---

## Financial Disclosure Deadlines

Virginia Code § 20-121.02 authorizes courts to require mandatory financial disclosure in divorce cases. Many Virginia circuit courts have local rules or standing orders requiring each party to exchange a **financial disclosure statement** — a detailed accounting of income, expenses, assets, and liabilities — within a specified time after the case is filed. Common deadlines are **21 to 45 days** after the responsive pleading is filed, but this varies by jurisdiction.

The financial disclosure statement is not the same as formal discovery. It is an initial mandatory exchange that establishes the financial picture of the marriage and each party's current situation. Failure to file it on time, or failure to file it accurately, can result in sanctions and adverse inferences.

## The Five-Year Dismissal Deadline

Virginia Code § 8.01-335 provides that any case in which no order has been entered for **five years** may be dismissed by the court for inactivity. While most actively contested divorces do not approach this threshold, it is relevant in cases that have stalled — through settlement negotiations that dragged on without resolution, through the illness of a party or attorney, or through other delays. If your case is approaching the five-year mark without resolution, take action. An administrative dismissal eliminates the case without prejudice to refiling, but refiling restarts the entire process and forfeits the original filing date.

## Post-Trial Deadlines

### Motion for Reconsideration or Motion to Reconsider

If the trial court enters a ruling that you believe is erroneous, a motion to reconsider or a motion to set aside must typically be filed within **21 days** of the entry of the final order under Virginia Code § 8.01-428. This deadline is jurisdictional in some contexts — missing it can forfeit the right to seek reconsideration entirely.

### Notice of Appeal

If you wish to appeal a final divorce decree or equitable distribution order to the Virginia Court of Appeals, the notice of appeal must be filed within **30 days** of the entry of the final order — Rule 5A:6. This deadline is strictly enforced. Missing it ends your right to appeal regardless of the merits of your argument.

The appeal deadline applies to the final order, not to interlocutory orders entered during the case. However, certain pendente lite orders may be separately appealable under limited circumstances.

### Enforcement of the Final Order

After the divorce decree is entered, the parties have ongoing obligations under it — property transfers, retirement account division through a QDRO (Qualified Domestic Relations Order), insurance changes, deed transfers. Many of these have their own deadlines built into the order itself.

**QDROs** in particular must be prepared and submitted to the retirement plan administrator to be effective. There is no universal statutory deadline for QDRO submission, but unreasonable delay can complicate the process — particularly if the account holder retires, changes jobs, or dies before the QDRO is entered.

## Local Rules and Jurisdiction-Specific Deadlines

Virginia's circuit courts have significant autonomy in how they manage their dockets, and **local rules vary substantially** by jurisdiction. Fairfax County's family law docket operates differently from Richmond City Circuit Court, which operates differently from smaller rural circuits. Some courts have standing orders that impose additional deadlines on family law cases.

Your attorney should be familiar with the specific local rules of the circuit where your case is pending. If you are representing yourself, review the circuit court's local rules on the court's website or in person at the clerk's office before doing anything that could affect a deadline.

## A Practical Note on Managing Deadlines

The deadline structure of a contested divorce creates a situation where missing a single date can materially harm a case that is otherwise strong. The discipline required to manage these deadlines — docketing them, working backward from them, building in lead time for preparation — is part of what you are paying your attorney to do.

That said, clients who understand the deadline structure are better clients. When your attorney tells you they need financial documents from you in the next two weeks, that request exists in a deadline context. When they ask you to review and approve an interrogatory response, the 21-day window for serving it is already running. The deadlines in your case are not abstract — they are the structure around which everything else is built, and they do not move without court approval.
