School registration feels like a logistical task. In a high-conflict divorce, it is a legal minefield.

The decisions made on a school registration form — which address the child is enrolled under, whose name appears first on emergency contacts, who is listed as the primary parent, who has pickup authorization, what phone numbers the school has — all carry implications that extend well beyond the administrative. In contested custody cases, these details can affect the status quo the court considers at trial, who gets information about the child, and whether one parent is being systematically excluded from their child's school life.

This post covers what you need to know about school registration during a high-conflict divorce, so that you are informed before registration season arrives — not after the form has already been submitted.

---

## Who Has the Right to Enroll

In Virginia, either parent with legal custody has the right to enroll the child in school. If you have joint legal custody — which is the default in most Virginia custody orders — both parents share decision-making authority over major educational decisions, including where the child attends school.

This means that a unilateral enrollment decision by one parent — particularly one that involves enrolling the child in a new school, in a new district, or in a school the other parent does not know about — is a violation of joint legal custody. Courts take this seriously.

### What to Do If Your Spouse Enrolls the Child Without Your Knowledge

If you discover that your spouse has enrolled your child in a new school without your agreement or knowledge:

- Contact your attorney immediately
- Do not contact the school directly and demand to un-enroll the child without legal guidance — this creates confusion for the school and can be characterized as interference
- Ask your attorney to send a formal letter to the school notifying them of the joint legal custody arrangement and requesting that all communications be sent to both parents
- File a motion with the court addressing the unilateral enrollment if the school is different from the one established during the marriage or in any existing court order

A parent who enrolls a child in a school specifically designed to change the child's geographic status quo — to establish them in a new area, with new connections, at a distance from the other parent — is engaging in conduct that courts view as an attempt to manipulate the custody outcome. Document the circumstances and address it promptly.

### Enrollment and Residency Requirements

Virginia public schools enroll children based on the address of the enrolling parent. This means that which parent enrolls the child, and under which address, determines which school the child attends. In a high-conflict case where the parents live in different school districts, this becomes a contested decision.

If you and your spouse live in different districts and have not yet reached a custody agreement:

- The school the child is currently attending — the established school — should generally continue unless both parents agree to a change or a court orders one
- A parent who moves to a new district and then enrolls the child in the new district's school without the other parent's agreement is making a unilateral educational decision that violates joint legal custody
- Courts asked to resolve a school district dispute will apply the best-interests framework and give significant weight to continuity — keeping the child in the school they know, with the teachers and friends they have

If your custody order specifies a school or school district, follow it. If it does not, document the status quo and raise the issue with your attorney before enrollment decisions are made unilaterally.

## The Registration Form: Every Line Matters

School registration forms feel like paperwork. In a high-conflict divorce, every line is potentially significant. Here is what to watch for.

### The Primary Parent or Guardian Field

Many school registration forms have a field for the "primary parent" or "primary guardian." In a joint custody arrangement, the designation of one parent as "primary" on the school's records can have downstream effects — who receives the default phone calls, who the school consults when decisions need to be made, who is assumed to have authority.

**If you are the enrolling parent:** Fill in the primary parent field with your own information. Do not leave the other parent off the form entirely — include them in all relevant sections. A registration form that omits the other parent entirely can be characterized as an attempt to exclude them from the child's educational life.

**If your spouse enrolled the child without you:** Contact your attorney about ensuring the school's records are updated to reflect both parents equally. Most schools are receptive to a straightforward request from a parent with joint legal custody to be added to the records — but confirm with your attorney first, and make the request in writing so there is a record of it.

### Emergency Contacts

The emergency contact section is where many high-conflict divorces produce their first serious school-related conflict. Emergency contacts determine:

- Who the school calls when the child is sick or injured and needs to leave
- Who is authorized to pick the child up
- Who the school turns to if a parent cannot be reached

**Both parents should be listed as emergency contacts.** A school registration form that lists only one parent — or that lists the other parent's name but with a number that is wrong, outdated, or goes to someone else — may reflect a deliberate attempt to ensure the school can reach only one parent in an emergency.

Verify that your contact information is on file with the school, that it is current, and that you are listed as an emergency contact. You can call the school's main office and confirm this directly. If your information is missing or incorrect, submit a written request to be added with the correct information, and copy the request to your attorney.

**Who to list beyond the parents:** Emergency contact lists typically have slots for additional contacts — grandparents, neighbors, family friends — to be called if parents are unavailable. In a high-conflict situation, be thoughtful about who is listed. A mutual friend who has aligned with the other parent is not an appropriate emergency contact for your child. The emergency contact list is not a place to establish social alliances.

### Pickup Authorization

Separately from emergency contacts, many schools maintain a list of individuals authorized to pick the child up. This list should be consistent with your custody order — if your custody order specifies that only parents may pick up the child, or that specific other individuals are authorized, the school's authorization list should reflect that.

If your custody order restricts the other parent's access in any way, provide the school with a copy of the relevant provisions. Schools cannot and should not be expected to enforce orders they have not seen.

If there is no court order yet, both parents should be on the pickup list. Neither parent can unilaterally remove the other from the pickup authorization list in the absence of a court order restricting access.

### Parent Communication Preferences

Most schools can be set up to send all communications — report cards, newsletters, event notices, progress reports, disciplinary notices, counselor communications — to both parents independently. This means both parents receive all information directly, without needing to rely on the other parent to pass it along.

Request this explicitly in writing at the time of registration, and confirm it is in place. A parent who controls the flow of information from the school to the household has a significant advantage in a high-conflict custody case — and in a child's daily life. Ensuring you receive information directly prevents you from being dependent on a non-cooperative co-parent to keep you informed about your own child's education.

### Medical Information and Healthcare Decision Authority

Registration forms often include fields for health information: allergies, medications, healthcare providers, and authorization for emergency medical treatment. In a joint legal custody arrangement, both parents share authority over medical decisions.

Make sure the school has accurate, complete medical information for your child regardless of which parent enrolled them. If you have concerns that the other parent has omitted important medical information — or has listed a healthcare provider you are not aware of — raise it with your attorney.

## Registration Fees and Who Pays

School registration in Virginia public schools does not typically involve a tuition fee, but there are common associated costs that can become a source of conflict in a high-conflict divorce.

### Common Registration-Related Costs

**School supply lists:** Many Virginia schools distribute supply lists at registration. The cost of school supplies — notebooks, pencils, folders, backpacks, calculators — ranges from under $50 to well over $100 depending on the grade level. These costs are incurred at the start of each school year and are recurring.

**Activity fees:** Many Virginia public schools charge annual activity or student fees — fees that cover access to extracurriculars, school events, and certain programs. These can range from $50 to several hundred dollars depending on the school.

**Technology fees:** Schools that issue devices to students or require students to provide their own devices may charge technology fees or include device costs in registration.

**Uniform or dress code costs:** If the school has a uniform requirement or specific dress code, the cost of appropriate clothing is part of the registration-season expense.

**Yearbooks, photos, and optional purchases:** Schools typically offer optional purchases — yearbooks, school photos, class merchandise — at registration time. These are optional but carry emotional weight when a child wants them and a parent is wrestling with the other parent over who should pay.

**Sports participation fees:** Many Virginia public school athletic programs charge participation fees — sometimes called pay-to-play fees — on top of any required equipment purchases. If your child is playing a school sport, these fees are part of the registration-season cost picture.

### How to Handle Cost-Sharing

The same principles that apply to youth sports costs apply here. Costs should be:

- **Addressed proactively in your separation agreement or co-parenting plan**, not negotiated ad hoc each August
- **Split in a manner agreed upon in advance** — either 50/50 or proportional to income, consistently applied
- **Documented** — keep receipts and payment records for every school-related expense you pay
- **Communicated in advance** — both parents should know what costs are coming before they are incurred

A common and workable approach is to alternate primary responsibility for back-to-school expenses by year — one parent covers school supplies and activity fees in odd years, the other in even years — with both parents contributing to large expenses like required technology. Whatever arrangement you use, put it in writing.

### When One Parent Refuses to Contribute

If you are covering registration-related costs that should be shared and the other parent is not contributing:

- Document every expense: keep receipts, note the date and what was purchased
- Send a written request for reimbursement through your co-parenting app or by email with a clear summary of what is owed
- If your co-parenting plan or court order establishes cost-sharing and the other parent refuses to comply, this is a contempt issue your attorney can address
- If no formal agreement is in place, the pattern of non-contribution is evidence for the court when establishing one

Do not deprive the child of school supplies, activity access, or participation in school programs because the other parent has not paid their share. The child's school experience should not be the casualty of an adult financial dispute. Pay what is necessary to ensure the child is fully equipped and participating, document what you paid, and pursue reimbursement through appropriate channels.

## Specific High-Conflict Scenarios

### The Other Parent Tries to Enroll the Child in a New School Without Your Agreement

Act immediately. The longer a child attends a new school, the more the new school becomes the status quo. A child who has been in a new school for three months has formed relationships, adjusted to a new routine, and begun to establish a new normal — which a court will be reluctant to disrupt.

Your attorney can file an emergency motion asking the court to require the child to remain at the established school pending a custody hearing. Bring documentation of:
- The school the child was attending before the change
- When you learned of the change
- The custody arrangement in place at the time
- Any evidence that the change was made without your knowledge or consent

### The Other Parent Lists You Incorrectly or Not at All on the Registration Form

Submit a written correction to the school's main office, addressed to the principal, stating your name, your relationship to the child, your contact information, and your legal status (joint legal custodian). Request in writing that the records be updated. Copy the letter to your attorney.

Schools are generally obligated under the Family Educational Rights and Privacy Act (FERPA) to provide both parents with access to educational records when both have legal custody. A school that systematically provides information only to one parent, at the other parent's direction, is not in compliance with FERPA. Your attorney can send a formal letter invoking your FERPA rights if the school is unresponsive.

### The Other Parent Uses a Different Address to Control the School District

If your spouse has used their new address to enroll the child in a school in their district — even though the child splits time between households and previously attended a school in your district — this is a unilateral educational decision that potentially violates joint legal custody.

Raise it with your attorney immediately. The legal question is which address is the child's primary domicile for school enrollment purposes, which is determined by the custody arrangement. If the custody arrangement is still being litigated, this may need to be resolved by the court.

### The Child's School Records Are Being Used as Leverage

In high-conflict cases, one parent occasionally attempts to use school records — attendance records, disciplinary records, counselor notes — selectively, producing them in litigation when they seem helpful and withholding them when they do not. FERPA gives both parents with joint legal custody the right to access their child's educational records independently. You do not need the other parent's permission or cooperation to request your child's records directly from the school.

Submit a written request to the principal's office for a complete copy of your child's educational records. Schools typically have 45 days to respond under FERPA. Review the records for anything you were not aware of and share relevant information with your attorney.

## Building a Co-Parenting Education Record

Regardless of the level of conflict in your case, maintaining your own active record of your child's educational life protects you and demonstrates your involvement to the court.

Keep a file — physical or digital — that includes:

- Copies of all registration forms and school documents
- Report cards and progress reports
- Teacher communications and your responses
- Records of school events you attended
- Notes on parent-teacher conferences you participated in
- Receipts for all school-related expenses you paid
- Any correspondence with the school about your parental rights or access to records

This file is evidence of your involvement as a parent. It is also a resource if your spouse later claims in custody proceedings that you were disengaged from your child's education. A complete, organized record of your engagement is more persuasive than any general description of it.

---

School registration is not the most dramatic moment in a high-conflict custody case. But it is one of the most consequential administrative moments — because the decisions made on a registration form in August shape your child's daily reality for the entire school year, and because courts evaluating custody look at whether both parents are genuinely involved in and informed about their child's education.

Being deliberate about school registration is not about winning a battle. It is about making sure your child's school is not another arena in which one parent has quietly consolidated control.
