The question of when to start dating again after a divorce does not have a single right answer. People arrive at the end of a marriage from vastly different places — some have been emotionally done with the relationship for years before the legal process began, others are still processing the loss when the decree is signed. The circumstances of the marriage, the length of the separation, the presence of children, and the degree of conflict all shape what readiness actually looks like.

What is consistent across most situations is this: dating before you are genuinely ready — whether that readiness is measured in legal, psychological, or relational terms — tends to create complications that extend the difficulty of the divorce rather than relieving it.

## The Legal Dimension: Dating While the Divorce Is Pending

In Virginia, dating during the pendency of the divorce carries legal risks that many people do not fully appreciate until they are already in a relationship.

### Adultery as a Ground for Divorce

Virginia is one of the few remaining states where adultery is both a ground for absolute divorce and a bar to spousal support. Under Virginia Code § 20-107.1, a court may deny spousal support entirely to a spouse who committed adultery — unless doing so would be "manifestly unjust" given the circumstances.

The critical point: **adultery in Virginia is not limited to affairs that occurred during the marriage.** Sexual conduct with a new partner after separation but before the divorce is finalized can, in some circumstances, be characterized as adultery for purposes of Virginia divorce law. Courts have historically applied this narrowly to conduct during the intact marriage, but the law is not entirely settled, and the risk is real enough that most Virginia family law attorneys advise against physical intimacy with new partners until the divorce is final.

If your spouse is seeking spousal support — or if you are — a new relationship that involves sexual contact can become a significant issue in the litigation. Opposing counsel will pursue it through discovery if they have reason to believe it exists.

### Impact on Equitable Distribution

Dating itself does not directly affect property division in Virginia. However, the **financial aspects of a new relationship** can. If you are spending marital funds on a new partner — dinners, gifts, travel, hotel stays — that spending can be characterized as dissipation of marital assets. Courts can, and do, adjust equitable distribution to account for dissipation, crediting the non-dissipating spouse for what was spent.

Keep marital finances and any new relationship entirely separate until the divorce is final. Do not pay for a new partner's expenses with joint accounts, credit cards in both names, or funds that are subject to equitable distribution.

### Impact on Custody

A new relationship during a contested custody dispute carries its own risks, independent of adultery law.

Virginia courts evaluate custody under the best interest of the child standard, which includes the moral fitness of each parent and the nature of each parent's home environment. A parent who introduces a new romantic partner — particularly one who moves in, or who is present during the child's parenting time — before the custody case is resolved gives the other parent a line of attack that is otherwise unavailable.

The argument is not that dating is inherently disqualifying. Courts are generally not interested in regulating adult romantic lives except where children are directly affected. The argument is that introducing a new partner into children's lives before the ink is dry on the custody order, before the children have had time to stabilize, and in the middle of a contested proceeding demonstrates a lack of judgment and prioritization of the parent's own needs over the children's stability.

Whether that argument is fair is a separate question from whether it will be made. It will be made, in virtually every contested custody case where a new relationship exists.

**Practical guidance:**
- Do not introduce any new partner to your children until the custody case is resolved and, ideally, until the relationship has sufficient stability to warrant it
- Do not allow a new partner to be present during exchanges or school events where the other parent will be present
- Do not post photographs of a new relationship on social media — opposing counsel screens social media as a matter of routine in contested cases
- Do not have a new partner stay overnight while the children are in your home, particularly during the pendency of the divorce

## The Psychological Dimension: Are You Actually Ready?

The legal risks of dating during the divorce are concrete and manageable — stop when the decree is entered, keep finances separate, keep new partners away from the children. The psychological risks are subtler and longer-lasting.

### The Rebound Relationship

The rebound relationship is not a myth. It reflects a real psychological dynamic: people who exit a significant relationship are vulnerable to forming attachments quickly, intensely, and without the usual level of scrutiny — because the attachment itself meets needs that the divorce has left unmet. Loneliness, the desire to feel desirable, the need to prove that the marriage's failure was not a reflection of your worth — all of these create pressure toward connection that can override the judgment you would otherwise apply.

Rebound relationships often feel unusually significant very quickly. That intensity is partly a product of the emotional conditions under which they form, not purely a reflection of the quality of the connection. People who recognize this are better equipped to assess what they are entering into.

This does not mean that relationships begun soon after divorce cannot become lasting. They can. What it means is that the assessment of whether a relationship is genuinely good for you requires a degree of emotional equilibrium that most people do not have in the acute phase of a divorce.

### Unprocessed Grief and Anger

A person who enters dating primarily from a place of unprocessed grief or active anger toward their ex-spouse tends to bring those states into new relationships in ways that damage them. Grief makes you attached too quickly, too desperately, and too resistant to recognizing incompatibility. Anger makes you compare new partners to your ex-spouse, either favorably (they are not like that, which is enough) or unfavorably (no one measures up to the idealized pre-conflict version of your ex).

Neither of these is a foundation for a functional relationship. The goal is not to eliminate these feelings before dating — they do not resolve on a timeline you control. The goal is to have sufficient awareness of them to prevent them from driving your choices.

### Identity After the Marriage

A long marriage structures identity in ways that are not fully visible until the marriage ends. You knew who you were partly through the relationship: your role, your routines, what you wanted, what you were willing to accept. A divorce strips that structure away, which is disorienting and, eventually, an opportunity.

People who date before they have begun to reconstruct a post-marriage identity often seek in a new partner the structure that the marriage provided — which is not the same as seeking a person whose life and values genuinely complement theirs. The difference matters.

The question worth sitting with before dating is not *am I over the divorce* — most people are not fully over it for years, and waiting for that standard to be met is not realistic. The question is: *do I have enough of a sense of who I am now to engage with another person from a position of genuine self-knowledge rather than dependency?*

## The Social Dimension: Your Children, Your Community, and Your Ex

### Your Children's Readiness

Children process a parent's new relationship differently depending on their age, the circumstances of the divorce, the quality of their relationship with each parent, and how the new relationship is introduced. What is consistent across most research on post-divorce families is that children benefit from:

- Time between the divorce and the introduction of a new partner (most family therapists suggest at minimum six months after the divorce is final, and longer when children are still actively struggling)
- A gradual introduction — not meeting a new partner as a romantic figure immediately
- Reassurance that the new person is not replacing the other parent
- Stability in the primary household before a new relationship is added to it

Children who are introduced to a parent's new partner too soon, or in contexts that feel destabilizing — the partner present at exchanges, moving in before the children have adjusted to the post-divorce structure, the parent's emotional energy clearly redirected toward the new person — show measurable stress responses. This is not about protecting children from the reality that parents are people with romantic lives. It is about sequencing: first stability, then, gradually, expansion.

**Do not introduce a new partner to your children as your girlfriend or boyfriend until the relationship has demonstrated meaningful staying power** — months, not weeks. Even then, the introduction should be low-key and gradual, not an event.

### Your Community

In a high-conflict divorce, your social world is watching. Friends, neighbors, family members, and — in smaller communities — people you do not know well have formed views about what happened and about who each of you is. A new relationship that becomes visible too soon can confirm narratives your ex-spouse has been advancing about you, provide fuel for litigation, and alienate people who might otherwise have been neutral or supportive.

This is not an argument for permanent privacy about your personal life. It is an argument for discretion during the acute period. The community will adjust to your post-divorce reality. It is not necessary to navigate that adjustment at the same time you are navigating the divorce.

### Your Ex-Spouse

In a co-parenting situation, your new relationship eventually becomes part of the shared parenting environment — particularly if the relationship becomes serious. How your ex-spouse reacts to the existence of a new partner is not fully within your control, but how and when that person enters the co-parenting dynamic is.

A new partner who becomes a source of conflict in co-parenting communications, who is named in accusations about parenting environment, or who interacts poorly with the other parent at exchanges creates a problem that is easier to avoid than to resolve. Introduce a new partner into the co-parenting dynamic slowly, deliberately, and only when the relationship has enough substance to warrant the disruption it may create.

## Signs You May Be Ready

None of these are absolutes, and all require honest self-assessment rather than wishful thinking:

- The divorce is final, or if it is not, you have had a candid conversation with your attorney about the legal risks and are making an informed decision
- You can describe what you want in a relationship in terms of who you are now, not in terms of what was missing from your marriage
- You are not primarily seeking connection to escape loneliness or to prove something to yourself or to your ex-spouse
- You have sufficient emotional bandwidth to be genuinely present for another person, not just for what that person provides you
- You have thought through how any new relationship would intersect with your children's lives and have a plan for managing that intersection carefully
- You are not still in regular, intense emotional conflict with your ex-spouse — meaning the active hostility of the contested divorce has sufficiently settled that your attention is not predominantly occupied by it

---

## Signs You Are Probably Not Ready

- You find yourself thinking about how your ex-spouse will react to the news that you are dating
- You are looking for someone who is "nothing like" your ex-spouse, without much further criteria
- You are not sleeping, eating, or functioning consistently at a basic level
- The divorce is still actively contested and custody is unresolved
- Your children are in active crisis — refusal to attend school, behavioral deterioration, expressed distress about the divorce — and have not yet stabilized
- You would describe your primary motivation as not wanting to be alone

---

There is no universal timeline. The question is not how many months since the separation, or whether friends think you have waited long enough, or whether your ex-spouse has already moved on. The question is whether you are entering a new relationship with enough self-knowledge, enough stability, and enough attention available to give it a genuine chance — and whether the circumstances of the divorce allow you to do so without creating legal, parenting, or co-parenting problems that outweigh what you are gaining.

For most people in contested Virginia divorces, the honest answer is to wait until the decree is entered. For many, the honest answer is to wait somewhat longer than that.

The person you will be a year after the divorce is final — someone who has rebuilt a life, reconstructed an identity, and processed the losses of the marriage — is a much better candidate for a healthy new relationship than the person you are in the middle of it. That version of yourself is worth waiting for.
