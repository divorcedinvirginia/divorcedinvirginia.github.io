Most people going through a divorce focus almost entirely on the legal and financial dimensions — the custody schedule, the house, the retirement accounts. The psychological dimension gets whatever is left over, which is usually very little.

This is a mistake. The decisions you make during a divorce are some of the most consequential of your life, and you will be making them under sustained emotional stress, with disrupted sleep, fractured social support, and a constant stream of new crises. Your judgment is compromised at exactly the moment you can least afford it.

Therapy is not a luxury for people in this situation. It is a practical tool. And how you use it — what you bring, what you work on, what you are honest about — determines how much it actually helps.

---

## When to Start

The short answer: earlier than you think you need to.

Most people do not seek therapy until they are in acute crisis — when they can no longer function at work, when they break down in front of their children, or when they do something they regret and realize they are not managing as well as they thought. By that point, they have often already made decisions in an unregulated emotional state that they cannot undo.

### If You Are the One Initiating the Divorce

You may feel like you do not need therapy because you made the decision, you have had time to process it, and you feel — comparatively — in control. But initiating a divorce carries its own psychological weight: guilt, second-guessing, the grief of ending something that once mattered, and the social and relational fallout that follows. People who initiate divorces often underestimate how hard it will be after they file, when the reality of what they have set in motion hits them.

Start therapy before you file if possible. The months leading up to a filing are a time when having a structured space to process your decision — without involving your children, your friends, or your spouse — is genuinely valuable.

### If the Divorce Was Sprung on You

If you were blindsided, therapy should begin in the first week if at all possible. You are in acute psychological distress. You are being asked to make major legal and financial decisions — hiring an attorney, responding to a complaint, potentially navigating a custody crisis — while in a state that resembles trauma. You need support immediately, not in a few months when things have "settled down."

Things do not settle down in contested divorces. The need for psychological support does not diminish as the case proceeds — it shifts.

### During the Active Litigation Phase

Even if you managed the early stages without therapy, the active litigation phase of a contested divorce — discovery, depositions, pendente lite hearings, mediation failures, trial preparation — creates sustained stress that accumulates over time. People who seem to be handling things well in month three are often falling apart by month twelve.

If you are in active contested litigation, you should be in therapy. Full stop.

### After the Decree

Many people experience a counterintuitive collapse after the divorce is finalized. The structure of the litigation — the constant activity, the sense of fighting toward a resolution — disappears, and what remains is a new life that does not yet feel like yours. Post-decree depression and anxiety are common and underrecognized. Therapy should continue through this transition.

## What Kind of Therapist to Look For

You want a licensed mental health professional with experience in life transitions, grief, and — ideally — divorce specifically. In Virginia, look for a Licensed Professional Counselor (LPC), Licensed Clinical Social Worker (LCSW), Licensed Marriage and Family Therapist (LMFT), or Licensed Clinical Psychologist.

### Specific Things to Ask

- Do you have experience working with people going through divorce or high-conflict separation?
- What is your approach to helping clients manage major life transitions?
- Do you have any background in the legal process of divorce, or have you worked with clients in contested custody situations?

The last question matters because a therapist who understands how divorce litigation works — the timeline, the pressures, the specific decision points — can help you navigate those moments far more effectively than one who has no frame of reference for what you are experiencing.

### Individual Therapy, Not Couples Therapy

Once you are separated and heading toward divorce, couples therapy is generally not appropriate unless you are specifically working on an amicable co-parenting relationship with a therapist both of you trust and have chosen together. Couples therapy undertaken during contentious litigation can be counterproductive — information shared in sessions can be distorted later, and the power dynamics of a contentious divorce make genuine therapeutic work difficult.

What you need is individual therapy: your own space, your own therapist, who is entirely on your side.

## What to Actually Bring to Your Sessions

This is where many people miss the most value from therapy during divorce. There is a tendency to use sessions primarily as emotional release — to recount what your spouse did, what their attorney argued, what your mother thinks you should do, and how unfair all of it is. This is understandable. It is also not the most effective use of therapeutic time.

Emotional processing has to happen, and a good therapist will facilitate it. But if every session is primarily a recounting of the week's grievances, you are spending significant money and time on something that venting to a trusted friend could partially substitute for. The unique value of therapy lies elsewhere.

Here is what to actually work on.

### 1. Your Decision-Making Under Stress

One of the most valuable things therapy can do during a divorce is help you become aware of how you make decisions when you are frightened, angry, or exhausted — and help you build enough self-awareness to catch yourself before those states drive choices you will regret.

Bring specific decisions to your therapist. Not just: *"I'm so angry at what my spouse did."* But: *"I'm thinking about sending this email to my spouse. Here is what it says. Help me think through whether this is a good idea."*

Or: *"My attorney wants me to file a motion to compel that I don't fully understand. I feel pressure to say yes because I trust my attorney, but I'm also not sure it's the right move. How do I think this through?"*

Or: *"My mother keeps telling me I should ask for the house even though I know I can't afford the mortgage alone. I'm tempted to dig in anyway because the house is where the kids grew up. What's happening with me here?"*

These conversations — grounded in specific, real decisions you are facing — will serve you far better than a generalized discussion of how hard everything is.

### 2. The Emotional Patterns That Are Getting You Into Trouble

Divorce surfaces patterns that were present long before the marriage ended. People who avoid conflict find themselves unable to communicate necessary information to their attorney. People who over-control become micromanagers of their legal case, alienating the very professionals they need. People who catastrophize cannot distinguish between setbacks that are serious and setbacks that are normal and manageable.

Ask your therapist to help you identify what your specific patterns are under stress — not as a judgment, but as a map. Once you know that you tend to shut down when you feel steamrolled, or that you tend to escalate when you feel ignored, you can begin to recognize those moments before they result in an email you should not have sent or a position you should not have taken.

### 3. Grief — All of It

Divorce involves multiple simultaneous losses: the marriage itself, the vision of the future you had planned, the family structure your children are growing up in, possibly your home, your financial security, your social network (which often fractures along loyalty lines), and sometimes your sense of identity.

These losses deserve direct attention in therapy, not just as background noise behind the legal and logistical discussions. Many people suppress grief during the active litigation phase because they need to stay functional — and then find themselves overwhelmed by it a year or two later when the immediate crisis has passed.

Bring the grief. Not just the anger — the grief underneath the anger. The things you miss. The future you are mourning. A good therapist will help you move through it rather than around it.

### 4. Your Relationship With Your Spouse — What It Was and What It Is Now

It is common to enter divorce with a deeply distorted view of your spouse — either idealizing what the relationship was, in a way that makes the divorce feel unbearable, or demonizing your spouse, in a way that makes you feel justified in decisions that will harm you, your children, or the legal process.

Therapy is a place to develop a more accurate and nuanced understanding of your marriage: what it was, what went wrong, what your role was in that, and who your spouse actually is — not a monster, not the person you fell in love with twenty years ago, but a human being who is also in distress and who will be co-parenting with you for the next decade.

This matters practically. People who have genuinely processed their anger and grief make better settlement decisions, communicate more effectively with the other parent, and are less likely to do things in litigation that harm their children. Courts can often sense when a parent has done this emotional work — and when they have not.

### 5. Identity and the Person You Are Becoming

A long marriage shapes your identity. Who you are as a spouse, as a parent in a two-parent household, as part of a particular social unit — all of that is changing. Therapy should include active attention to who you are outside of the marriage and who you want to be when this is over.

This is not navel-gazing. It is practical. People who have a clear sense of themselves as individuals — with their own values, their own goals, their own community — make better long-term decisions during divorce than people who are entirely defined by the marriage and its dissolution.

Bring questions about the future to your sessions, not just problems from the past week. What kind of life do you want in five years? What kind of co-parent do you want to be? What matters most to you in how this ends? Working backward from clear answers to those questions often clarifies which legal battles are worth fighting and which are not.

### 6. Your Children

If you have children, your mental health directly affects theirs. Your ability to regulate your own emotions in front of them, to avoid putting them in the middle, and to actively support their relationship with the other parent is determined largely by how well you are managing your own distress.

Bring your children to your therapy sessions in the sense of bringing your parenting — not the logistics, but the emotional and relational dimensions. Are you present with them, or are you too consumed by the case? Are you saying things to them you later regret? Are you using them for emotional support in ways that are not appropriate? Are you struggling to hold your own grief together when they are with you?

A therapist can help you become a more regulated, more present parent during the period when your children need you most.

### 7. The Things You Are Ashamed Of

Divorce brings out behavior in people that they are not proud of — things said in anger, decisions made out of spite, moments where they prioritized winning over their children's wellbeing. These are often the things people most avoid bringing to therapy, because they are the most uncomfortable.

They are also the most important to examine.

Shame that is not addressed tends to drive behavior. People who are deeply ashamed of something they did early in the divorce sometimes double down on aggressive litigation as a way of proving they are the wronged party. People who did something that hurt their children sometimes overcompensate in ways that create new problems. Examining these things honestly in therapy — with a professional who will not judge you, but will help you understand what drove the behavior and what to do differently — is one of the most valuable things you can do.

## What Not to Use Your Therapist For

### A Validator for Your Legal Positions

Your therapist is not a legal expert and is not in a position to tell you whether your custody position is reasonable, whether your financial demands are justified, or whether your attorney's strategy is sound. Using therapy as a place to get emotional validation for legal positions that may or may not be supported by the law is counterproductive.

Bring your feelings about the legal process to therapy. Bring your decisions to your attorney.

### A Substitute for Your Attorney

Therapy is not a place to strategize about discovery, draft responses to motions, or work through the financial dimensions of an equitable distribution offer. If you are spending session time on logistics that belong in a conversation with your lawyer, you are paying twice for the same work — and getting it done by the wrong professional.

### A Place to Rehearse the Narrative of Your Case

If you find yourself going into therapy sessions with the goal of building a coherent story of who did what to whom, who is the victim, and who is to blame — you are using therapy as case preparation, not as healing. This is not therapy. It is rumination with an audience, and it will leave you less able to move forward, not more.

A good therapist will notice this pattern and redirect you. A less experienced one may not.

## A Note on Cost and Access

Good individual therapy in Virginia typically costs $100–$200 per session out of pocket, or significantly less with insurance. Many employers offer Employee Assistance Programs (EAP) that provide four to twelve sessions at no cost — use them if you have them, particularly in the early acute phase.

If cost is a barrier, options include:
- Community mental health centers, which offer sliding-scale fees based on income
- Training clinics at Virginia universities, which provide lower-cost therapy with supervised graduate students
- Open Path Collective (openpathcollective.org), a network of therapists offering reduced-rate sessions for people in financial hardship
- Telehealth platforms, which typically offer lower rates than in-person therapy

The cost of therapy during a divorce is almost always less than the cost of a single legal motion driven by an unregulated emotional reaction. Frame it that way when you are deciding whether you can afford it.

---

You will not come out of a contested divorce the same person who went in. The question is whether that change is one you have some hand in shaping, or one that simply happens to you. Therapy is how you get some agency over that — not by avoiding the pain, but by moving through it with more awareness, more intention, and more support than you would have alone.
