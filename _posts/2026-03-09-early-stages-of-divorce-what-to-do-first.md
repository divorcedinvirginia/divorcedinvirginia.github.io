The period immediately after deciding to divorce is critical. Decisions made in the first days and weeks can affect everything from the division of your finances to the outcome of a custody case. Whether your divorce is likely to be straightforward or deeply contested, the right early actions protect you and put you in the strongest possible position.

## Step 1: Establish Your Date of Separation

Virginia's divorce clock does not start until you and your spouse are living separately **with the intent that the separation is permanent**. Document this date clearly.

- If possible, send an email or text on the day you separate stating your intent. This creates a timestamped record.
- If you remain in the same home (a "separation under the same roof"), document separate sleeping arrangements, separate finances, and the absence of a marital relationship.
- Avoid reconciling. Even a brief resumption of the marital relationship can reset the clock.

The minimum separation period in Virginia is **six months** (if you have no minor children and a signed separation agreement) or **one year** (all other situations). The clock only runs if the separation is continuous.

## Step 2: Gather Financial Documents

Before any accounts are frozen, assets are moved, or documents become difficult to access, collect copies of the following:

- **Tax returns** — at least the last three years
- **Bank and investment account statements** — at least 12 months of history
- **Retirement account statements** — 401(k), IRA, pension benefit summaries
- **Mortgage statements and property deeds**
- **Vehicle titles**
- **Credit card and loan statements** — including any accounts you may not have known about
- **Pay stubs** for both spouses, if accessible
- **Business records** if either spouse owns a business
- **Life insurance policies** — note beneficiary designations

Store copies somewhere your spouse cannot access — a secure cloud account, a trusted family member's home, or a safe deposit box in your name only.

## Step 3: Open Accounts in Your Name Only

If you do not already have individual bank and credit accounts, open them now.

- Move a reasonable share of joint liquid funds to your individual account. What is "reasonable" depends on your circumstances, but courts generally frown on one spouse draining marital accounts. Taking half of a joint checking account for living expenses is generally acceptable; emptying investment accounts is not.
- Apply for a credit card in your name only to establish or rebuild individual credit.
- Do **not** close joint accounts unilaterally — this can look like financial misconduct to a court and may leave your spouse without access to funds needed for living expenses.

## Step 4: Stop Sharing Digital Accounts and Devices

Change passwords to your personal email, social media, and cloud storage accounts. Remove shared device access where possible.

Virginia courts can and do admit text messages, emails, and social media posts as evidence. Anything you send or post from this point forward may end up in front of a judge.

- Assume every text message is discoverable.
- Do not post about the divorce, your spouse, dating, finances, or anything emotionally charged on social media.
- Do not access your spouse's accounts, email, or devices — this can constitute illegal interception under federal and state wiretapping laws.

## Step 5: Consult an Attorney Early

Even if you plan to handle the divorce yourself or expect it to be uncontested, an initial consultation with a Virginia family law attorney is worthwhile. Most attorneys offer paid consultations for $150–$300.

An attorney can help you:
- Understand your rights to the marital home, retirement accounts, and other assets
- Assess whether your case is likely to be contested
- Identify any time-sensitive steps specific to your situation
- Review a separation agreement before you sign it

## If Your Divorce Is Likely Uncontested

An uncontested divorce means you and your spouse agree — or expect to agree — on all issues: property division, debt allocation, spousal support, and (if applicable) custody and child support.

### Prioritize the Separation Agreement

A written separation agreement is the backbone of an uncontested divorce in Virginia. It is a binding contract that resolves all marital issues before the court is ever involved. A well-drafted agreement allows your divorce to proceed quickly, often without either party appearing in court.

**Early actions for an uncontested case:**

- **Have an honest financial disclosure conversation.** Both spouses should fully disclose assets and debts. A court can void an agreement later if it was based on concealment.
- **Identify your priorities and your spouse's priorities.** Trades are easier when you know what each side values most.
- **Consider mediation.** A neutral mediator can help you reach agreement on terms before attorneys draft the paperwork, often at a fraction of the cost of litigation.
- **Hire an attorney to draft or review the agreement** — even in an amicable divorce. The terms of a separation agreement are difficult to modify once signed and incorporated into a court order.
- **Do not rush signing.** An agreement signed under pressure, without understanding its full implications, can cause lasting financial harm.

### Typical Uncontested Timeline

Once you have a signed separation agreement and have met the separation period requirement, an uncontested Virginia divorce can be finalized in as little as **four to eight weeks** through a court affidavit process — no hearing required in most cases.

## If Your Divorce Is Likely Contested

A contested divorce means you and your spouse disagree on one or more significant issues. These cases require more preparation and a different mindset from the start.

### Hire an Attorney — Do Not Wait

In a contested case, the decisions your spouse makes (and the moves their attorney makes) in the early weeks can affect your case for years. Contested divorces involve discovery, depositions, expert witnesses, and sometimes dirty tactics. You need experienced representation.

**When selecting an attorney for a contested case:**
- Look for someone with courtroom experience in Virginia family law, not just a general practitioner.
- Ask specifically about their experience with cases involving your key issues — high-asset division, custody disputes, business valuation, or military retirement, as applicable.
- Review the [Virginia State Bar's attorney search tool](https://www.vsb.org/site/members/search) to verify licensing and check for disciplinary history.

### Document Everything From Day One

In a contested case, the record you build in the early stages matters.

- **Keep a written log** of significant events: dates your spouse has the children, incidents of conflict, threats, or any behavior relevant to fault grounds or custody.
- **Save communications.** Do not delete text threads, voicemails, or emails from your spouse.
- **Photograph the condition of the marital home** and any valuable personal property.
- If domestic violence is a factor, contact an attorney immediately. A protective order can be obtained quickly and affects custody arrangements.

### Understand Pendente Lite Relief

If you need financial support, access to the marital home, or a temporary custody arrangement while the divorce is pending, you can file a motion for **pendente lite** ("during litigation") relief. Virginia courts can order:

- Temporary spousal support
- Temporary child support
- Temporary exclusive use of the marital home
- Temporary custody and visitation schedules

Filing early for pendente lite relief can establish a temporary status quo that often persists throughout the case. Courts are reluctant to disrupt arrangements that have been in place and are working.

### Avoid Self-Inflicted Damage

Contested divorces generate evidence. Courts see the full picture of how both spouses behaved during the process.

**Things that hurt your case:**
- Moving out of the marital home without a plan (it can weaken your claim to the home and, in custody cases, the status quo)
- Introducing a new romantic partner to your children before the divorce is final
- Making unilateral financial decisions — selling assets, running up debt, or canceling insurance
- Communicating through your children
- Violating any court order, however temporary

## Both Contested and Uncontested: Universal Early Steps

Regardless of how you expect the divorce to proceed, these actions apply to everyone:

| Action | Why It Matters |
|---|---|
| Update your will and healthcare directive | Your spouse may currently control medical decisions and inherit everything by default |
| Update beneficiary designations | Life insurance and retirement accounts pass outside a will — update them after separation |
| Notify your employer's HR department | Coordinate health insurance coverage before any changes take effect |
| Keep paying joint obligations | Missing a mortgage or car payment during divorce harms your credit regardless of fault |
| Avoid major purchases | Large new debts incurred after separation can still be treated as marital debt |

---

The early choices you make set the tone for everything that follows. Taking deliberate, informed steps — rather than reactive ones — gives you the best chance of reaching a fair resolution as efficiently as possible.
