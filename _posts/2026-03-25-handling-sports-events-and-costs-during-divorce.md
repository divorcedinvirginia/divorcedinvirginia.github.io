For many families, youth sports are the center of the weekly calendar — practices twice a week, games on Saturdays, tournaments that consume entire weekends. During a divorce, all of that continues. The registration fees still come due. The equipment still needs replacing. The Saturday game still happens whether it falls on your parenting time or the other parent's.

How you handle sports during a divorce tests your co-parenting capacity in a very specific, very visible way. The sideline at your child's soccer game is one of the most observed social environments a divorcing parent will encounter. It is also where some of the most damaging parental behavior in custody cases occurs — and some of the most meaningful moments of genuine co-parenting.

---

## Sports and Your Custody Order

Before anything else, understand how your custody arrangement — whether formal or informal — interacts with your child's sports schedule.

### Attendance Rights

Both parents generally have the right to attend their child's sporting events, regardless of whose parenting time it falls on. A parent's right to watch their child play is not the same as parenting time — it is a separate and recognized interest that Virginia courts support. A custody order that restricts one parent from attending events open to the public is unusual and would require specific justification.

This means that even if a Saturday game falls entirely within the other parent's parenting time, you are typically entitled to attend. What you are not entitled to do is use your attendance as an opportunity to extend your physical time with the child, interfere with the other parent's transportation and care responsibilities, or create conflict that disrupts the child's experience.

Show up. Cheer. Leave when it is over. That is the framework.

### Transportation and Schedule Coordination

If practices and games fall on both parents' time, a clear transportation plan is essential. Without one, logistics become a source of conflict that children absorb directly.

Work out through your attorneys or in your co-parenting app:

- Who is responsible for transporting the child to practices and games that fall during their parenting time
- What happens when a game falls near the beginning or end of a parenting time transition (does the dropping-off parent take the child to the game first, or does the receiving parent pick them up there?)
- How schedule changes or cancellations are communicated
- What happens with tournaments that span multiple days across a parenting time boundary

These logistics are not exciting to negotiate, but an agreement that covers them prevents the Saturday game from becoming a flashpoint every week.

### Activities Initiated During the Marriage vs. After

If your child was already playing a sport when the divorce began, courts generally expect both parents to support continuity in that activity. Unilaterally withdrawing a child from an established sport during a divorce — particularly one the child loves — can be viewed as disrupting the child's stability and is likely to be raised in custody proceedings.

If a new activity is proposed after separation, both parents with joint legal custody should agree before enrollment. Signing a child up for a new sport that requires significant time and financial commitment during the other parent's parenting time, without their knowledge or consent, is a co-parenting breach that creates unnecessary conflict.

## Handling the Sideline

The sports sideline is a specific social environment with its own dynamics, and it requires its own approach during a divorce.

### Where to Stand or Sit

The same logic from school events applies here, with one additional consideration: sporting events often involve sustained periods of time — an hour-long soccer match, a three-hour baseball game — rather than the brief seated experience of a school concert. The sustained nature of sports events makes it more important to establish your positioning deliberately from the moment you arrive.

If you and your spouse can be near each other without visible tension, standing or sitting in the same general area is typically better for the children — it signals normalcy and avoids the conspicuousness of two parents standing at opposite ends of the field while their child plays in the middle.

If the relationship is contentious, establish different but equally good viewing positions. The other side of the field is not a punishment or a concession — it is a practical arrangement that prevents forced proximity from producing conflict. Your child should be able to see both of you when they look over.

**What to avoid:** The parent who positions themselves close enough to the other parent to create ambient tension without technically doing anything confrontational — hovering near enough to be felt, making comments that only the other parent can hear — is engaging in a subtle form of provocation that other parents and coaches notice.

### Behavior on the Sideline

Children who are playing sports are deeply aware of their parents on the sideline. They hear what you say. They notice when you are tense. They feel the difference between a parent who is present and enthusiastic about the game and a parent who is distracted by a conflict with the other parent.

**What helps your child perform:**
- Enthusiastic, specific encouragement about the game (*"Great pass!"* not *"Show your dad how good you are"*)
- Calm, consistent presence
- Cheering for all the children on the team, not just yours

**What hurts your child's performance and experience:**
- Visible hostility between parents on the sideline
- Coaching instructions that contradict the actual coach (this is disruptive regardless of divorce)
- Loud arguments, even whispered ones with suppressed energy that children sense
- Using halftime or post-game time to have difficult conversations with your spouse
- Leaving early in a way your child will notice and wonder about

### Coaches and Team Parents

Tell the coach and team manager early that you are going through a separation. You do not need to provide details. A brief message is sufficient:

*"I wanted to give you a heads up that [child's name]'s parents are separating. Both of us will continue to attend games and are committed to [child's] participation in the sport. If there are any logistics or communication questions, please copy both of us on any team emails."*

This accomplishes several things: the coach knows not to deliver messages through one parent to the other, both parents continue to receive all team communications directly, and the coach is aware of the context if either parent behaves unusually on the sideline.

Most coaches have navigated this situation before and are quietly relieved to have it acknowledged so they do not have to manage an unexpected conflict.

**Team group chats and parent communication:** If the team communicates through a group chat or app, both parents should be included if possible. A child whose divorced parent is excluded from team communications because the other parent controls that communication channel will eventually notice — and it will be raised in the custody case.

### When Your Spouse's New Partner Is on the Sideline

This is one of the most emotionally charged situations in youth sports during a divorce, and it is increasingly common. A new partner at your child's game — particularly if the relationship began during or close to the marriage — triggers a specific set of feelings that are genuinely difficult to manage.

What you are not going to do: react visibly, make comments to other parents, position yourself near the new partner to signal your objection, communicate your discomfort to your child, or leave the game in protest.

What you are going to do: manage your reaction privately, stay for your child, and behave exactly as you would if the person were not there. Your child deserves to have both their parents on the sideline. The presence of a third person does not change that.

Over time, if the new partner is a consistent presence in your child's life, your child's relationship with that person will be their own — influenced by their direct experience, not by your reaction on a Saturday morning.

## Sharing the Costs of Youth Sports

This is where some of the most persistent and draining conflict in co-parenting occurs. Sports are expensive — registration, equipment, uniforms, tournaments, travel, training — and the costs arrive on their own schedule, sometimes without much notice.

### What Virginia Courts Expect

Virginia's child support guidelines account for "reasonable work-related childcare costs" and can include certain child-related expenses, but youth sports costs typically fall into the category of "unreimbursed expenses" or "extracurricular activity costs" that are addressed separately from the base child support calculation.

Courts expect parents to discuss and agree on extracurricular activity costs, particularly for activities the child was already enrolled in before the divorce. A court can order specific cost-sharing arrangements for extracurricular activities, and many separation agreements and custody orders include provisions for this.

The general principle courts apply: activities that were established during the marriage should continue, and the costs should be shared proportionally to income. Activities proposed after separation require agreement between the parents with joint legal custody before enrollment and cost-sharing can be obligated.

### Types of Sports Costs and How to Handle Each

**Registration and league fees:** These are typically annual or seasonal and known well in advance. They should be addressed explicitly in your separation agreement or co-parenting plan. Common arrangements:

- Split 50/50 regardless of income difference
- Split proportionally to each parent's gross income (e.g., 60/40 if one parent earns significantly more)
- One parent pays registration and the other covers equipment, or vice versa

Whichever arrangement you use, establish it in writing before the fees come due — not in a text exchange the week registration closes.

**Equipment:** Equipment costs can range from minor (a sports bag, shin guards) to significant (hockey gear, baseball equipment, competitive-level gear for advanced programs). The same income-based or 50/50 split logic applies, but equipment raises an additional question: who keeps the equipment, and what happens if it is not returned after the child visits the other household?

Establish which household keeps which equipment. For items the child needs at both homes (cleats, protective gear), decide whether duplicates will be purchased or whether the equipment travels with the child. Do not use equipment as a leverage point — withholding a child's cleats because you are angry with the other parent affects only the child.

**Uniforms:** Uniforms are generally purchased once per season and stay with the child. These should be included in the cost-sharing calculation.

**Tournament and travel costs:** Tournaments that require travel — hotel stays, entry fees, meals on the road — can be expensive and often come up on short notice. Address these proactively:

- Who is responsible for costs when the tournament falls on whose parenting time?
- Are both parents expected to attend out-of-town tournaments, or only the parent on whose time the tournament falls?
- How are hotel and travel costs shared if both parents attend?
- What is the process for deciding whether the child attends an optional tournament?

**Private coaching and training:** If your child receives private coaching or participates in elite training programs, these costs can be substantial. Both parents should agree before any such program is initiated during the divorce. A parent who unilaterally enrolls a child in expensive private coaching and then demands the other parent pay half has created an unnecessary conflict and is unlikely to receive full cooperation from the court or the other parent.

**Fundraising obligations:** Many youth sports programs require families to participate in fundraising — selling items, working volunteer hours, or paying a buy-out fee. Establish who handles fundraising obligations and how costs are split.

### Creating a System That Works

The biggest source of sports-cost conflict is not the amounts — it is the unpredictability and the informality. A parent who receives an unexpected request for $300 for a tournament three weeks out, via a terse text message, is set up to respond negatively regardless of the merits.

**Establish a shared expense tracking system.** Co-parenting apps like OurFamilyWizard have built-in expense tracking and reimbursement request features that create a clear, timestamped record of what was requested, when, and what was paid. Using a structured system removes the emotional charge from individual expense requests and creates accountability on both sides.

**Build a shared sports fund.** Some co-parenting pairs establish a joint account specifically for the children's activities — both parents contribute a set amount monthly, and activity costs are paid from that account. This removes the friction of individual reimbursement requests entirely. It requires a functional level of cooperation, but when it works, it is significantly lower-conflict than the alternative.

**Document all payments you make.** Keep receipts, bank records, and screenshots of payment confirmation for every sports-related expense you pay. If the cost-sharing arrangement ever becomes a legal dispute, you need a complete and accurate record of what you have paid and what has not been reimbursed.

**Address major expenses before they arise, not after.** When you know that a new season is approaching, that the equipment needs replacing, or that a major tournament is scheduled, raise the cost-sharing question through your co-parenting app or through attorneys before the expense is incurred. Retroactive requests for reimbursement for expenses the other parent did not know about in advance are the ones most likely to be refused and litigated.

### When the Other Parent Refuses to Pay Their Share

If cost-sharing is established in a court order or separation agreement and the other parent refuses to pay their portion, the remedy is a motion for contempt. Document what was owed, when it was due, and that it was not paid. Your attorney can seek reimbursement and attorney's fees through the contempt process.

If cost-sharing is not yet formalized and the other parent simply refuses to contribute, document their refusal — the dates, the amounts, what you requested, and what response you received. This becomes evidence in a motion to establish formal cost-sharing arrangements.

Do not unilaterally withdraw the child from sports because the other parent is not contributing. Courts view the withdrawal of an established activity — particularly one the child loves — as harmful to the child, and will not reward the parent who made that decision unilaterally. Pay what you need to pay to keep the child in the activity, document what you paid, and pursue reimbursement through proper channels.

## The Larger Point

Youth sports, at their best, are one of the most powerful positive forces in a child's life during a divorce. They provide structure, peer connection, physical activity, a sense of competence and achievement, and a predictable schedule in a period when everything else feels uncertain.

The parents who understand this — who show up on the sideline, who split the registration fee without a fight, who make sure the cleats get to the right house on Friday afternoon — give their children something invaluable: a part of their life that the divorce has not managed to damage.

The parents who use sports as a venue for conflict, who withhold equipment as leverage, who make the Saturday game about themselves instead of their child — they take something that could have been a refuge and turn it into another source of stress.

The choice is made one Saturday morning at a time.
