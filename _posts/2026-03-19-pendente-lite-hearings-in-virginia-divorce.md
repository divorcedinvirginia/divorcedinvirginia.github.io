The words *pendente lite* are Latin for "while the suit is pending." A pendente lite (PL) hearing is a court proceeding held before the divorce is finalized — often months or years before trial — to establish temporary orders governing the most immediate and pressing aspects of your situation: where the children live, who stays in the house, and how the bills get paid.

What happens at a PL hearing can define your family's daily reality for the entire duration of the divorce. In custody cases, the PL order often establishes the status quo that the court is reluctant to disturb at trial. In financial cases, it can determine whether one spouse has enough money to retain adequate legal representation. These are not preliminary skirmishes — they are consequential proceedings that deserve serious preparation.

---

## The Legal Authority for Pendente Lite Relief

Virginia Code § 20-103 gives Virginia courts broad authority to enter temporary orders while a divorce case is pending, including orders related to:

- Spousal support (temporary maintenance)
- Child custody and visitation
- Child support
- Exclusive use and possession of the marital home
- Use of vehicles and other personal property
- Payment of specific debts and household expenses
- Attorney's fees and litigation costs
- Injunctions restraining either party from dissipating, concealing, or transferring marital assets

A PL order is temporary by definition — it governs the situation during the pendency of the divorce and is superseded by the final decree. However, "temporary" in a contested divorce can mean twelve, eighteen, or twenty-four months or more. The practical weight of a PL order is substantial.

## What You Can Ask For

### Temporary Spousal Support

One of the most common PL requests, particularly in marriages where there is a significant income disparity. A spouse who lacks sufficient income to pay their own living expenses while the divorce is pending can ask the court to order temporary support from the higher-earning spouse.

Virginia courts determine temporary spousal support using the same general framework as final support — the needs of the requesting party, the ability of the paying party to pay, and the marital standard of living. In practice, many judges in Virginia follow what is often called the Fairfax formula as a starting point: approximately 28% of the payor's gross income minus 58% of the recipient's gross income. This is not a statutory formula — it is a commonly applied benchmark, and courts adjust it based on the specific circumstances.

Temporary spousal support is often higher than the final award, because temporary support is intended to maintain the marital standard of living during litigation, while final support is calibrated to long-term needs and earning capacity.

### Temporary Child Support

If child support is disputed, the court can enter a temporary order using Virginia's child support guidelines — the income shares model — based on the parties' current gross incomes and the temporary custody arrangement. If the final custody arrangement is still being litigated, the temporary custody schedule established at the PL hearing directly affects the temporary support calculation.

### Temporary Custody and Visitation

This is often the most contested element of a PL hearing. Each party can request temporary primary custody, a specific parenting schedule, or restrictions on the other parent's time with the children. The court applies the same best-interests-of-the-child framework used at trial, but with limited evidence and limited time.

The custody arrangement established at the PL stage carries significant weight. Courts are reluctant to disrupt arrangements that appear to be working, particularly where children have adjusted to a routine. A party who secures primary physical custody in a PL order — even on a temporary basis — holds a meaningful advantage heading into trial.

### Exclusive Use and Possession of the Marital Home

Either party can request that the court award them exclusive use of the marital home during the pendency of the divorce, requiring the other spouse to vacate. Courts do not take this lightly — ordering a person out of their own home is a significant step — and typically require a showing of domestic violence, harassment, or specific circumstances that make shared occupancy untenable.

If no such circumstances exist, courts may instead order that both parties remain in the home (in separate areas if possible), or leave the housing situation to the parties to resolve informally while the case proceeds.

### Use of Vehicles and Personal Property

Either party can request temporary use of specific vehicles or access to personal property. Courts generally try to ensure each party has reliable transportation and access to the belongings they need for daily life.

### Payment of Debts and Expenses

The court can order either party to pay specific ongoing expenses during the case: mortgage payments, utilities, insurance premiums, car payments. This is particularly important when the higher-earning spouse has vacated the home and the lower-earning spouse cannot afford to maintain it alone. Courts do not want the marital home to go into foreclosure or for children to lose health insurance while litigation proceeds.

### Attorney's Fees

Under Virginia Code § 20-103, courts can award temporary attorney's fees to a party who lacks the financial resources to retain adequate legal representation. This is important in marriages where one spouse controls the finances and the other would otherwise be unable to hire and maintain counsel.

A temporary attorney's fee award is not a gift — it is often repayable from the requesting spouse's share of the marital estate at final resolution. But it ensures that both parties can participate meaningfully in the litigation rather than the better-resourced spouse having an overwhelming advantage.

### Injunctions Against Asset Dissipation

Courts can restrain either party from selling, transferring, encumbering, concealing, or dissipating marital assets while the divorce is pending. These injunctions are particularly important in cases where one spouse controls marital funds and there is concern about money being moved before it can be divided.

## The Process Before the Actual Hearing

Most people assume a PL hearing means filing a motion and appearing before a judge. In many Virginia jurisdictions, there are one or more steps before the matter reaches that point.

### Filing the Motion

A PL motion is filed with the circuit court by either party's attorney, along with supporting documents: a financial statement (required in most jurisdictions), affidavits, and any exhibits the party intends to rely on. The motion identifies what relief is being requested and the factual basis for it.

Once filed, the motion must be served on the other party, who has the opportunity to file a response and cross-motion for their own requested relief.

### The Financial Statement

Virginia courts require both parties to file a detailed financial statement in connection with PL proceedings — a sworn document listing monthly income, monthly expenses, assets, debts, and information about the children. The financial statement is one of the most important documents in the entire PL process.

Prepare your financial statement carefully. It is signed under oath, and inaccuracies — whether overstating needs or understating income — can seriously damage your credibility with the court. Bring documentation: pay stubs, bank statements, bills, and records that support the numbers you are reporting.

### The Role of Conciliators

In many Virginia jurisdictions, particularly in cases involving custody, the court does not immediately schedule a full PL hearing before a judge. Instead, the case is first referred to a **conciliator** — typically a court-affiliated family law practitioner, social worker, or domestic relations counselor — for a conciliation conference.

The conciliation process is an attempt to reach agreement on temporary arrangements before the matter is fully litigated. A conciliator meets with both parties (sometimes together, sometimes separately) and tries to facilitate agreement on the immediate issues. The process is generally more informal than a courtroom hearing and can move more quickly, since it is not constrained by the court's docket.

**What happens in a conciliation conference:**

- Both parties typically attend without their attorneys present, or attorneys may attend in a limited advisory capacity depending on the jurisdiction's rules
- The conciliator reviews the financial statements and any submitted documentation
- The conciliator discusses the parties' positions on each disputed issue
- If agreement is reached on all or some issues, the conciliator drafts a consent order reflecting those agreements, which the parties sign and the court enters
- If agreement is not reached, the conciliator may note areas of agreement and disagreement and refer the matter to a judge or commissioner for a full hearing

**The importance of conciliation:** Even in a contested case, agreements reached in conciliation can save significant time and attorney's fees by resolving some issues without full litigation. A party who approaches conciliation in bad faith — refusing reasonable proposals, using the conference to gather information rather than negotiate, or coming in with maximalist positions they have no intention of moving from — may find themselves before a judge within days and at a disadvantage in terms of how the court perceives their willingness to cooperate.

### Scheduling the Hearing

If conciliation does not resolve the matter, the case proceeds to a hearing. Depending on the jurisdiction and the complexity of the issues, this hearing may be:

- Before a circuit court **judge** — the most common path in jurisdictions without a dedicated domestic relations commissioner
- Before a **Commissioner in Chancery** — a court-appointed attorney authorized to hear evidence, take testimony, and make recommendations to the circuit court judge; used in some Virginia jurisdictions for domestic relations matters
- In some jurisdictions, before a **General District Court judge** — for matters like child support that can be initially addressed at the district court level before going to circuit court

PL hearings are typically scheduled within a few weeks to a few months of the motion being filed, though the specific timeline varies considerably by jurisdiction, judicial availability, and docket pressure. Some urban Virginia circuits have PL hearing slots that are booked out months in advance.

### Exchanges Before the Hearing

Before the hearing, both parties typically exchange the documents they intend to rely on and the evidence they plan to present. Depending on local rules, this may include:

- Updated financial statements reflecting current income and expenses
- Pay stubs or business income records
- Tax returns
- Proposed custody schedules, if custody is at issue
- Evidence relevant to any specific claims — text messages, emails, police reports, or other documentation

Your attorney will also prepare you for the hearing itself: what you will be asked on direct examination, what the opposing attorney is likely to raise on cross-examination, and how to present your evidence clearly and credibly in a compressed timeframe.

## How Courts Generally Judge PL Requests

### The Evidentiary Constraints

The most important thing to understand about PL hearings is that they are not trials. A typical PL hearing lasts one to three hours. The rules of evidence apply, but the parties have limited time to present their case. Witnesses may be limited. Expert testimony is rarely used. Documentary evidence must be organized and efficient.

Courts are making temporary decisions with limited information and limited time. This means they are making judgment calls based on what the evidence suggests is most likely true — not on the same thorough factual record a trial would produce.

### For Financial Requests: Needs and Ability to Pay

On temporary support questions, the court is looking at two things: the requesting party's demonstrated need and the other party's demonstrated ability to pay. Both parties' financial statements are central.

Courts at the PL stage are skeptical of financial statements that seem designed to inflate need or minimize income. A spouse who claims monthly expenses far in excess of what the marital standard of living would support, or who reports income significantly lower than what prior tax returns would suggest, will lose credibility with the court — and that credibility loss can carry into subsequent proceedings.

The court is not trying to achieve perfect accuracy at the PL stage. It is trying to establish a workable temporary arrangement that prevents financial disaster on either side while the case proceeds to full resolution.

### For Custody Requests: Status Quo and Best Interests

On temporary custody, courts in Virginia give heavy weight to the status quo — the arrangement that existed immediately before the separation. A parent who was the primary caregiver before the separation, who has the children in the school they are already attending, and who can demonstrate a stable routine has a significant advantage in a PL custody hearing.

Courts also look at:
- Which parent has been exercising the parenting functions since separation
- The children's adjustment to their current living situation
- Any safety concerns, including domestic violence or substance use
- The feasibility of each proposed parenting schedule given the parties' work schedules, geographic proximity, and the children's extracurricular activities
- The children's stated preferences, to the extent they are old enough to express them meaningfully

At the PL stage, courts generally prefer arrangements that maintain as much stability as possible for the children while the case proceeds. Radical departures from the established pattern require a compelling justification.

### For Exclusive Use of the Home

Courts are reluctant to remove a party from their home without substantial cause. A showing of domestic violence, threats, harassment, or behavior that makes shared occupancy genuinely untenable — supported by police reports, protective orders, photographs, or witness testimony — is typically required.

A party who simply finds it uncomfortable or emotionally difficult to share a home with their spouse will not obtain exclusive use on that basis alone. Courts know that contested divorces are uncomfortable and that both parties are in distress. The standard is a specific, documented need — not general incompatibility.

## When Both Parties File Competing PL Motions

It is common in contested divorces for both parties to file their own PL motions — each asking for things the other opposes, and sometimes asking for directly contradictory relief. One party asks for primary custody; the other asks for primary custody. One party asks for exclusive use of the home; the other does too. One party asks for spousal support; the other asks for the same.

When competing motions are filed, the court hears them together. Both attorneys present their client's position on each issue, and the court resolves all of them in a single order.

**Strategic considerations when competing motions are filed:**

**Lead with your strongest evidence.** In a compressed hearing, the evidence that is most clear, most credible, and most directly relevant to the standard the court applies wins. Do not bury the court in documents — identify the two or three most compelling pieces of evidence for each issue and make sure they are presented clearly.

**Anticipate what the other side will argue.** Your attorney should know, based on the other party's filing and financial statement, what their theory of the case is and what evidence they intend to rely on. Prepare to address it — not defensively, but by putting your own evidence in context.

**Do not overreach.** Asking for relief you cannot support with the available evidence damages your credibility on the things you are legitimately entitled to. Judges notice when a party comes in with maximalist demands and minimal evidence. A more measured, well-supported request is generally more persuasive than an aggressive one.

**Be prepared for the court to split the difference.** On contested issues where the evidence is genuinely in conflict, courts often reach a result that neither party fully wanted — a 50/50 custody schedule when one party asked for primary and the other asked for primary, or a support amount between the two parties' proposals. This is not failure; it is a realistic outcome of adversarial proceedings with limited information.

**The first order sets the baseline.** Whatever the court orders at the PL stage becomes the starting point for negotiations and for trial. Both sides will be living under that order for the duration of the case. If the order is unfavorable, the pressure to litigate aggressively toward a better outcome at trial increases. If it is reasonable, settlement becomes more likely. Coming into the PL hearing prepared — with complete financial documentation, clear testimony, and well-organized evidence — is one of the most important investments you can make in the entire case.

## After the PL Order Is Entered

A PL order is not permanent, but it is enforceable as soon as it is entered. Violations — failing to pay support, refusing to follow the custody schedule, removing assets in violation of an injunction — can be addressed through contempt proceedings.

Either party can file a motion to modify the PL order if circumstances change materially — a significant change in income, a relocation, a safety concern — but courts set a meaningful threshold for modification. The PL order is intended to provide stability during litigation, not to be relitigated every time one party is dissatisfied.

At trial, the PL order does not bind the court. The final decree can award custody, support, and property division that differs substantially from the temporary arrangement. But practically speaking, arrangements that have been stable and working for twelve to eighteen months carry significant momentum into trial — which is why getting the PL order right matters so much.

---

The pendente lite hearing is often the most important proceeding in the first year of a contested Virginia divorce. Treat it accordingly.
