The first two weeks after separation are volatile. Emotions are raw, the legal situation is unclear, and both spouses are often saying things they will later regret — sometimes in writing, in front of witnesses, or in formats that can be printed and handed to a judge.

This is not the time to communicate freely. It is the time to communicate carefully.

---

## The Fundamental Shift You Need to Make

Before separation, your spouse was your partner. You communicated casually, shared information freely, and trusted that what was said privately stayed private.

That dynamic has changed entirely.

Your spouse is now an adverse party in a legal proceeding — or will be soon. Their attorney will review your communications. Anything you write, text, email, or say in front of witnesses can be introduced as evidence. The legal system does not distinguish between a message sent in grief at 2 a.m. and a considered legal position. Both can end up in front of a judge.

This does not mean you should become cold or hostile. It means every communication with your spouse should be filtered through a simple question: **Would I be comfortable if a judge read this?**

---

## What You Should Do

### Communicate in Writing Whenever Possible

Written communication — text messages and email — creates a record that protects both of you. It prevents disputes over "who said what," gives you time to think before responding, and produces documentation that can support your case if your spouse later misrepresents what was agreed.

**Email is preferable to text for anything significant.** It is easier to organize, search, and produce in discovery. It also tends to produce more measured communication than texting, which feels immediate and often triggers reactive responses.

For urgent, time-sensitive logistics (school pickup, medical appointments), texting is fine. For anything involving money, property, children's arrangements, or decisions about the divorce itself, use email.

### Keep Communications Child-Focused and Business-Like

If you have children, most of your communication with your spouse in the early weeks will — and should — be about them. Treat these conversations the way you would treat a business communication with a colleague you do not particularly like:

- Stick to the topic
- Be clear and specific
- Confirm agreements in writing
- Do not editorialize, criticize, or relitigate past grievances

A message like *"Can you confirm that you'll have the kids back by 6 p.m. Sunday?"* is appropriate. *"Can you confirm that you'll have the kids back by 6 p.m. Sunday, unlike last week when you showed up two hours late and upset everyone"* creates a record of conflict that can color a judge's perception of how you co-parent.

### Respond to Legitimate Questions Promptly

Ignoring communications from your spouse — especially about children or shared finances — can be used against you. If your spouse asks a reasonable question and you do not respond, their attorney can argue that you are uncooperative or are hiding something. If the question leads to a court motion, your silence becomes part of the record.

You do not have to respond immediately to everything. But do not let days pass without acknowledging legitimate requests, even if your full answer is: *"I've received your email and will respond once I've had a chance to speak with my attorney."*

### Confirm Verbal Agreements in Writing

If you and your spouse have a phone call or in-person conversation and reach any kind of agreement — about pickup schedules, who pays which bill, who stays in the house — follow up with a brief written summary.

*"Following up on our call this morning: we agreed that you'll take the kids Thursday and Friday this week, and I'll have them Saturday through Monday. Please let me know if that's not your understanding."*

This is not confrontational. It prevents misunderstandings and creates a record of cooperation, which courts view positively.

## What You Should Not Do

### Do Not Send Emotional Messages

This is the most important rule, and the hardest to follow. In the first two weeks of separation, you will have moments of grief, rage, fear, and desperation — sometimes all in the same afternoon. The temptation to express those feelings to your spouse directly is powerful.

Resist it.

An angry text sent at midnight does not communicate how much you are hurting. It communicates that you are unstable, threatening, or unreasonable — and it goes directly into the opposing attorney's evidence file.

If you need to express what you are feeling, call a friend, a family member, or a therapist. Do not call your spouse.

**Specific messages that routinely damage divorce cases:**

- Threats of any kind, explicit or implied
- Messages accusing your spouse of wrongdoing (especially fault grounds like adultery or cruelty) without legal advice
- Messages about what you will "make sure" happens in the divorce
- Any communication that references your spouse's new relationship in hostile terms
- Anything that could be read as harassment — repeated messages after no response, messages sent through third parties (including your children), showing up uninvited

### Do Not Use the Children as Messengers

Asking your children to relay messages, questions, or emotional content to your spouse is harmful to the children and damaging to your case. Virginia courts take parental alienation and the weaponization of children very seriously.

*"Tell your dad he needs to pick you up by 6"* seems minor. But a pattern of communicating through children demonstrates poor judgment and an inability to co-parent — two things that matter enormously in custody cases.

### Do Not Share Financial Information Voluntarily

In the first two weeks, you and your spouse have not completed financial disclosure. Do not volunteer information about accounts your spouse may not know about, income changes, new debts, or assets you are planning to sell. This is not deception — mandatory financial disclosure happens through the formal legal process. Volunteering information in an unstructured way can be misleading or used out of context.

Conversely, do not attempt to hide assets or accounts in your communications. If your spouse asks directly about a financial account or asset, do not deny it exists. Simply say: *"Financial matters are something I'll need to address through the legal process. My attorney will be in touch."*

### Do Not Discuss the Divorce Strategy

Do not tell your spouse what you plan to ask for, what grounds you intend to use, what witnesses you plan to call, or what you believe you will receive. This information belongs in conversations with your attorney, not with your spouse.

Even in an amicable separation, revealing your strategy puts you at a disadvantage. In a contested case, it can seriously undermine your legal position.

### Do Not Negotiate Settlement Without Your Attorney Present

Your spouse — or their attorney — may approach you in the first two weeks with a proposed settlement, a suggested agreement, or a "reasonable" offer. Do not agree to anything without your attorney reviewing it first.

Settlement offers made in the first weeks are often made before full financial disclosure has occurred. You may not know what you are agreeing to. An agreement signed under emotional pressure, without complete information, is very hard to undo.

This also applies to verbal agreements. *"We agreed you'd get the house"* is a claim your spouse can make in court even if nothing was signed.

### Do Not Copy Your Spouse on Communications With Your Attorney

This should be obvious, but it happens: people accidentally cc their spouse on emails to their attorney, or forward "the wrong thread." Attorney-client communications are privileged — copying your spouse destroys that privilege. Be careful with auto-complete in email.

## What Form Should Communications Take?

| Situation | Recommended Format |
|---|---|
| Child pickup/drop-off logistics | Text or email — brief and factual |
| Schedule changes | Email — confirm in writing |
| Financial decisions affecting both spouses | Email only |
| Emergency involving children | Phone call, followed by email summary |
| Anything your attorney should know about | Email, so you can forward it to counsel |
| Emotional conversations | Do not have them with your spouse |
| Settlement discussions | Through attorneys only |

**Avoid:** Phone calls for anything important unless you follow up in writing. Phone calls leave no record. If your spouse says something significant on the phone, send an email summary immediately after.

**Avoid:** Communicating through family members or mutual friends. These conversations are not privileged, those people can be called as witnesses, and it puts them in an impossible position.

## If Your Spouse Wants to Collect Belongings From the House

This is one of the most fraught situations of the early separation period, and it is handled badly far more often than it should be. Tensions are high, both spouses have claim to items in the home, and an in-person meeting in an emotionally charged environment can escalate quickly.

### Understand the Legal Situation First

If you are still in the marital home and your spouse has moved out, they still have a legal interest in the marital property inside it. You cannot simply refuse to allow them access to their belongings indefinitely — but you also do not have to allow unannounced, unsupervised access.

If your spouse has been ordered out of the home by a court (via a pendente lite order), the order itself may specify what they are and are not permitted to take, and whether they may re-enter. Read any court order carefully and follow it precisely.

If there is no court order, the following guidance applies to informal property retrieval.

### Set It Up Through Written Communication

Do not agree to a spontaneous visit. Respond to any request to collect belongings by email, and propose a specific date, time, and duration.

*"I'm happy to arrange a time for you to collect your personal belongings. I'd suggest Saturday, March 14th between 10 a.m. and noon. Please let me know what items you plan to take so I can make sure they're accessible."*

A scheduled visit with a defined window:
- Gives you time to prepare
- Prevents open-ended access to the home
- Creates a written record of what was agreed
- Reduces the chance of a surprise confrontation

### Have a Third Party Present

Do not be alone in the house with your spouse during a property retrieval visit if there is any history of conflict, threats, or volatility. Options include:

- A trusted friend or family member (not a mutual friend who may be called as a witness)
- A neighbor who can simply be present nearby
- In high-conflict situations, ask your attorney about arranging a police civil standby — many Virginia sheriff's offices will send an officer to keep the peace during property retrieval at no cost

A police civil standby is not an accusation. It is a practical tool that keeps the situation calm and gives both parties a witness.

### Agree in Advance on What Will Be Taken

Ask your spouse to provide a list of items they intend to collect before the visit. Respond with any objections before they arrive — not at the door.

Disputed items (artwork, furniture, family heirlooms, electronics) should not be removed during an informal property visit. Note any disputes in writing and address them through the formal divorce process. If your spouse attempts to take a disputed item, calmly state your objection and make a written record of it immediately afterward — do not physically block them or escalate.

### Document What Leaves the House

Before the visit, photograph the condition of the home and the items your spouse plans to take. After the visit, note in writing (email to yourself or your attorney) what was taken, what condition items were in, and anything that was said or that happened during the visit.

If your spouse takes something you believe is marital property or something you specifically objected to, tell your attorney promptly. Courts can order items returned or their value accounted for in the final distribution.

### What to Do If Your Spouse Arrives Unannounced

If your spouse arrives at the home without notice and demands entry:

- You are not legally required to allow them in immediately, even if they have a legal interest in the property, if there is no court order requiring it
- Speak through the door or on the porch if necessary
- Stay calm — do not raise your voice or make any physical contact
- Tell them you will arrange a scheduled time and follow up by email
- If they become aggressive or threatening, call 911 — do not attempt to manage a threatening situation alone

If your spouse has a key and enters while you are not home, make a written record of when they entered, what they took, and any damage. Change the locks only after consulting your attorney — in some circumstances, changing the locks on a spouse with equal legal right to the home can create legal complications.

---

The first two weeks of separation are a test of discipline as much as anything else. Every message you send, every agreement you make, and every conversation you have is laying groundwork — either for a manageable resolution or for years of litigation. The choices that feel small in the moment often turn out to be the ones that matter most.
