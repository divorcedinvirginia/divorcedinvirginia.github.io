Threats are currency in high-conflict divorce. They are deployed to produce fear, to force concessions, to destabilize your decision-making, and to establish dominance in a process that one or both parties have reframed as a war rather than a legal proceeding.

Some threats are genuine and require immediate action. Many are tactical and require documentation. A significant number are empty and require nothing but your ability to recognize them for what they are and not react.

The inability to distinguish between these categories — treating every threat as an emergency, or dismissing every threat as bluster — is one of the most common and costly mistakes people make in high-conflict divorces. This post is about learning to make that distinction.

## Legal Threats

Legal threats are the most common type in a contested divorce. They arrive in many forms: *"I'm going to take the children,"* *"I'm going to make sure you get nothing in this divorce,"* *"My attorney is going to destroy you in court,"* *"I'm going to expose everything you've done,"* *"I'll file bankruptcy and you'll get nothing,"* *"I'm going to have you arrested."*

Understanding which of these carry any legal weight — and which are performances — requires a basic understanding of how the legal system actually works.

### Threats to Take the Children

**"I'm going to take the children and you'll never see them again."**

This is one of the most common and emotionally devastating threats in a high-conflict custody case. It is also, in most circumstances, legally hollow.

Virginia courts do not give children to one parent simply because that parent wants them. Custody is determined by the best-interests-of-the-child standard, weighing ten specific statutory factors. A parent who has been actively involved in their children's lives, who has provided a stable home environment, and who supports the children's relationship with the other parent has legal protections that do not evaporate because the other parent makes a threat.

**When to take it seriously:** If your spouse has a specific, credible plan to relocate with the children — has a job offer in another state, has moved to an area far from the marital home, has a history of attempting to limit your contact — this moves from threat to warning and requires immediate legal action. File for emergency pendente lite custody immediately. Do not wait to see if they follow through.

**When to ignore it:** A threat made in anger, with no concrete steps behind it, by a parent with no credible plan to relocate and no history of custodial interference, is most likely emotional pressure. Document it. Tell your attorney. Do not react to it in a way that produces communications that can be used against you.

### Threats to Get You Nothing in the Divorce

**"My attorney is going to make sure you walk away with nothing."**

This is almost never legally possible in Virginia. Equitable distribution is governed by statute (Virginia Code § 20-107.3) and applied by a judge — not determined by which attorney makes louder promises. Even in cases where one spouse committed adultery or engaged in financial misconduct, the court's remedies are constrained and calibrated. A spouse who says they will ensure you receive nothing is making a promise their attorney cannot keep.

**When to take it seriously:** Never as stated. What deserves attention is whether the threat is accompanied by specific actions: transferring assets, running up marital debt, hiding financial records, or involving third parties in asset concealment. The threat itself is noise; the actions behind it are signal.

**When to ignore it:** Almost always. The appropriate response is to ensure you have competent legal representation and complete financial documentation — not to escalate in response to a threat that is legally unenforceable.

### Threats to Expose You

**"I'm going to tell everyone what you did."** **"I'm going to testify about everything."** **"I'm going to make sure the judge knows the truth about you."**

Disclosure threats come in two forms. The first is a threat to share genuinely damaging information — an affair, a past legal problem, a financial mistake, conduct the other party has documented. The second is a threat to fabricate or exaggerate information.

**When to take it seriously:** If there is something real that your spouse can disclose that would be legally relevant — evidence of adultery that affects a spousal support claim, financial misconduct that affects equitable distribution, evidence of parenting conduct that affects custody — assume they will use it and discuss it with your attorney immediately. The worst outcome in litigation is being surprised by damaging information you did not disclose to your own attorney. Let your attorney know what exists, so they can advise you how to address it proactively.

**When to ignore it:** If the threatened disclosure involves information that is either not true, not legally relevant, or that you are confident can be addressed in context, the threat is a pressure tactic. Do not offer concessions to suppress it. Concessions made under threat create a pattern — the other party learns that threats produce results, and the threats escalate.

### Threats of Criminal Complaints

**"I'm going to have you arrested."** **"I'm reporting you for child abuse."** **"I'm going to tell the police you stole from me."**

Threats of criminal prosecution or protective orders are among the most alarming in a high-conflict divorce, partly because they can be filed unilaterally — a party who makes a false complaint to law enforcement or child protective services can set an investigative process in motion regardless of the truth of the allegation.

**When to take it seriously:** Always, to the extent of immediately informing your attorney. Even unfounded criminal complaints require a response, and the window to respond effectively is often narrow. Your attorney needs to know immediately when a criminal complaint or CPS report has been threatened or made.

If your spouse has threatened a protective order, do not dismiss it. Even a temporary protective order issued ex parte — based only on your spouse's account — can restrict your access to your home and your children until a hearing can be held. The hearing is your opportunity to respond, but you need to know about the threat to prepare.

**When to ignore it:** A pattern of repeated, never-executed threats — *"I'm going to call the police"* delivered weekly for months — begins to lose credibility. Document every instance. If the threat is not followed through, it becomes evidence of harassment and bad faith. If it is followed through with a false report, Virginia Code § 18.2-461 makes filing a false police report a Class 1 misdemeanor, and Virginia Code § 63.2-1513 addresses false reports to CPS. Courts are aware that false reports are sometimes used as litigation tools and have mechanisms to address them.

### Bankruptcy Threats

**"I'll file for bankruptcy and you'll get nothing."**

This is more legally sophisticated than most financial threats, but it is also overstated. Certain divorce-related obligations — particularly domestic support obligations like spousal support and child support — are non-dischargeable in bankruptcy under 11 U.S.C. § 523(a)(5). A property settlement obligation may have more limited protection depending on how it was structured. But the threat that bankruptcy eliminates all divorce obligations is simply false.

**When to take it seriously:** If your spouse has significant pre-existing debt, is self-employed with unstable income, or has mentioned bankruptcy more than once as a specific plan rather than a general threat, raise it with your attorney. The structure of your separation agreement and the characterization of obligations can affect their treatment in a potential bankruptcy.

**When to ignore it:** As a general threat designed to pressure you into a favorable settlement, it is largely bluster. The bankruptcy process has significant costs and consequences for the filing party, and courts that see it used primarily to avoid divorce obligations have tools to respond.

## Physical Threats

Physical threats in a high-conflict divorce exist on a spectrum from veiled intimidation to direct statements of intent to harm. The way you respond must be calibrated to where on that spectrum any given threat falls — because over-reaction to minor intimidation and under-reaction to genuine danger carry opposite but equally serious consequences.

### Recognizing the Spectrum

**Intimidation without explicit threat:** Blocking your exit, standing too close, controlling tone of voice, using physical size or presence as an implied threat, making aggressive gestures. These behaviors are threatening in context without being legally explicit.

**Explicit verbal threat:** *"You'll regret this,"* *"I know where you live,"* *"You don't know what I'm capable of,"* *"You should be scared."* These are explicit statements of threatening intent, even without specifying a particular act.

**Specific threats of harm:** *"I'm going to hurt you,"* *"If you take the kids I will kill you,"* *"I know where you park your car."* These are specific, actionable threats that warrant immediate response.

**Threats involving weapons:** Any statement that references weapons — *"I have a gun"* — must be treated as the highest-priority category regardless of whether you believe the person would follow through.

### What to Do

**Document it immediately.** The moment a physical threat is made, write down the exact words used, the date, the time, the location, who was present, and your physical response. If it was made in writing — text, email, social media — screenshot it immediately and back it up.

**Take threats involving weapons seriously every time.** Do not calibrate your response to how upset the other person seemed, how long you have known them, or whether you think they "really mean it." Threats involving weapons warrant an immediate call to law enforcement.

**File for a protective order.** In Virginia, a victim of family abuse or threats of family abuse can petition for a protective order in the Juvenile and Domestic Relations District Court. A preliminary protective order can be issued by a judge or magistrate on an ex parte basis — meaning without the other party being present — if a judge finds that there is good cause to believe that a family abuse act has occurred or is likely to occur. The protective order hearing is typically scheduled within 15 days.

A protective order can prohibit the threatening party from contacting you, from being within a specified distance of your home or workplace, and from possessing firearms. Violation of a protective order is a criminal offense in Virginia.

**Involve law enforcement when appropriate.** If you have received a specific threat of physical harm, contact law enforcement. You do not need to wait for violence to occur before calling police. A threat is itself potentially criminal, depending on its specificity and the circumstances.

**Adjust your physical safety immediately.** If you have received a credible physical threat:
- Do not be alone with the threatening person, including at custody exchanges — conduct exchanges in public places, at police stations, or with a third party present
- Tell trusted people in your life what is happening and who to contact if they cannot reach you
- Review who has access to your home — keys, alarm codes, garage door openers
- If you have children, ensure the school has updated emergency contacts and knows that the other parent's access may be restricted

### When You Can Ignore a Physical Threat

There is a narrow category of physical threat that is more reasonably interpreted as an expression of frustration than as a genuine statement of intent — and experienced attorneys, judges, and law enforcement officers know the difference. *"I could kill you right now"* said in obvious emotional frustration by a person with no history of violence and no access to weapons, in a context where it is clearly hyperbolic, is different from the same words delivered calmly by someone with a documented history of violence.

This does not mean you dismiss it. It means you document it, report it to your attorney, and let the pattern of behavior — rather than a single statement — determine your response level. A single isolated expression of frustration in highly charged language may not warrant an emergency protective order. A pattern of escalating threats, physical intimidation, or behavior that follows you into public spaces does.

**When in doubt, err toward protection.** The cost of a protective order you turned out not to need is minor. The cost of dismissing a threat that was genuine is not recoverable.

## Social Threats

Social threats are designed to damage your reputation, your relationships, and your standing in the community. They are less immediately dangerous than physical threats but can have lasting effects on your personal life, your professional life, and — in some circumstances — your custody case.

### Common Social Threats

**"I'm going to tell everyone what you did."** Threats to expose you to mutual friends, the community, your employer, or your children's school.

**"I'm going to post everything online."** Threats to publish damaging information, photographs, or communications on social media.

**"Your family is going to find out."** Threats to contact your parents, siblings, or extended family with information designed to damage those relationships.

**"I'm going to make sure no one in this town trusts you."** General threats to campaign against your reputation in the community.

**"I'll tell your employer."** Threats to contact your workplace with allegations designed to affect your employment.

### When to Take Social Threats Seriously

**Professional threats are the highest priority.** A threat to contact your employer — particularly with false allegations — can affect your income, which directly affects your divorce case. If your spouse threatens to contact your employer, tell your attorney immediately. Depending on what is threatened and whether it is carried out, this may support a defamation claim, a harassment injunction, or a sanctions motion in the divorce proceedings.

**Social media threats involving photographs or private communications** may implicate specific laws. Virginia Code § 18.2-386.2 criminalizes the non-consensual dissemination of intimate images. Threats to publish private communications can support requests for injunctive relief. Tell your attorney before something is posted, not after.

**Threats to involve your children's school or extended family** are worth documenting but are rarely actionable in advance. What you can do is notify those communities proactively with your own brief, measured account — *"Things are difficult between [spouse] and me right now, and I wanted to give you a heads up in case you hear from them"* — so that your spouse's campaign lands in a context you have partially set.

### When to Ignore Social Threats

Most social threats are never executed. They are deployed in moments of high emotion to produce fear and destabilize you. A spouse who says they are going to tell everyone what you did, and then does not, has revealed that the threat was a pressure tactic — not a plan.

**Do not offer concessions in response to social threats.** This is the critical rule. A concession made in response to a social threat — settling a financial issue unfavorably, agreeing to a custody arrangement you do not want, withdrawing a legal motion — teaches the other party that social threats produce results. The threats will escalate.

**Do not retaliate with counter-threats.** Responding to a social threat with your own social threat — *"If you tell people that, I'll tell them about what you did"* — creates a mutually assured destruction dynamic that escalates the conflict and produces the exact scenario both parties are threatening. It also creates a communication that will be read in court.

**The strongest response to most social threats is to do nothing.** Document the threat. Tell your attorney. Then return to living your life with your integrity intact. People who follow through on social campaigns against an ex-spouse routinely damage their own credibility more than their target's. Courts, communities, and friends eventually recognize a campaign for what it is.

## The Pattern Recognition Problem

One of the most disorienting aspects of living with a high-conflict spouse's threats is that the volume and frequency of threats makes it difficult to identify which ones require genuine action. When someone threatens you weekly, the cumulative effect is a kind of numbing — which is itself dangerous, because the threat that requires action can arrive in the same tone and language as the twenty that preceded it.

Protect against this numbing by:

**Maintaining your documentation log regardless of whether you think a threat is serious.** The pattern — dates, frequency, escalation or de-escalation, what preceded each threat — is often more informative than any individual incident.

**Consulting your attorney when threats escalate in specificity or frequency.** A generalized threat that becomes specific, or a pattern that is accelerating, should not be evaluated in isolation. Your attorney has seen patterns like this before and can advise on whether the trajectory is concerning.

**Trusting your instincts about physical safety, but not your emotional reactions about legal and social threats.** Your nervous system is well-calibrated to detect physical danger — if something feels genuinely unsafe, take it seriously. Your emotional reactions to legal and social threats, however, are not reliable guides to their severity, because high-conflict dynamics are specifically designed to produce disproportionate emotional responses to tactical maneuvers.

**Having a trusted third party reality-check your assessment.** A therapist, a trusted friend outside the conflict, or your attorney can provide perspective on whether your response to a given threat is calibrated or whether you are reacting to the emotional charge of a tactic designed to produce exactly that reaction.

---

The goal of threats in a high-conflict divorce is almost always the same: to replace your rational decision-making with reactive, fear-based decision-making. A person who is reacting to threats is not thinking clearly about their legal strategy, their financial interests, or their children's needs.

The best response to any threat — after ensuring immediate physical safety where that is at issue — is to slow down, document what happened, consult your attorney, and make your next decision based on your own interests and your children's wellbeing. Not based on fear.
