You call your child at the agreed time. No answer. You text. No response. You try again an hour later. Still nothing. This happens once, and you tell yourself there must be a reasonable explanation. It happens repeatedly, and you start to understand that your access to your children during the other parent's time is being deliberately controlled.

Blocked communication between a parent and their children during the other parent's parenting time is one of the most common forms of interference in contested custody cases — and one of the most damaging to the interfering parent's legal position. It is also one of the most distressing experiences a parent can go through.

This post covers what it looks like, what it means legally, and what to do about it.

---

## What "Blocking Communication" Actually Looks Like

Interference with parent-child communication is rarely a single dramatic event. It is usually a pattern that builds gradually:

- Calls to the children go unanswered during the other parent's time, consistently
- The children's phones are taken away, turned off, or have their numbers changed
- Texts are delivered but never responded to, even when the children would ordinarily respond
- Video calls are declined or the children are unavailable every time you try
- When you do reach the children, they seem rushed, monitored, or unable to speak freely
- The children report that the other parent is present during calls, listening or watching
- The children say they are not allowed to call you, or that they got in trouble for it
- Your calls are forwarded to voicemail immediately — suggesting the phone is being screened
- The other parent answers the children's phone and tells you the children are "busy" or "asleep" at times when this seems implausible
- Text messages to the children appear to be read and not responded to, or the children later say they did not see the messages

Any of these patterns, sustained over time, represents interference with your parental relationship. Courts in Virginia recognize this conduct and have specific tools to address it.

## Why This Matters Legally

### It Affects the Best-Interests Analysis

Virginia courts determine custody based on the best interests of the child, weighing the factors set out in Virginia Code § 20-124.3. One of those factors is explicitly:

*"The willingness and ability of each parent to actively support the child's contact and relationship with the other parent."*

A parent who blocks communication between the children and the other parent is failing this factor directly and visibly. Courts take seriously any conduct that interferes with a child's relationship with both parents, and a documented pattern of communication interference can significantly damage a parent's custody position — both in a pendente lite hearing and at trial.

### It May Constitute Parental Alienation

Systematically blocking communication is one of the recognized behaviors in parental alienation — a pattern of conduct by one parent that undermines and erodes the child's relationship with the other parent. Virginia courts are increasingly attentive to alienating behavior, and guardians ad litem are specifically trained to identify it.

Communication blocking is particularly insidious because children who are prevented from regular contact with a parent begin to feel that the absent parent has abandoned them — not that their access has been controlled. Over time, this can shift the child's loyalty and affect their expressed preferences in custody proceedings.

### It Violates Most Court Orders

If you have a pendente lite custody order, a consent order, or any court order addressing the parenting schedule, that order almost certainly includes or implies a right to reasonable communication with the children during the other parent's time. Many Virginia custody orders include explicit communication provisions: specific times for phone calls, requirements that calls be answered or returned, and prohibitions on interference with parent-child communication.

Blocking communication in violation of a court order is contempt. The consequences — fines, attorney's fee awards, modification of custody — are the same tools that apply to any other contempt of a custody order.

## What to Do

### Step One: Document Every Instance

Before anything else, build the record. For every instance of blocked or interfered-with communication, record:

- The date and time you attempted contact
- The method (call, text, FaceTime, etc.)
- What happened (no answer, declined call, voicemail, no text response, call ended abruptly)
- Any response you did receive, including from the other parent rather than the children
- Anything the children later told you about why they could not communicate
- Screenshots of unanswered calls, unread or unresponded-to texts, and declined video calls

Most phones log call history with timestamps. Screenshot your call log regularly — it shows the pattern of attempted and unanswered calls in a format that is easy to present in court. Text threads with delivered but unresponded-to messages tell a similar story.

Keep a separate written log in a document or notes app with the date, time, and a brief factual description of each incident. Courts respond to specific documented patterns far more than to general claims that the other parent "never lets me talk to the kids."

### Step Two: Try Multiple Methods and Document the Attempts

If calls are being blocked, try text. If text goes unanswered, try email. If the children have their own devices, try contacting them directly rather than through the other parent's phone. If you have a messaging app the children use, try that.

The goal is two-fold: to actually reach your children if possible, and to create a record demonstrating that the blocking is systematic — not that you only tried once by one method.

Keep the communications you send to the children warm and appropriate. *"Hi sweetheart, I'm thinking of you. Call me when you can — I love you."* Not: *"Your dad/mom won't let you call me and I'm going to tell the judge."* Your children may eventually see these messages, and the content reflects on you.

### Step Three: Do Not Escalate Toward the Other Parent

The temptation when your children are being withheld from communication is to escalate your messages to the other parent — to demand they hand over the phone, to threaten legal consequences, to express your anger directly. This is understandable. It is also consistently counterproductive.

Angry, threatening, or accusatory messages to the other parent about communication access become part of the evidentiary record. A message that says *"You are deliberately cutting me off from my children and I'm going to make sure the judge knows what you're doing"* is inflammatory, and the other parent's attorney will frame it as harassing communication — which inverts the narrative from "they are blocking access" to "you are being aggressive."

Send measured, factual messages to the other parent when necessary: *"I've been unable to reach [child's name] for the past three evenings. Please ensure they have access to their phone during our normal call time."* That is sufficient. Document the response or absence of response.

### Step Four: Notify Your Attorney

Tell your attorney about the pattern as it develops — not after months have passed. Your attorney can:

- Write a formal letter to the other parent's attorney documenting the pattern and demanding it stop — this often produces a different response than messages from you directly, because it signals that the conduct is being officially noted
- Assess whether the pattern warrants an emergency motion or can be addressed through a regularly scheduled hearing
- Build the communication record into your broader custody case narrative
- Advise you whether a formal communication protocol should be proposed (specific call times, required response windows, use of a co-parenting app)

### Step Five: Propose a Formal Communication Protocol

If no court order currently governs communication, this is the moment to formalize one — either by agreement (through attorneys) or by court order.

A communication protocol typically specifies:

- A set time (or window) for calls each day the children are with the other parent
- The platform to be used (phone, FaceTime, a specific app)
- A maximum response window if calls are missed
- Prohibitions on monitoring or being present during calls
- Prohibitions on coaching children on what to say (or not say)
- Consequences for interference, by reference to the contempt provisions of a court order

Proposing a specific, reasonable communication schedule through your attorney — *"We request that each parent have a minimum of one video call per day of thirty minutes during the other parent's parenting time, to occur between 7:00 and 8:00 p.m., with calls to be answered or returned within thirty minutes"* — is a constructive, court-friendly approach. It demonstrates that you want to resolve the issue, not simply punish the other parent. If they refuse a reasonable proposal, that refusal is itself evidence.

## Co-Parenting Apps as Both a Solution and Evidence

Co-parenting communication apps — such as OurFamilyWizard, TalkingParents, or Coparently — are specifically designed for separated and divorced parents. They log all messages with timestamps that cannot be altered, archive communications for court use, and create a neutral record of who sent what, when, and whether it was read.

Courts in Virginia increasingly order or strongly recommend the use of co-parenting apps in contested cases. Proposing their use is almost always well-received by a judge. Refusing to use one when proposed is not.

**As a documentation tool:** If you are already using a co-parenting app, every unanswered message, every declined call initiated through the app, and every message left unread is automatically timestamped and preserved. This evidence is more difficult to dispute than screenshots from a personal phone because the app's records are independently verifiable.

**As a solution:** Shifting all communication to a co-parenting app can reduce the other parent's ability to claim they did not receive your messages, did not know you called, or that the children's phones just happened to be unavailable. The app creates accountability on both sides.

## When There Is a Court Order Governing Communication

If your court order includes communication provisions and the other parent is violating them, your tools become significantly stronger.

**File a motion for rule to show cause.** This is the contempt mechanism for custody order violations. Bring your documented record — the call logs, the messages, the written log — and ask the court to order the other parent to appear and explain the violations.

Courts can and do impose meaningful consequences for communication interference:

- Monetary fines
- Attorney's fee awards in your favor
- Modification of the custody schedule — including reduced parenting time for the interfering parent
- Required use of a specific co-parenting communication app
- Addition of explicit communication provisions to the existing order
- In severe or repeated cases, a change in primary physical custody

**Courts notice patterns.** A single missed call might be explained away. A documented pattern of dozens of missed calls, declined video calls, and unresponded-to texts spanning weeks or months — presented with timestamps and screenshots — is not something a judge can reasonably accept as coincidental.

## When the Children Say They Don't Want to Call

This adds a layer of complexity. Some children, particularly adolescents, genuinely prefer not to be on the phone with the absent parent. Others say they do not want to call because the other parent has coached them, made them feel guilty for enjoying time with you, or punished them in subtle ways for expressing warmth toward you.

The difference matters, but it is not always easy to identify from the outside.

**What to do:**

- Do not force the children or make them feel guilty for not calling. The goal is to keep the relationship open, not to create another source of anxiety for them.
- Keep your attempted contacts brief and warm. Leave voicemails they can listen to when the other parent is not present. *"Hey — just wanted to hear your voice. I love you. Call me when you want to talk."*
- If you believe the children are being coached or pressured not to communicate, document the specific things they say when they do have access. *"Mom said I didn't have to call if I didn't feel like it"* or *"Dad said he would be upset if I spent the whole visit on the phone with you"* are the kinds of statements that are relevant to the broader alienation picture.
- Report the pattern to your attorney. If a guardian ad litem has been appointed, report it to them as well — they will assess the children's communication preferences as part of their overall evaluation.

## What Not to Do

**Do not show up at the other parent's home unannounced** to demand to see or speak with the children. Even if your instinct is to verify that they are safe and that they know you care, an unannounced appearance at the other parent's home during their parenting time is legally problematic and will be characterized as harassment.

**Do not contact the children through third parties** — grandparents, school friends, or other family members who can relay messages. This workaround puts other people in the middle and creates its own set of problems.

**Do not send the children devices specifically to circumvent the other parent's control.** A secret phone, a hidden tablet, or an account the other parent does not know about may feel like a solution, but it creates distrust, puts the children in the middle, and will not reflect well on you in court.

**Do not use the children as the vehicle for your legal complaints** when you do reach them. Do not tell them you are going to tell the judge, that the other parent is breaking the rules, or that what is happening to you is unfair. Children who are used to carry legal messages between parents are being harmed, and courts identify this quickly.

---

## The Larger Picture

Communication interference during the other parent's time is not a peripheral issue in a custody case. It is evidence about who these parents are, how they co-parent, and what life with each of them looks like for the children.

A parent who facilitates the children's relationship with the other parent — who answers the phone at the agreed time, who encourages the children to call, who ensures the children have access to their devices — is demonstrating exactly the co-parenting capacity that Virginia courts are looking for.

A parent who systematically blocks, interrupts, or undermines that communication is demonstrating the opposite. And the record you build — of every attempt, every non-answer, every declined call — is the evidence that shows the court which parent is which.
