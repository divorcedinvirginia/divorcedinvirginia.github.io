School events are unavoidable. The spring concert, the science fair, the basketball game, the awards ceremony — these happen on a fixed schedule, they matter deeply to your children, and they require both parents to be in the same place at the same time. During a contested divorce, that combination can feel impossible to navigate.

It is not impossible. But it requires deliberate preparation, clear expectations, and an unflinching focus on what the events are actually about: your children.

---

## Why This Is Harder Than It Looks

In a stable marriage, attending school events together is automatic. During a divorce, that same event becomes a choreography problem, an emotional minefield, and sometimes a legal matter — all at once.

The difficulty is compounded by the fact that the school community is watching. Teachers, other parents, coaches, and administrators notice how divorcing parents behave at these events. In custody cases, impressions formed by school personnel can find their way into testimony, declarations, or a guardian ad litem's report. The school counselor who observed how you handled the third-grade play in October may be interviewed by a guardian ad litem in December.

Your children are also watching — more closely than you realize. How you treat their other parent at the moments that matter most to them tells them more about who you are than almost anything else you could do.

## Before the Event: Preparation Is Everything

### Communicate About Logistics in Advance — in Writing

Establish the practical details before the day of the event, not in the parking lot. Through a co-parenting app, email, or text, confirm:

- What time you each plan to arrive
- Where you each plan to sit (adjacent, or in different areas — more on this below)
- Whether you will acknowledge each other when you arrive
- What happens with the children before the event if one parent is dropping them off
- Whether you will speak at all, and how briefly
- Any specific plans involving the children after the event (who takes them home if it is not a scheduled exchange day)

Sorting out logistics in writing before the event accomplishes two things: it reduces the chance of a surprise confrontation in a public place, and it creates a record of your effort to co-parent cooperatively.

If your spouse does not respond to reasonable logistical messages, document that too. An unanswered message about event logistics is evidence of the other parent's approach to co-parenting.

### Tell the School About the Divorce

If you have not already done so, inform the school's main office and your child's teacher that you and your spouse are separating. This matters for several reasons:

- Schools need to know who has authority to pick up the children and under what circumstances. If a custody order is in place, provide a copy.
- Many schools can set up separate parent-teacher communication so that both parents receive all information independently — report cards, newsletters, event notices — rather than one parent having to relay it to the other.
- School counselors who know a child is going through a divorce can provide additional support and are better positioned to notice and respond to changes in behavior.
- If the divorce becomes acrimonious and a parent does something inappropriate at a school event, school staff who are already aware of the situation are better equipped to respond.

Notify the school calmly and briefly. You do not need to explain the reasons for the divorce, assign blame, or predict how the other parent might behave. Simply say: *"We want to make sure the school has current information. We're separating, and we'd like to make sure both of us continue to receive all communications about [child's name] directly."*

### Prepare Your Children

Talk to your children before significant events so they know what to expect. Keep it simple and reassuring:

*"Both Mom and I are coming to your concert tonight. We're both so excited to see you perform. We might sit in different spots so there's room for everyone, but we'll both be there cheering for you."*

Children who are not prepared for both parents to appear at the same event — particularly if the separation was recent or volatile — can feel ambushed by the situation. A brief, calm, matter-of-fact preview removes the element of surprise and gives them something concrete to hold onto.

Do not use the preparation conversation to prime the children to manage your emotional needs. *"I really hope your father doesn't make a scene"* is not preparation — it is using your child as an emotional support. They should walk in expecting the event to be about them, not braced to manage their parents.

## At the Event: What to Do

### Arrive With a Plan and Stick to It

Know before you walk in where you are going to sit, what your interaction with your spouse will look like (a brief, civil acknowledgment or, if appropriate, sitting separately without interaction), and how you will respond if the unexpected happens.

Going in with a plan prevents reactive decisions made in the moment. The parent who wanders in without a plan and ends up seated next to their spouse by accident, or who has to improvise a response to an unexpected approach, is the parent who is most likely to behave in ways they will later regret.

### Sitting Together vs. Sitting Apart

This is one of the most common questions divorcing parents have, and there is no universal right answer. It depends on:

**The temperature of the relationship.** If you and your spouse can sit near each other without visible tension, suppressed hostility, or the risk of a whispered argument breaking out during the event, sitting in the same general area is often better for the children — it projects normalcy and keeps the focus on them rather than on which parent is where in the auditorium.

**The children's awareness and sensitivity.** Some children are acutely aware of their parents' positions relative to each other. Some are completely absorbed in their own performance and barely register where their parents are sitting. Know your child. If your child will spend their entire performance scanning the room to confirm both parents are there and safe, sitting visibly close together may be reassuring. If your child is old enough to feel embarrassed by parental proximity that looks forced, two seats apart in the same row may actually be less conspicuous than a conspicuously separate arrangement.

**The level of conflict.** If there is active litigation, domestic violence history, a protective order, or a level of hostility that makes proximity genuinely unsafe or destabilizing, sit separately — in different sections if the venue allows it. The goal is not to perform togetherness. The goal is for the children to see both parents present and calm.

### The Brief Civil Acknowledgment

In most circumstances, a brief, civil acknowledgment of the other parent at a school event is appropriate and expected. A nod, a short greeting, an exchange of a few words about the event — *"She looked so nervous backstage, she did great"* — is the minimum standard of civilized behavior that the surrounding community expects and that your children benefit from seeing.

This is not a moment for conversation about the divorce, the custody case, attorney fees, the house, or anything else related to the legal proceedings. If the other parent attempts to start such a conversation, it is entirely appropriate to say: *"Today's about [child's name]. Let's keep it that way."*

### When the Other Parent's New Partner Is There

This adds a layer of complexity that many parents are not prepared for. If the other parent brings a new partner — particularly early in the separation — the emotional response is often significant.

Your job at the school event is to manage that response privately. Do not:

- React visibly in a way your children or other parents will notice
- Make comments about the new person's presence to your children, your friends, or anyone at the event
- Attempt to speak to the new partner in a way that creates a scene
- Position yourself near them in a way that invites confrontation

You are allowed to feel whatever you feel. You are not allowed to perform it at your child's school event in front of their teachers, their friends, and the school community.

### What to Do If the Other Parent Is Causing a Problem

If the other parent is visibly hostile, is attempting to start an argument, or is behaving in a way that is inappropriate for the setting:

**Do not engage.** The instinct to defend yourself, to explain your position, or to make clear to bystanders that this is not your fault is powerful — and almost always makes the situation worse. A parent who responds to provocations by escalating is indistinguishable from a parent who instigated them.

**Move away.** Physically remove yourself from the proximity of the conflict. Go to a different part of the venue. The other parent's ability to involve you in a confrontation depends on your being present for it.

**Stay calm and visible.** The goal is for bystanders — including school staff — to observe that one parent is calm and one is not. That observation is more powerful than anything you could say.

**Document it afterward.** When you get home, write a brief factual account of what happened: the date, the event, what the other parent said or did, who was present, and how you responded. If school staff or other parents witnessed the conduct, note that. This may be relevant in your custody case.

**Do not retaliate in kind.** An outburst at a school event that begins with the other parent but ends with both of you arguing in the parking lot is, in the record of a custody case, simply two parents fighting at a school event.

## What Not to Do

### Do Not Use the Event as a Venue for Adult Business

School events are not the place to discuss the custody schedule, negotiate financial terms, exchange documents, confront your spouse about their behavior, or deliver messages from your attorney. The event is for the children. Any conversation that is not about the event itself and the child at its center should be deferred.

If your spouse attempts to initiate a substantive conversation at a school event, you are completely within your rights to say: *"I'm not going to discuss that here. Contact my attorney."* Say it quietly, without hostility, and then redirect your attention to the event.

### Do Not Recruit Witnesses or Allies at the Event

Some parents use school events to manage their social narrative — making sure mutual friends see them as the composed, reasonable parent, positioning themselves near people who are sympathetic, or subtly steering conversations toward what the other parent has done. This is transparent to anyone paying attention, and it puts the other parents at the event in an uncomfortable position.

Your only job at the event is to be present for your child. Everything else is secondary.

### Do Not Compare Notes With Other Parents About the Other Parent's Behavior

The other school parents are not your support group, your strategists, or your intelligence network. Conversations at school events that drift toward your spouse's behavior, your custody situation, or the details of your divorce create social awkwardness, spread information you cannot control, and put other parents in a position of feeling like they have been recruited into the conflict.

Other parents have their own complicated feelings about divorce — many of them are either going through one themselves or have friends who are. The parent who uses pickup lines or halftime at the soccer game to process their divorce is recognized for what they are doing. The parent who simply shows up, cheers for the kids, and goes home with their dignity intact is respected.

### Do Not Create a Scene

This should not need to be said, but it does. Raised voices, visible crying, confrontational body language, or any conduct that causes surrounding parents to stare or children to stop what they are doing and look — all of it harms your children and your legal case simultaneously.

Your child is on the stage, on the field, or in the classroom. They know where their parents are. They will remember how their parents behaved.

## Parent-Teacher Conferences: A Special Case

Parent-teacher conferences are different from performances and sports events because they involve a direct conversation about your child with a teacher or administrator. When both parents attend the same conference — together or separately — additional considerations apply.

**Request separate conference times if necessary.** Most schools will accommodate a request to hold separate parent-teacher conferences if the relationship between the parents is too contentious for a joint meeting to be productive. This is a common accommodation and does not require extensive explanation. *"We are separating and would find it helpful to meet separately so each of us can focus fully on [child's name]"* is sufficient.

**If you attend together:** Do not use the conference to air grievances about the other parent's parenting. The teacher is there to discuss your child's academic progress and wellbeing — not to be a witness to your co-parenting conflict, and not to take sides. A conference that devolves into the parents disagreeing about custody arrangements or blaming each other for the child's distress is remembered by the teacher.

**What to listen for:** A parent-teacher conference during a divorce is an important opportunity to understand how your child is doing from the perspective of someone who sees them every day in a neutral context. Teachers often observe things about a child's emotional state, social behavior, and academic engagement that parents miss because they are too close to the situation. Ask directly: *"We're going through a significant change in our family right now. Have you noticed anything in [child's name]'s behavior or mood that we should be aware of?"*

**Share what the teacher says with the other parent.** If you attend the conference and the other parent does not (or vice versa), share the substance of the conversation. This is both the courteous and legally appropriate thing to do — withholding school information from the other parent can be characterized as interference with their parental rights. A brief email summary after the conference serves this purpose and creates a record of your cooperative approach.

## The Standard to Hold Yourself To

Every school event during a contested divorce is, in some sense, an audition. Not a performance — an audition. The question each event implicitly asks is: *Can this parent put their child's needs above their own emotional state when it is hardest to do so?*

The teachers who notice that you are composed. The other parents who see that you treated your spouse civilly even when it was clearly difficult. The school counselor who observes that you were fully present for your child's performance rather than absorbed in whatever was happening between you and your spouse in the third row. These people form impressions that can surface in a custody case in ways you will never fully anticipate.

More importantly, your child will carry a memory of whether they were able to look up from the stage, the field, or the podium and find both of their parents watching them — calm, present, and proud — rather than watching each other.

That memory is worth more than any legal advantage a school event confrontation could possibly produce.
