Hiring a divorce attorney is one of the most consequential decisions you will make during the entire process. The right attorney protects your interests, gives you honest assessments, and helps you reach a resolution efficiently. The wrong one can drag a manageable case into years of litigation, drain your savings, and leave you emotionally destroyed — while their own bank account grows with every motion filed.

This post covers what to look for in an attorney for both contested and uncontested divorces, and how to identify attorneys who are likely to work against your best interest.

---

## Start with What Kind of Case You Have

Before you can evaluate attorneys, you need an honest assessment of your situation. The type of divorce you are facing determines what kind of attorney you actually need.

**Uncontested divorce:** You and your spouse agree (or expect to agree) on all major issues — property, debt, support, and custody. You need an attorney who is efficient, organized, and skilled at drafting a comprehensive separation agreement. You do not need a courtroom warrior.

**Contested divorce:** You and your spouse disagree on significant issues. You need an attorney with genuine litigation experience, the ability to manage complex discovery, and the judgment to know when to fight and when to settle. Not every family law attorney who calls themselves a litigator has actually tried cases.

**High-conflict contested divorce:** Involves parental alienation, hidden assets, domestic violence, business interests, or a spouse who is determined to litigate every issue. You need an attorney with a track record in exactly these situations — and the temperament to manage a case that may drag on for years without losing strategic focus.

Mismatching the attorney to the case is expensive. A litigation-heavy attorney hired for an uncontested case will find issues to fight over. A transactional attorney hired for a high-conflict case may be overwhelmed when the other side turns aggressive.

## What Actually Matters When Evaluating an Attorney

### 1. Virginia Family Law Experience — Specifically

Family law is a specialized field. An attorney who handles business litigation, criminal defense, or general civil matters on the side is not the same as one who practices Virginia family law full time. Divorce law involves:

- Virginia-specific equitable distribution standards (§ 20-107.3)
- Local court rules that vary by circuit
- Judges and commissioners who have individual preferences and tendencies
- Relationships with the local bar that affect negotiation dynamics

Ask specifically: **What percentage of your practice is Virginia family law?** If the answer is less than a majority, keep looking unless you have a specific reason to use that attorney.

### 2. Relevant Case Experience, Not Just General Experience

Years in practice matter less than the right kind of experience. An attorney who has handled 500 uncontested divorces is not necessarily equipped for a high-asset contested case involving a business valuation and a pension. Ask directly:

- Have you handled cases involving [your key issue — custody dispute, business interest, military retirement, hidden assets]?
- Have you taken cases to trial in [your county]?
- Have you appeared before [the specific judge or commissioner] assigned to your case?

An attorney who has tried cases before your judge knows what arguments land, what the judge cares about, and what is unlikely to get traction. That knowledge has real dollar value.

### 3. Honest Case Assessment, Not Flattery

Be wary of any attorney who tells you exactly what you want to hear in an initial consultation. A good attorney will give you an honest read of your case — including the parts that do not favor you. They will tell you:

- What you are likely to get in equitable distribution based on the facts
- Whether fault grounds are worth pursuing given the cost and proof required
- What a realistic custody outcome looks like given Virginia's statutory factors
- What this case is likely to cost under different scenarios

If an attorney validates every position you take and promises outcomes they cannot guarantee, they are either telling you what you want to hear to land the retainer, or they lack the experience to assess your case accurately. Either way, it is a problem.

### 4. Clear Communication About Fees

A trustworthy attorney explains their fee structure clearly and in writing before you sign a retainer agreement. You should know:

- The hourly rate for the attorney and any associates or paralegals who may work on your case
- What the retainer covers and when it will be replenished
- How billing increments work (most Virginia firms bill in 0.1-hour or 0.25-hour increments)
- What a realistic total cost range looks like for your type of case
- Whether they offer any flat-fee services for uncontested matters

If an attorney is vague about fees, deflects questions about total cost, or makes you feel uncomfortable for asking, that is a red flag.

### 5. Responsiveness and Communication Style

Your attorney does not need to be your therapist, but they do need to communicate clearly and return calls within a reasonable time. In the initial consultation, notice:

- Do they listen, or do they dominate the conversation?
- Do they explain legal concepts in plain language?
- Are they distracted, rushed, or dismissive of your questions?
- Do they have a clear system for keeping clients informed?

A pattern of poor communication in the consultation predicts poor communication throughout your case — which leads to missed deadlines, surprises, and avoidable errors.

### 6. Local Court Knowledge

Divorce law is administered county by county in Virginia. Each circuit court has its own local rules, docket practices, preferred formats for motions and orders, and informal norms that experienced local practitioners know well. An attorney who regularly practices in Fairfax will not know the unwritten preferences of the Roanoke City Circuit Court, and vice versa.

Hire an attorney who regularly appears in the jurisdiction where your case will be filed.

## Hiring for an Uncontested Divorce

An uncontested divorce does not require a litigator. What it requires is an attorney who can:

- Draft a thorough, enforceable separation agreement that anticipates future disputes
- Identify issues you may not have thought of (QDRO language for retirement accounts, beneficiary designations, tax treatment of support, life insurance obligations)
- Move efficiently through the process without creating unnecessary back-and-forth

**For an uncontested case, ask:**
- Do you offer flat-fee or limited-scope representation for uncontested divorces?
- How many uncontested divorces do you handle per year?
- What is your typical timeline from engagement to filing?
- Will you review an agreement my spouse and I have already drafted?

Many Virginia attorneys offer uncontested divorce packages ranging from $1,500 to $3,500. If an attorney insists on an open-ended hourly retainer for a straightforward uncontested case with no children and simple assets, that is worth questioning.

**One caution:** Do not use the same attorney as your spouse. Even in an amicable divorce, one attorney can only represent one party. The other spouse can have the agreement reviewed by a separate attorney for a flat fee — this is called limited-scope representation and is worth the cost.

## Hiring for a Contested Divorce

A contested divorce requires more from your attorney — and more scrutiny from you in the hiring process.

**For a contested case, ask:**
- How many contested divorces have you taken to trial in the last three years?
- What is your approach to discovery in a case like mine?
- How do you decide when to settle versus when to litigate?
- Who else in your firm might work on my case, and what are their rates?
- How do you keep clients informed about case status and strategy?

The answer to the settlement question is particularly revealing. A good contested divorce attorney approaches every case with settlement as the goal and litigation as the last resort. They know that going to trial is expensive, uncertain, and emotionally exhausting — and that most clients are better served by a negotiated resolution. An attorney who talks primarily about "fighting" and "winning" may be priming you for expensive litigation that serves their interests more than yours.

## How to Identify an Attorney Who Will Prolong Your Case

This is the part of attorney selection that most guides skip. The uncomfortable truth is that divorce litigation is a business, and some attorneys — not all, but some — have financial incentives to keep cases active rather than resolve them. Knowing the warning signs can save you tens of thousands of dollars.

### The Scorched-Earth Litigator

Some attorneys build a reputation around aggressive, take-no-prisoners litigation. They file motions for everything, contest every discovery response, and treat every issue as a battle to be won. In rare high-conflict cases with genuine bad actors on the other side, this approach has its place.

In most cases, it is catastrophically expensive and counterproductive. Courts become frustrated with attorneys who litigate in bad faith or engage in excessive motion practice. Judges can — and do — sanction attorneys and award fees against parties who abuse the process.

**Signs you may be talking to a scorched-earth attorney:**
- They immediately recommend aggressive motions before fully understanding your situation
- They speak disparagingly about the opposing spouse before knowing any facts
- They suggest fault grounds (adultery, cruelty) without explaining the high evidentiary standard and the cost of proving them
- They encourage you to "go after" things you have already said you do not care about
- They express enthusiasm for litigation rather than treating it as a necessary cost

### The Conflict Escalator

Some attorneys subtly — or not so subtly — encourage clients to take harder positions than necessary, raising the temperature of the case and making settlement less likely. Every time settlement talks break down, more billable hours follow.

**Signs of a conflict escalator:**
- They characterize your spouse's reasonable positions as outrageous or abusive
- They discourage you from communicating directly with your spouse, even on routine matters
- They send aggressive letters on your behalf without discussing the strategic cost
- They tell you that you are "entitled" to more than what the law actually supports
- They react negatively when you suggest settling

### The Slow Drafter

Some attorneys are simply disorganized or overextended — they take too many cases, miss deadlines, and let your case sit idle for weeks at a time while your retainer is being billed for routine correspondence. This is not always malicious, but the effect on your case (and your bill) is the same.

**Signs of slow drafting:**
- They repeatedly miss internal deadlines they set themselves
- Agreements, motions, and orders take significantly longer to draft than estimated
- You receive documents with obvious errors (wrong names, wrong dates, terms you did not agree to)
- You hear about developments in your own case from your spouse before your attorney tells you

### The Therapy Biller

A divorce is emotionally traumatic, and many clients need to process those emotions with their attorney. Some attorneys allow — or encourage — long emotional conversations during billable time, resulting in invoices that reflect grief counseling at litigation rates.

This is not to say that attorneys should be cold or dismissive. But if you are regularly spending 30–60 minutes on the phone with your attorney venting about your spouse's behavior, and those calls are appearing on your invoice at $400/hour, you are paying an enormous premium for a service a therapist could provide for a fraction of the cost.

**The fix:** Keep attorney calls focused on legal strategy and decisions. Hire a therapist for emotional support — this is not weakness, it is smart financial management.

### The Motion Filer

Some attorneys generate revenue by filing motions that are unlikely to succeed but consume significant preparation time. These include repetitive motions to compel discovery responses, motions in limine on minor evidentiary issues, and objections to commissioner's reports that have little chance of being sustained.

Motion practice has its place. But look at your invoices carefully. If you are being billed for motions that did not advance your case, were denied, or that your attorney later admits were unlikely to succeed, ask directly why they were filed.

### The False Urgency Creator

Some attorneys generate a sense of constant emergency that keeps clients anxious and calling. Every development is a "critical issue" requiring immediate attention. Every filing by the other side requires an "urgent response." This keeps the billable hours flowing and keeps the client emotionally dependent on the attorney.

Real emergencies in divorce cases exist — emergency custody hearings, financial asset dissipation, violations of protective orders. But not every motion filed by opposing counsel is a crisis. A good attorney helps you distinguish between the two.

## Practical Steps Before You Sign a Retainer

1. **Consult at least two attorneys.** Most charge for consultations ($150–$300), and it is worth the cost. The differences in how attorneys assess your case, communicate with you, and discuss fees will be immediately apparent.

2. **Check the Virginia State Bar.** Search the [VSB Member Directory](https://www.vsb.org/site/members/search) to verify the attorney is licensed and in good standing. Check for any public disciplinary actions.

3. **Ask for a written fee agreement.** Virginia Rules of Professional Conduct require written fee agreements for most matters. If an attorney resists putting fees in writing, walk away.

4. **Ask about staffing.** Will your case primarily be handled by the attorney you met, or by an associate or paralegal? This is not necessarily a problem, but you should know what you are paying for.

5. **Trust your instincts about communication.** If you felt dismissed, rushed, or confused after the initial consultation, that experience will repeat throughout the case.

6. **Review your invoices.** When invoices arrive, read them line by line. Question charges you do not recognize. A good attorney will explain any item without defensiveness. If an attorney reacts badly to invoice questions, that tells you something important.

---

The right attorney does not promise you the world. They give you an honest assessment, explain your options clearly, pursue efficient resolution when possible, and go to the mat for you when necessary. That combination is what you are looking for — not the most aggressive, the cheapest, or the one who tells you you deserve everything.
