In a contested Virginia divorce, equitable distribution requires the court to divide the marital estate fairly — but that process depends entirely on knowing what is in the estate. When a spouse hides assets, income, or debt, every downstream calculation is corrupted: property division is skewed, spousal support is understated, and the outcome bears no relationship to the actual financial picture of the marriage.

Hidden assets are more common than many people expect, and they take forms that range from obvious to sophisticated. Discovery — the formal process by which each party obtains financial information from the other — is the primary mechanism for finding them. Used aggressively and methodically, discovery can surface assets a spouse believes are invisible.

---

## Why Spouses Hide Assets

Understanding the motivation helps identify the method. Spouses who hide assets typically do so for one of a few reasons:

- **To reduce the marital estate** so there is less to divide
- **To deflate apparent income** to minimize spousal support or child support obligations
- **To shelter a specific asset** — often a business, an inheritance, or cash — from equitable distribution
- **To gain negotiating leverage** by controlling information

The spouse doing the hiding typically has more control over the marital finances. In marriages where one spouse managed the money, paid the bills, ran a business, or filed the tax returns, the other spouse is often genuinely unaware of what exists. That information asymmetry is the precondition for concealment.

## Start With What You Know

Before using formal discovery tools, inventory what you already have access to:

- **Tax returns** — federal and state, for at least the last three to five years. These show income from all sources: wages, business income, rental income, capital gains, interest, dividends. Discrepancies between reported income and lifestyle are often visible here.
- **Bank statements** — for every account you know about. Look for large cash withdrawals, transfers to unfamiliar accounts, or recurring payments to unknown payees.
- **Credit card statements** — these reveal spending patterns, travel, purchases, and payments to people or businesses you don't recognize.
- **Mortgage and loan documents** — these disclose assets pledged as collateral and may reveal accounts or property you didn't know existed.
- **Investment and brokerage statements** — some spouses open separate brokerage accounts without their partner's knowledge.
- **Pay stubs and employer documents** — look at the full earnings statement, including deferred compensation, stock options, and retirement contributions.

Gather and preserve everything you have access to before filing, or as early as possible in the case. Once litigation begins, a spouse who knows they are being scrutinized may move money or destroy records.

## Formal Discovery Tools

Virginia's Rules of Civil Procedure give each party robust discovery rights. In a contested divorce with financial disputes, these tools should be used systematically and early.

### Interrogatories

Interrogatories are written questions the other party must answer under oath. Financial interrogatories in a divorce case typically ask:

- List all bank accounts, investment accounts, retirement accounts, and financial accounts of any kind, including account numbers and institutions
- List all real property in which you have any ownership interest
- List all businesses in which you have any ownership interest, including percentage of ownership
- Identify all sources of income during the marriage, including self-employment, consulting, and cash payments
- Identify all transfers of money or property over $500 during the past three years
- List all debts and liabilities
- Identify all safe deposit boxes and their contents
- List all cryptocurrency holdings or digital assets

Interrogatories are answered in writing and signed under oath. Lying in response to interrogatories is perjury. More commonly, a spouse who is hiding assets will give incomplete, evasive, or technically accurate but misleading answers — which gives you grounds for follow-up discovery and, if the concealment is exposed, sanctions.

### Requests for Production of Documents

Requests for production require the other party to provide actual documents. In a financial discovery context, this means:

- Bank statements for all accounts for the past three to five years
- Credit card statements
- Tax returns (personal and business)
- Business financial statements, profit and loss statements, balance sheets
- Retirement account statements
- Investment account statements
- Loan applications (these are gold — people tell the truth about their assets when they want a loan)
- Insurance policies with cash value
- Appraisals of property
- Business valuation reports
- Records of any asset transfers during the past three years

Loan applications deserve particular attention. A spouse who has concealed assets will often disclose them accurately on a loan application because lenders verify information and understating assets could constitute bank fraud. A mortgage application from two years ago showing a $500,000 brokerage account that does not appear in discovery is a significant finding.

### Requests for Admission

Requests for admission ask the other party to admit or deny specific factual statements. In asset discovery, they are used to lock in facts that are difficult to deny: "Admit that you hold a financial interest in XYZ LLC." "Admit that you received a payment of $75,000 from ABC Company in March 2024." Failure to respond within 21 days means the matter is **deemed admitted** — a powerful consequence for a spouse who ignores discovery obligations.

### Depositions

Depositions are in-person examinations under oath. A deposition of your spouse about their finances allows your attorney to ask follow-up questions in real time — something written interrogatories cannot do. When a deposition witness gives an evasive or implausible answer, the examining attorney can press immediately: "You said you have no interest in any business. Who owns the LLC that paid $8,000 a month into your checking account for the past two years?"

Depositions are also useful for deposing **third parties** — the spouse's business partner, accountant, employer, or financial advisor. Third-party witnesses are often less motivated to be evasive, and their testimony can directly contradict the spouse's.

### Subpoenas to Financial Institutions

If you have reason to believe an account exists at a particular institution, your attorney can subpoena the institution directly. A subpoena to a bank or brokerage requires them to produce records regardless of what your spouse says. This is particularly useful when you have circumstantial evidence of an account — a deposit slip, a statement fragment, a reference in an email — but your spouse denies it exists.

Subpoenas can be issued to:
- Banks and credit unions
- Brokerage and investment firms
- Cryptocurrency exchanges
- PayPal, Venmo, Cash App, and similar platforms
- Retirement plan administrators
- Insurance companies
- Business entities

## Red Flags That Suggest Hidden Assets

Certain patterns in financial documents are associated with concealment. Watch for:

**Sudden income decline.** A spouse who runs a business may report sharply reduced income as the divorce approaches, through deferred invoicing, inflated expenses, or payments to fictitious vendors. Compare business income over multiple years. A profitable business that suddenly breaks even the year before the divorce filing warrants scrutiny.

**Large unexplained withdrawals.** Cash withdrawals of several thousand dollars at a time, with no corresponding expenditure, may represent funds being moved into a cash reserve or transferred to a third party for temporary safekeeping.

**Transfers to family members or friends.** A spouse may "loan" money to a parent, sibling, or close friend with the expectation that it will be returned after the divorce. These transfers show up in bank records and tax returns. The loan documentation, if any exists, is often thin.

**Overpayment of taxes.** A spouse who overpays withholding tax gets a refund — but if they file separately after the divorce, they receive a refund they did not share with you. This is a documented method of sheltering cash.

**Delayed bonuses or commissions.** An employer or business partner who agrees to delay paying a bonus until after the divorce is finalized is a party to the concealment. Email communications between the spouse and their employer or business partners may reveal this arrangement.

**Unfamiliar accounts or payees.** Regular payments to a name or company you don't recognize may represent payments to a shell entity, a storage unit, a hidden account, or a mistress whose expenses the spouse is absorbing from marital funds.

**Cryptocurrency.** Digital asset holdings are harder to trace but not invisible. Cryptocurrency purchases show up as transfers from bank accounts to exchanges. If your spouse was active in cryptocurrency, a forensic accountant with digital asset experience can trace transactions on public blockchain ledgers.

**Business accounts used for personal expenses.** A spouse who runs a business may pay personal expenses through the business — vacations, car payments, meals, home renovations — and deduct them as business expenses. This reduces apparent personal income while maintaining the actual lifestyle.

## The Role of a Forensic Accountant

In cases involving significant assets, business ownership, or suspected income manipulation, a **forensic accountant** is often essential. A forensic accountant is a CPA with specialized training in financial investigation. In a divorce case, they can:

- Analyze business records to determine true income versus reported income
- Reconstruct cash flow from lifestyle analysis — comparing reported income to actual spending
- Trace transfers and identify undisclosed accounts
- Identify inflated business expenses or fictitious vendors
- Value a business while accounting for income that has been hidden through the business

Lifestyle analysis is particularly powerful. If your spouse reports $80,000 in annual income but you spent $200,000 a year on housing, travel, cars, private schools, and meals during the marriage, someone has to explain the gap. A forensic accountant can document the discrepancy systematically.

The forensic accountant must be designated as an expert witness before the expert designation deadline in your scheduling order. Retain them early — their investigation takes time, and their conclusions need to be complete before the discovery cutoff.

## What Happens When Hidden Assets Are Found

When discovery reveals that a spouse has concealed assets, the consequences are significant:

**Sanctions.** Virginia courts have authority under Rule 4:12 to sanction a party who fails to comply with discovery obligations or who is found to have provided false responses. Sanctions can include fee awards, adverse inferences, and in serious cases, striking pleadings or entering default.

**Adverse inference.** A court may instruct that the hidden asset be valued against the hiding spouse — that is, the court assumes the most favorable value for the other party when the hiding spouse's conduct has made accurate valuation impossible.

**Equitable distribution adjustment.** Virginia Code § 20-107.3 gives courts broad authority in equitable distribution. A court that finds one spouse concealed assets may weigh that conduct in dividing the marital estate, awarding a larger share to the other spouse.

**Criminal exposure.** Perjury in a deposition or in written discovery responses under oath is a criminal offense. In cases of systematic, documented concealment, a referral to the Commonwealth's Attorney is possible, though uncommon.

## Practical Steps

If you suspect your spouse is hiding assets:

1. **Preserve documents now.** Photograph or scan anything you have access to — tax returns, account statements, business documents. Once litigation begins, access to records you previously could see may be restricted.
2. **Retain an attorney experienced in financial discovery.** Not all divorce attorneys conduct aggressive financial discovery. If your case involves business ownership, significant assets, or a spouse with complex finances, you need someone who has done this before.
3. **Consider a forensic accountant early.** If there is a business involved, retain a forensic accountant before the scheduling order's expert designation deadline.
4. **Use discovery systematically.** Serve broad requests early, follow up on evasive responses, and use subpoenas to third parties when direct requests are stonewalled.
5. **Track inconsistencies.** Keep a log of statements your spouse has made — in writing, in deposition, in financial documents — that contradict each other. Inconsistency is evidence of concealment.

The discovery process is designed to surface the truth. A spouse who has hidden assets can delay, complicate, and obstruct — but they cannot make documents disappear from institutions they do not control, and they cannot unsay what they said under oath in a deposition. Persistence and the right professional support are the most effective tools available.
