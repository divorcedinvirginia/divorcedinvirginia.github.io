Navigating a divorce in Virginia can feel overwhelming, especially if you are unfamiliar with how the state's legal system handles the end of a marriage. This post covers the foundational concepts you will encounter from the moment you consider filing through the final decree.

## Virginia Is an Equitable Distribution State

Virginia does not automatically split marital property 50/50. Instead, courts divide property **equitably** — meaning fairly, based on the circumstances of the marriage. Equitable often lands close to equal, but factors like the length of the marriage, each spouse's financial contributions, and non-monetary contributions (such as raising children or supporting a career) can all shift the outcome.

## Grounds for Divorce Matter

Virginia allows both no-fault and fault-based divorce. The most common path is **no-fault separation**: if you and your spouse have lived apart for at least one year (or six months with a separation agreement and no minor children), you can file without proving wrongdoing.

Fault grounds — adultery, cruelty, desertion, and felony conviction — still exist and can affect spousal support awards. Adultery, in particular, can bar a spouse from receiving spousal support entirely, though courts retain a narrow "manifest injustice" exception.

## Separation Is Not Automatic

In Virginia, the separation clock starts when at least one spouse intends the separation to be permanent **and** the spouses stop cohabiting. A single night apart does not restart the clock; courts look at the totality of the circumstances. If you reconcile and resume the marital relationship, the clock resets.

Document the date of separation clearly. Disputes over when separation began can delay your case significantly.

## Uncontested vs. Contested

If you and your spouse agree on everything — property, support, custody, and debt — you can pursue an **uncontested divorce**, which is faster and far less expensive. Many uncontested divorces in Virginia are finalized by affidavit without either party appearing in court.

A **contested divorce**, where spouses disagree on one or more issues, proceeds through discovery, possible mediation, and potentially a full trial. These cases routinely take one to three years and cost substantially more.
