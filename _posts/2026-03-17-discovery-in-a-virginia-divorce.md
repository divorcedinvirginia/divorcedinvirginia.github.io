If you ask a divorce attorney what part of a contested case is most likely to drag on longer than expected and cost more than planned, the answer is almost always the same: discovery.

Discovery is the formal process through which each party gathers information from the other — financial records, communications, employment documents, asset valuations, and anything else relevant to the issues being litigated. It sounds straightforward. In practice, it is frequently one of the most frustrating, expensive, and time-consuming phases of the entire divorce.

This post explains what discovery involves, why it is so difficult even when documents seem readily available, what to do when documents are not available, and what you should expect to pay.

---

## What Discovery Is and Why It Exists

In a contested Virginia divorce, neither side is required to simply take the other's word about finances, assets, income, or anything else. The discovery process gives both parties formal legal tools to demand information, compel production of documents, and get sworn testimony about facts that affect the outcome of the case.

The premise is sound. Divorces routinely involve hidden assets, overstated debts, underreported income, and mischaracterized property. Discovery is the mechanism that prevents one party from controlling the narrative through selective disclosure.

The reality is that even in cases where both parties are making a reasonable effort to cooperate, the discovery process is slow, expensive, and generates enormous amounts of friction.

## The Main Discovery Tools

### Interrogatories

Interrogatories are written questions that must be answered under oath within a set time period — typically 21 days in Virginia. They cover things like: list all bank accounts you have had in the last five years, list all real property you own, describe your employment and income for the last three years, identify all debts you currently owe.

The responding party answers in writing. Their attorney drafts the answers. The answers are signed under oath — meaning false answers can constitute perjury.

**The frustration:** Interrogatories that seem simple can produce answers that are incomplete, vague, or technically compliant but practically useless. An answer that says *"Respondent refers to documents to be produced"* when no documents have been produced yet is maddening and common. Getting complete, useful interrogatory answers often requires follow-up letters, meet-and-confer calls between attorneys, and sometimes a motion to compel.

### Requests for Production of Documents

This is a formal demand that the other party produce specific categories of documents: bank statements, tax returns, retirement account statements, credit card records, loan documents, business records, and anything else relevant to the case.

The receiving party has 21 days to respond. The response may be production of documents, objections to specific requests, or a combination.

**The frustration:** This is where the process becomes genuinely maddening. Even when the document in question is a bank statement that exists and is accessible, the responding party can object on grounds of relevance, scope, burdensomeness, or privilege. Each objection requires a response from your attorney. Negotiations over what will and will not be produced consume billable time at attorney rates.

Then the documents actually arrive — sometimes thousands of pages, sometimes in formats that are difficult to organize, sometimes with key pages missing. Reviewing document productions is itself a significant time and cost item.

### Depositions

A deposition is sworn testimony taken outside of court, usually in an attorney's office. The witness — your spouse, a business partner, an employer, a financial advisor, or anyone with relevant knowledge — is placed under oath and questioned by the opposing attorney, with their own attorney present.

Depositions are expensive: you pay your attorney to prepare for and attend, you may pay for a court reporter, and the resulting transcript costs additional money to obtain. A single deposition can cost $2,000–$5,000 or more when all expenses are included.

Depositions are also powerful. A deposition creates a sworn record that can be used to impeach a witness who changes their story at trial. They are most valuable in cases involving business income, hidden assets, or witnesses whose credibility is central to the outcome.

**The frustration:** Scheduling depositions in contested divorces is an exercise in patience. Attorney schedules, witness availability, and the strategic game of delay make booking depositions weeks or months after they are first requested common. A party that does not want to be deposed has many tools available to delay the process without technically refusing.

### Subpoenas

When the information you need is not in your spouse's possession — or when you do not trust your spouse to produce it — you can subpoena it directly from the source. Banks, employers, brokerages, mortgage companies, pension administrators, and even social media companies can be served with subpoenas requiring them to produce records.

Third-party subpoenas bypass your spouse entirely. A bank does not have the same incentive to delay or object that your spouse does.

**The frustration:** Third parties take time. Financial institutions typically take two to six weeks to respond to subpoenas. Some require a court order before they will produce records. Some charge fees for document retrieval. If the institution is out of state, additional process requirements may apply.

### Requests for Admission

These are formal requests that the other party admit or deny specific factual statements: *"Admit that you opened the account ending in XXXX on or about March 1, 2022."* Admissions narrow the issues at trial by establishing facts that are not genuinely in dispute.

They are less commonly used in divorce cases than interrogatories and document requests, but are useful for pinning down facts that the other side might otherwise try to deny or qualify at trial.

## Why Discovery Is Frustrating Even When Documents Seem Easy to Get

This is the part that surprises most people. You know the bank account exists. You know it is at a major bank. You know approximately what is in it. Why is it taking six months to get the statements?

### The Attorneys Are the Bottleneck — By Necessity

Every discovery request in a contested divorce goes through attorneys. Your attorney drafts the request. The other attorney reviews it, prepares objections, negotiates the scope, and supervises the response. Both attorneys communicate about disputes through letters and calls that are billed by the hour.

There is no shortcut to this. Contested divorces require that all communications go through counsel. Every time you think *"I could just call and ask"* — you cannot, and even if you did, anything said outside of the formal discovery process is not admissible in the same way.

### Objections Are Easy to Make and Hard to Overcome Quickly

A responding party can object to any discovery request on multiple grounds. Even if the objection will ultimately be overruled — by a court, if it comes to that — the time it takes to negotiate or litigate the objection adds weeks or months to the process.

Common objections that slow things down even when they are not ultimately sustained:
- *"This request is overly broad and unduly burdensome."* (Meaning: we do not want to produce this.)
- *"This request seeks information not relevant to any issue in this case."* (Often argued even when relevance is obvious.)
- *"This request seeks information protected by attorney-client privilege."* (Sometimes legitimate; sometimes a placeholder to slow production.)
- *"Responding party is in the process of gathering responsive documents."* (A holding pattern that can last for months.)

### People Cannot Find Their Own Documents

This sounds implausible, but it is extremely common. People who have not organized their financial records — which is most people — genuinely cannot locate years of statements for accounts they used casually and never thought to save records for.

Reconstructing financial history from scratch takes time. Your spouse may need to contact multiple banks, brokerage firms, and financial institutions to obtain records they do not have on hand. Each institution has its own process for producing historical records, and some charge per-page fees or require formal authorization.

### Electronic Records Create Their Own Problems

The era of paperless statements has made document production simultaneously more comprehensive and more chaotic. Records that exist only in online banking portals may require screenshots or downloads in formats that are difficult to produce in discovery. Email records can be enormous — tens of thousands of messages, most of them irrelevant, all of which need to be reviewed before production.

Some attorneys lack the technical infrastructure to efficiently manage electronic document production. The result is that document productions arrive in disorganized formats that require significant time to review and analyze.

### Strategic Delay Is Real

In contested divorces, information is power. A party who controls the timing of financial disclosure has an advantage — they know what you have, while you are still waiting to find out what they have. Some parties and their attorneys use every available procedural tool to slow down document production: maximum use of objection periods, requests for extensions, staged productions that provide some documents while withholding others, and technical compliance that falls short of genuine disclosure.

Identifying strategic delay for what it is — and deciding when to file a motion to compel versus when to continue negotiating — is one of the judgment calls where an experienced attorney earns their fee.

## When You Do Not Have Access to Documents

This is a common and serious problem. Many marriages involve a significant information asymmetry: one spouse manages the finances, while the other has little visibility into accounts, assets, and debts. When the more financially informed spouse controls document production, the less informed spouse starts the discovery process at a severe disadvantage.

Here is what to do.

### Start With What You Know or Can Access

Before anything goes through the formal discovery process, gather every document you can access on your own:

- Online banking portals — download statements going back as far as the system allows, which is often two to five years
- Tax returns — if you filed jointly, you are entitled to copies; request them from the IRS using Form 4506-C if you cannot access them directly
- Credit reports — pull your own credit report (and your spouse's, if you have their Social Security number and their consent is not required under the circumstances) through AnnualCreditReport.com to identify accounts you may not know about
- Mortgage statements, property tax records (available through your county assessor), vehicle registrations
- Retirement account statements — if you are the participant, log in and download; if you are the spouse, your attorney will need to obtain these through formal process
- Business records if your spouse owns a business and you have had any access to the business finances

Gathering what you can independently is faster and cheaper than waiting for discovery to produce it.

### Use Subpoenas to Go Around Your Spouse

If your spouse has not produced records you know exist — or if you suspect there are accounts you do not know about — subpoena the sources directly. Banks, brokerages, mortgage servicers, and employers will respond to properly issued subpoenas without going through your spouse at all.

For accounts you do not know about, subpoena financial institutions where you suspect activity. Your attorney can also request bank records from your spouse's known institutions going back five to seven years, which may reveal transfers to unknown accounts.

### Hire a Forensic Accountant

If your spouse is self-employed, owns a business, receives cash income, or has complex finances, a forensic accountant is not a luxury — it is a necessity. Forensic accountants are trained to reconstruct financial history from incomplete records, identify signs of asset concealment, trace funds through multiple accounts, and value businesses using methodologies that will hold up in court.

A forensic accountant will typically:
- Analyze tax returns for signs of underreported income (business owners who show low income on their returns sometimes live far better than those returns would suggest)
- Review bank statements for patterns of large cash withdrawals, transfers to unidentified accounts, or payments to individuals or entities that do not have obvious business purposes
- Identify unusual asset transfers in the period leading up to the divorce
- Calculate lifestyle analysis — comparing stated income to documented spending to identify the gap

Forensic accountants are expensive — expect $200–$500 per hour and retainers of $5,000–$15,000 or more depending on the complexity of the case. In a case involving a business or suspected hidden assets, they are almost always worth it.

### Request a Commissioner in Chancery

In Virginia, a Commissioner in Chancery is a court-appointed attorney who can take evidence, hear testimony, and make recommendations to the court on financial issues. If your spouse is stonewalling discovery and you cannot get the information you need through normal channels, your attorney can ask the court to appoint a Commissioner and require both parties to produce information directly to the Commissioner's office.

The Commissioner's involvement changes the dynamic — producing incomplete or misleading information to a court-appointed officer has direct and immediate consequences that producing the same information to opposing counsel does not.

## What Discovery Costs

There is no way to give a precise number because discovery costs vary enormously based on case complexity, the level of cooperation between the parties, and how contested each issue is. But here is a realistic picture of what the components cost.

### Attorney Time

Discovery is attorney-intensive. Drafting interrogatories, requests for production, and subpoenas; reviewing and responding to the other side's requests; negotiating objections; reviewing document productions; preparing for and attending depositions; and filing motions to compel when necessary all occur at your attorney's hourly rate.

In a moderately complex contested divorce, it is not unusual for discovery-related attorney fees alone to reach **$10,000–$25,000** per side. In high-asset cases with business interests, suspected hidden assets, or a highly uncooperative opposing party, discovery costs can reach **$50,000–$100,000** or more.

### Court Reporter and Deposition Transcript Costs

A court reporter for a deposition typically costs **$500–$800** for attendance plus a per-page fee for the transcript (often $4–$6 per page). A full-day deposition of a complex financial witness can produce a transcript of 200–400 pages. Expect **$1,500–$3,500** per deposition in transcript costs alone.

### Subpoena Response Fees

Financial institutions often charge for producing records in response to subpoenas. Fees of **$25–$200** per institution are common; some charge per page on top of a flat fee. If you are subpoenaing ten institutions, budget accordingly.

### Expert Witnesses

If discovery reveals the need for expert analysis — business valuation, forensic accounting, real estate appraisal, pension valuation — each expert brings their own cost structure. Forensic accountants and business valuators often charge **$5,000–$30,000** or more depending on the engagement. These costs are in addition to attorney fees.

### Motions to Compel

When the other side refuses to produce documents or provides inadequate responses to interrogatories, your attorney must file a motion to compel. This requires preparing a motion, filing it, potentially attending a hearing, and awaiting the court's ruling. Each motion to compel can cost **$1,500–$5,000** in attorney fees, plus court costs.

If the court grants the motion and finds that the non-producing party acted without substantial justification, the court can award attorney's fees for the motion — which provides some recovery, but only after the expense has already been incurred.

## How to Manage Discovery Costs

Discovery costs cannot be eliminated in a contested case, but they can be managed.

**Be organized.** Every hour your attorney spends sorting through documents you produced in disarray is billed to you. Organize your records chronologically, label them clearly, and provide a summary of what is included. Your attorney's document review time drops significantly when you do the organizational work in advance.

**Respond completely the first time.** Incomplete responses to the other side's requests generate follow-up letters, motions to compel, and court hearings — all at billable rates. Producing complete, well-organized responses to document requests upfront is almost always cheaper than the process of being compelled to do so.

**Prioritize what actually matters.** Not every discovery fight is worth having. An experienced attorney will help you identify which documents and issues are worth fighting for and which would cost more to litigate than they are worth to obtain. Spending $10,000 in attorney fees to prove that your spouse has a $3,000 hidden account is not a good use of your resources.

**Consider whether the case can settle.** The most effective way to control discovery costs is to resolve the case before discovery is complete. Many contested divorces settle during or shortly after the discovery phase, once both sides have enough information to assess the realistic range of outcomes. If the financial picture is becoming clear enough to negotiate intelligently, seriously consider whether settlement makes more sense than continuing to litigate.

---

Discovery is unglamorous, expensive, and often the thing that turns a "this shouldn't take too long" case into a multi-year ordeal. Understanding what you are dealing with — and approaching it strategically rather than reactively — is the only way to get through it without spending more than you have to.
