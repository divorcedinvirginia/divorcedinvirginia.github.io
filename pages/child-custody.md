# Child Custody and Visitation in Virginia

Few issues in a divorce carry higher stakes than the custody of children. The decisions made — by the parties or by the court — shape where children live, who makes decisions for them, and how much time they spend with each parent. Understanding how Virginia courts approach these decisions, what factors they weight most heavily, and where the real dangers lie in contested custody cases is essential for any parent navigating this process.

## Types of Custody

Virginia law distinguishes between two dimensions of custody that are decided independently: **legal custody** and **physical custody**.

### Legal Custody

Legal custody is the right and responsibility to make major decisions in a child's life, including:
- Education (school selection, tutoring, special education)
- Healthcare (medical treatment, mental health care, surgery decisions)
- Religious upbringing
- Extracurricular activities
- Passport and travel decisions

**Sole legal custody** means one parent has final decision-making authority on all major issues. The other parent may be consulted but does not have a legal right to jointly decide.

**Joint legal custody** means both parents share decision-making authority. Major decisions require agreement, or — when agreement is impossible — a return to court. Joint legal custody is the strong default in Virginia courts; it is ordered in the vast majority of cases unless one parent is demonstrated to be unfit, uninvolved, or so hostile to the other parent that shared decision-making is unworkable.

**What joint legal custody does not mean:** It does not mean both parents must agree on every routine daily decision. Day-to-day decisions (what the child eats, bedtime routines, homework during parenting time) are made by the parent who has the child at that moment. Joint legal custody applies only to major, long-term decisions.

---

### Physical Custody

Physical custody governs where the child lives and the day-to-day schedule.

**Primary physical custody** (sometimes called sole physical custody) means the child lives primarily with one parent — the custodial parent — and has scheduled visitation with the other — the non-custodial parent. In Virginia, a parent with fewer than 90 days per year of parenting time is typically considered a non-custodial parent for child support calculation purposes.

**Shared physical custody** means the child spends significant time with both parents — at least 90 days per year with each under Virginia's child support guidelines (Virginia Code § 20-108.2). True shared custody, where time is divided close to equally, is increasingly common.

**Split custody** — where siblings are divided between parents — is rare and generally disfavored. Courts prefer to keep siblings together absent compelling circumstances.

## How Courts Determine the Best Interests of the Child

Virginia Code § 20-124.3 requires courts to decide all custody and visitation matters based on the **best interests of the child**. The statute lists ten specific factors courts must consider. No single factor is controlling; courts weigh all of them based on the evidence.

### The Statutory Factors

**1. The age and physical and mental condition of the child**

Very young children (infants and toddlers) sometimes see different scheduling considerations than older children, though Virginia courts have moved away from rigid "tender years" presumptions. A child's developmental stage matters in designing a parenting schedule that minimizes disruption and supports healthy attachment.

**2. The age and physical and mental condition of each parent**

A parent's health, cognitive function, emotional stability, and physical ability to care for the child are all relevant. A parent with serious untreated mental illness, active substance abuse, or significant physical limitations affecting parenting capacity will face scrutiny. Courts are not looking for perfect parents — they are looking for parents capable of meeting the child's needs.

**3. The relationship existing between each parent and each child**

This is often the most important factor. Courts look at the depth and quality of the bond between each parent and the child: who has been the primary caregiver, who attends medical appointments, who participates in school activities, who is emotionally attuned to the child's needs. A parent who has been largely absent during the marriage will struggle to obtain significant parenting time without demonstrating a commitment to change.

**4. The needs of the child, giving due consideration to other important relationships of the child, including but not limited to siblings, peers, and extended family**

Courts consider the child's existing relationships with siblings, grandparents, cousins, and close family friends. An arrangement that severs a child from a deep-rooted extended family network may be disfavored. Sibling relationships are given significant weight — courts are reluctant to separate siblings without compelling reason.

**5. The role that each parent has played and will play in the future, in the upbringing and care of the child**

Past role informs predicted future role. A parent who primarily handled education, healthcare, and emotional support during the marriage is likely to continue doing so. Courts look at who made school pickups, attended pediatric appointments, managed medications, helped with homework, and provided emotional support during difficult times. Documentary evidence — school records, medical appointment logs, communication with teachers — can be decisive here.

**6. The propensity of each parent to actively support the child's contact and relationship with the other parent, including whether a parent has unreasonably denied the other parent access to or visitation with the child**

This factor is critical and frequently underestimated by litigants. Virginia courts take co-parenting cooperation seriously. A parent who interferes with the other parent's relationship with the child — by withholding parenting time, alienating the child, refusing to communicate about the child's needs, or making the child a messenger or spy — is viewed very negatively. Conversely, a parent who generously facilitates the other parent's relationship, even under difficult circumstances, is viewed favorably.

Unreasonably denying court-ordered parenting time is contempt of court and can result in a modification of custody against the denying parent.

**7. The relative willingness and demonstrated ability of each parent to maintain a close and continuing relationship with the child, and the ability of each parent to cooperate in and resolve disputes regarding matters affecting the child**

Courts assess each parent's ability to communicate with the other parent in a business-like, child-focused manner. High conflict, hostility, and inability to co-parent are factors that courts weigh heavily — and not always against the parent who is being antagonized. Parents who escalate conflict, file repeated motions over minor issues, or make every interaction a battle impose costs on their children that courts notice.

**8. The reasonable preference of the child, if the court deems the child to be of reasonable intelligence, understanding, age, and experience to express such a preference**

Virginia does not set a specific age at which a child's preference becomes determinative. Courts consider the child's maturity, the reasons behind the preference, and whether the preference appears to be genuine or coached. Teenage children's preferences carry more weight than those of younger children, but no child — regardless of age — has the right to choose where they live. A 16-year-old's strong, reasoned preference will be taken seriously; it will not be automatically dispositive.

Courts are also alert to children who express a preference that appears to have been shaped by one parent. A child who repeats talking points, uses adult legal language, or parrots a parent's grievances is often more evidence of parental influence than of genuine preference.

**9. Any history of family abuse as defined in § 16.1-228 or sexual abuse**

This factor is given significant weight. Documented domestic violence — physical abuse, sexual abuse, emotional abuse — is a serious consideration. Virginia Code § 20-124.3 specifically provides that any evidence of abuse is a factor courts must weigh. A history of family abuse creates a rebuttable presumption in some proceedings that sole custody should be awarded to the non-abusive parent. Courts also look at whether a parent has failed to protect the child from abuse by a third party.

**10. Such other factors as the court deems necessary and proper to the determination**

This catch-all allows courts to consider circumstances not specifically enumerated — substance abuse not already addressed, criminal history, work schedules, housing stability, the child's attachment to school and community, and any other facts the court finds relevant.

## The Role of the Status Quo

Perhaps the most practically powerful concept in Virginia custody litigation is the **status quo** — the existing pattern of care and residence that develops during the separation period, before any final custody order is entered.

### Why status quo matters so much

Courts are reluctant to disrupt arrangements that appear to be working for the children. The reasoning is rooted in child development: stability, routine, and predictability are protective factors for children experiencing the stress of family dissolution. A child who has been living primarily with one parent, attending a particular school, and maintaining an established social network will not be uprooted by a court without a substantial reason.

This creates a powerful dynamic: the parenting arrangement that becomes established during the separation period — even informally — tends to become the baseline around which final orders are built.

### The separation period as the critical window

The months between physical separation and the final custody order are when the status quo forms. During this period:

- The parent who assumes primary day-to-day care tends to be favored in final orders
- The parent who maintains consistent, active involvement — even in a non-primary role — preserves their position
- The parent who steps back, travels frequently, or cedes parenting responsibilities to the other parent may find it difficult to recapture those responsibilities later

**Practical consequence:** If you are a parent who has shared parenting responsibilities during the marriage and you are now separating, do not allow an informal arrangement to develop that does not reflect the role you want to maintain. A few months of a primary-caregiver dynamic can be difficult to reverse.

### Pendente lite orders and the status quo

The temporary custody order entered at the pendente lite stage often becomes enormously influential on the final order — not because it is legally controlling, but because it creates the status quo that will exist for months or years while the case is litigated. A parent who obtains primary custody at the pendente lite stage has a significant structural advantage in the final case.

This is why pendente lite custody hearings matter far more than their temporary nature might suggest.

### What can overcome the status quo

Status quo is not insurmountable, but overcoming it requires evidence of changed circumstances or demonstrated harm:
- Evidence that the existing arrangement is actively harming the children
- A significant change in either parent's circumstances
- Evidence of abuse, neglect, or substance abuse that was not previously before the court
- A court-ordered custody evaluation that recommends a different arrangement

Inertia alone — "things have been this way for a while" — is not enough to lock in an arrangement permanently. But changing from an established status quo requires meeting a higher burden than establishing an initial order.

## Parenting Plans and Visitation Schedules

When parents agree on custody and visitation, they submit a **parenting plan** — a written schedule that governs the day-to-day logistics of the custody arrangement. When they cannot agree, the court imposes one.

### Common physical custody schedules

**Week on / week off (50/50):** The child alternates weekly between each parent's home. Works well when parents live close to each other, have flexible work schedules, and can communicate effectively. Can be hard on younger children who struggle with week-long separations.

**2-2-3 rotation:** The child spends 2 days with Parent A, 2 days with Parent B, then 3 days with Parent A, rotating each week. Keeps transitions more frequent but avoids long separations. Often used for younger children in shared custody arrangements.

**Every other weekend (traditional non-custodial schedule):** The non-custodial parent has the child every other weekend (often Friday to Sunday), plus one weeknight per week. Results in roughly 20-25% parenting time for the non-custodial parent. Courts increasingly view this as insufficient for a child's relationship with both parents in cases where both parents are fit and involved.

**Extended holiday and summer schedules:** Regardless of the regular school-year schedule, most parenting plans include detailed provisions for holidays (Thanksgiving, Christmas/winter break, spring break, school breaks) and summer. Holiday schedules typically alternate year to year (Parent A has Christmas in odd years, Parent B in even years, etc.).

### What courts require in a parenting plan

Virginia Code § 20-124.2 encourages parents to submit a parenting plan. Most Virginia courts require parenting plans to address:
- Regular parenting schedule (school year and summer)
- Holiday and school break schedule
- Process for handling schedule changes
- Communication between parents about the child
- Transportation responsibilities for exchanges
- Decision-making process for joint legal custody decisions
- Notification requirements for travel, medical emergencies, and changes in address

## Guardian ad Litem

In contested custody cases, Virginia courts routinely appoint a **Guardian ad Litem (GAL)** — a licensed Virginia attorney appointed to represent the **best interests of the child** independently of either parent.

### What a GAL does

A GAL is not the child's personal attorney and does not simply advocate for what the child wants. Their role is broader: to investigate and present to the court what outcome would best serve the child's interests, based on an independent investigation.

A GAL typically:
- Interviews both parents
- Interviews the child (usually in a child-appropriate setting without either parent present)
- Speaks with teachers, coaches, therapists, pediatricians, and other people involved in the child's life
- Reviews relevant records (school records, medical records, mental health records, police reports)
- Observes parent-child interactions when appropriate
- Investigates any allegations of abuse, neglect, substance abuse, or domestic violence
- Files a written report with the court
- May call witnesses and present evidence at the custody hearing

### The GAL's weight in court

The GAL's recommendations carry significant — sometimes decisive — weight with the judge. Because the GAL has conducted an independent investigation and has no stake in the outcome, courts trust their assessment. A GAL who recommends against a parent's custody position is a serious obstacle.

**This means the GAL interview matters enormously.** How you present yourself to the GAL — your demeanor, your child-focused attitude, your willingness to support the other parent's relationship, your concrete knowledge of the child's life — will shape their report.

### What GALs watch for

GALs are trained to spot:
- Parents who are more focused on hurting the other parent than on the child's welfare
- Children who have been coached or pressured to take sides
- Inconsistencies between what a parent claims and what records and third parties show
- Children who are distressed, overly adult in their responses, or unusually aligned with one parent's narrative
- Signs of alienation, abuse, or neglect

## Custody Evaluations

In high-conflict cases, courts may order a **custody evaluation** by a licensed clinical psychologist or other mental health professional. This is a more intensive investigation than a GAL review.

A custody evaluation typically involves:
- Psychological testing of both parents (MMPI, parenting assessments, etc.)
- Clinical interviews with each parent separately
- Observations of each parent with the child
- Interviews with the child
- Collateral interviews with teachers, therapists, and other relevant people
- Review of all relevant records

The evaluator prepares a written report with findings and recommendations on custody and visitation. These reports are detailed, carry substantial evidential weight, and are very difficult to overcome at trial without strong contrary evidence.

**Custody evaluations are expensive** — often $5,000 to $15,000 or more — and time-consuming, often taking months to complete. They slow the litigation timeline but provide courts with expert guidance on complex family dynamics.

## Relocation of Children

Relocation — when a parent seeks to move with the children to another city, state, or country — is one of the most contested and emotionally charged issues in Virginia custody law.

### The legal standard

There is no absolute right to relocate with the children. When a parent seeks to move in a way that would materially affect the existing custody arrangement, the relocating parent must obtain either:
- **The other parent's written consent**, or
- **A court order** authorizing the relocation

The court applies the **best interests of the child** standard under Virginia Code § 20-124.3, considering all the statutory factors in light of the proposed move.

### Notice requirements

Virginia Code § 20-124.5 requires that a parent who intends to relocate with a child give the other parent **30 days written notice** before the move. Failure to provide notice — or moving without consent or court approval — can constitute contempt of court and may be held against the relocating parent in custody proceedings.

**If a move is imminent and unannounced**, the other parent can seek an emergency order from the court preventing the relocation before it occurs.

### What courts consider in relocation cases

Courts do not apply a presumption for or against relocation. They weigh the specific facts, including:

**The reason for the proposed move:** A relocation for a significant career opportunity, to be closer to extended family support, or to escape economic hardship is viewed more favorably than a move that appears designed primarily to distance the child from the other parent. Courts distinguish between legitimate life reasons and tactical moves.

**The impact on the child's relationship with the non-relocating parent:** This is often the decisive issue. If a move would make meaningful parenting time by the non-relocating parent impossible or impractical — particularly for young children who need frequent contact — courts weigh this heavily. A move from Fairfax to Richmond is different from a move from Fairfax to Seattle.

**The child's ties to the current community:** School relationships, friendships, extracurricular activities, extended family, and the child's overall rootedness in the current location are considered. A child with a decade of established community ties faces a more disruptive relocation than one who has recently moved.

**The quality of life for the child in the new location:** Courts consider what the child's life will look like after the move — the schools, the housing situation, the social environment, and the support network available.

**The feasibility of alternative visitation arrangements:** If the non-relocating parent could maintain a meaningful relationship through modified visitation (longer school breaks, extended summers, video contact), courts are more likely to allow relocation. If the non-relocating parent's work schedule or financial situation makes travel-based visitation impractical, courts weigh this against relocation.

**The child's preference:** For older children, the court considers whether the child wants to move and the reasons behind the preference.

### When courts deny relocation

Courts are increasingly willing to deny relocation requests — and in some cases, to award primary custody to the non-relocating parent — when:
- The proposed move would effectively eliminate the other parent's ability to maintain a meaningful relationship with the child
- The stated reason for the move appears pretextual or motivated by a desire to interfere with the other parent's relationship
- The child has deep roots in the current location and the move would be highly disruptive
- The relocating parent has a history of interfering with the other parent's relationship

**The risk of non-compliance:** A parent who relocates without consent or court approval, or in violation of a court order, faces serious consequences: a contempt finding, an order compelling return of the child, and potentially a modification of custody to the non-relocating parent. Courts treat unilateral relocation as a serious violation.

### International relocation

International relocation cases involve an additional layer of complexity under the **Hague Convention on the Civil Aspects of International Child Abduction**, to which the United States is a signatory. A parent who removes a child to another country without consent or court approval may be subject to international legal proceedings requiring return of the child. Virginia courts will not authorize international relocation lightly, and the other parent's consent — or a court order — is essential.

## Modification of Custody Orders

Final custody orders are not permanent. Either parent can seek modification when circumstances change.

### The legal standard for modification

Virginia Code § 20-124.2 requires that a parent seeking to modify custody demonstrate:

1. **A material change in circumstances** since the last custody order, and
2. That modification is in the **best interests of the child**

The material change must be significant and not something that was foreseeable when the original order was entered. Minor changes in routine, personality conflicts, or general dissatisfaction with the arrangement do not constitute a material change.

Examples of material changes courts have recognized:
- A significant change in a parent's work schedule or employment
- Relocation by either parent
- The child's changing developmental needs as they grow older
- Documented deterioration in one parent's ability to care for the child (substance abuse, mental health crisis, new domestic violence)
- A parent's remarriage or introduction of a new partner, in some circumstances
- A substantial change in the child's own needs or preferences (particularly for older teenagers)

### What does not constitute a material change

- Normal developmental changes in the child
- Minor schedule conflicts or communication difficulties
- Disagreements about parenting style that do not affect the child's wellbeing
- One parent simply wanting more time without a changed circumstance to justify it
- The child expressing a preference for change (without accompanying changed circumstances)

## High-Conflict Custody Cases: What to Be Wary Of

High-conflict custody litigation is emotionally exhausting, financially devastating, and deeply harmful to children. Certain patterns of behavior — some by parties, some by attorneys — make bad situations worse. Knowing what to watch for can protect you and your children.

### Parental alienation

**Parental alienation** refers to conduct by one parent that undermines the child's relationship with the other parent — ranging from subtle negative comments to active campaigns to turn the child against the other parent.

Warning signs that alienation may be occurring:
- The child suddenly and dramatically rejects a parent they previously had a good relationship with
- The child uses adult language or concepts beyond their developmental level when describing the other parent
- The child parrots the alienating parent's grievances word-for-word
- The child claims to have witnessed events that they could not realistically have witnessed or understood
- The child expresses extreme, black-and-white views of one parent as all good and the other as all bad
- The alienating parent repeatedly schedules activities during the other parent's parenting time
- The alienating parent refuses to send the child to parenting time claiming the child "doesn't want to go"

Virginia courts take alienation seriously. A parent who is found to be actively alienating the child from the other parent risks a modification of custody — and courts have awarded primary custody to the targeted parent in documented alienation cases.

**If you are the targeted parent:** Document everything. Keep records of missed parenting time, communications where alienating conduct is evident, and the child's statements (without coaching the child). Do not interrogate the child or pressure them to talk — get the child into therapy with a qualified child therapist and bring the evidence to the GAL and the court.

**If you are worried about being accused of alienation:** Be scrupulously careful about what you say about the other parent in the child's presence or earshot. Children absorb everything. Even comments you believe are subtle — sighs, eye-rolls, muttered remarks — register with children and can be reported to a GAL or therapist in damaging ways.

### Using children as messengers or informants

Asking children to carry messages between parents, report on the other parent's household, or monitor the other parent's activities puts children in an impossible position. It forces them to choose sides and creates loyalty conflicts that cause lasting psychological harm. Courts view this conduct extremely negatively. Use written communication directly with the other parent. Do not send messages through the child.

### Coaching children

Preparing a child to say specific things to a GAL, therapist, or judge — even subtly — is one of the most damaging things a parent can do and one of the easiest for professionals to detect. GALs and child psychologists are trained to identify coached responses. A child who delivers a prepared narrative, uses terminology beyond their age, or shifts to a rehearsed tone is a red flag that immediately damages the coaching parent's credibility.

Tell your child the truth in age-appropriate terms: there will be someone who talks to them about how things are going, they can say what they honestly think and feel, and there are no right or wrong answers. Leave it there.

### Frivolous allegations

In high-conflict cases, one or both parties sometimes make serious allegations — abuse, neglect, substance abuse, criminal conduct — that are exaggerated, distorted, or fabricated. Courts have seen this pattern many times.

False or exaggerated allegations create several serious risks:
- If the allegations are investigated and found to be unsupported, the alleging parent's credibility suffers catastrophically — and courts remember
- The child is subjected to investigations, interviews, and potential examinations that are themselves traumatic
- GALs and evaluators often become skeptical of all allegations from a parent who has made demonstrably false ones

**Make allegations when you genuinely believe they are true and you have evidence.** Do not make allegations for strategic advantage. And if you have genuine concerns about the child's safety, address them through the proper channels — report to Child Protective Services and bring them to the GAL's attention — rather than weaponizing them in litigation.

### Over-litigation

Some attorneys — and some parties — approach high-conflict custody cases as a war of attrition, filing motions on minor issues, demanding unnecessary discovery, seeking depositions of marginal witnesses, and scheduling hearings that accomplish little except generating fees and exhausting the other side.

The problem with over-litigation is that courts notice. A parent who files repeated motions over trivial matters, who cannot allow a scheduling variance without seeking judicial intervention, or whose pleadings read more like a personal vendetta than a child-focused legal argument signals to the court that they may be more interested in fighting than in parenting. Courts sometimes explicitly address over-litigation in their rulings — and not favorably.

Ask yourself, before filing any motion or escalating any dispute: Is this about my child, or is this about me?

### Social media

What you post on social media during a custody dispute can and will be used against you. Photos from a night out while you claimed financial hardship, posts expressing contempt for the other parent, images of alcohol or drug use, or statements about the case that violate court orders are all potential evidence.

Assume that everything you post is being monitored. The safest approach during active litigation is to go dark on social media entirely.

### Violating court orders

Violating a custody order — withholding parenting time, failing to make the child available for exchanges, taking the child out of the jurisdiction without permission — is contempt of court. Courts take it seriously, impose sanctions, and — in patterns of repeated violation — modify custody to protect the child's access to both parents.

Even if you believe the other parent is violating the order, the proper response is to document the violation, communicate your objection in writing, and bring the matter to the court. Self-help remedies — retaliating by withholding your own court-ordered exchanges — will backfire.

### Working with a therapist

Both parents — and the children — benefit from individual therapy during high-conflict custody litigation. A therapist provides a confidential space to process the emotional weight of the case, helps you manage the stress that inevitably affects your behavior and judgment, and can help you identify when your responses are being driven by anger or hurt rather than your child's best interests.

Courts view parents who are actively engaged in their own mental health support positively — it demonstrates self-awareness and commitment to being a better parent.

## Domestic Violence and Custody

Domestic violence has a direct and significant impact on custody determinations in Virginia.

### The statutory presumption

Virginia Code § 20-124.3 explicitly requires courts to consider any history of family abuse. Where abuse is established, Virginia law creates pressure toward protecting the abused spouse and children, including through:
- Protective orders that limit contact between the abusive parent and the child
- Supervised visitation requirements
- Conditions on parenting time (no overnight contact, no new partners present, completion of batterer intervention programs)
- In serious cases, denial of parenting time until safety can be assured

### Documenting abuse

If you have experienced domestic violence during the marriage or separation:
- Document incidents in real time — photographs, medical records, police reports, emergency room visits
- Save threatening texts, emails, and voicemails
- Keep a written log of incidents with dates, descriptions, and any witnesses
- Report serious incidents to law enforcement — a police report creates an official record
- Seek a protective order if you are in fear of ongoing harm
- Tell your attorney immediately and completely

Courts cannot act on abuse they do not know about. Documentation is essential.

### Supervised visitation

When a parent poses a credible risk to the child's safety but has not been shown to be so dangerous that all contact should be prohibited, courts may order **supervised visitation**: parenting time that occurs only in the presence of a designated supervisor — either a trusted third party or a professional visitation center.

Supervised visitation is intended as a protective measure, not a permanent arrangement. Courts typically include a path to unsupervised visitation when the concerning conduct has been addressed (e.g., completion of anger management, sustained sobriety, completion of a batterer intervention program).

### Protective orders and custody

An emergency protective order obtained due to domestic violence can immediately restrict the abusive parent's contact with the child. Protective orders are issued by juvenile and domestic relations courts and can be extended and made permanent after a hearing. Violation of a protective order is a criminal offense.

If there is active domestic violence in your case, a protective order and the associated custody restrictions may provide immediate protection while the divorce case proceeds.
