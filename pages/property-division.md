# Property Division in Virginia (Equitable Distribution)

When a Virginia marriage ends, the court must divide what the spouses accumulated together. Virginia follows the **equitable distribution** model — property is divided fairly, but not automatically equally. What looks like a straightforward process on paper becomes far more complex when dealing with retirement accounts, a shared home, tax consequences, commingled assets, and a spouse who has been financially deceptive.

## The Legal Framework

Virginia Code § 20-107.3 governs the classification and division of property in divorce. The statute sets out a three-step process courts follow:

1. **Identify and classify** all property as marital, separate, or hybrid
2. **Value** the marital property
3. **Divide** the marital property equitably, considering the statutory factors

Only **marital property** is subject to equitable distribution. Separate property belongs to the spouse who owns it and is not divided by the court. Hybrid property is divided between its marital and separate components.

Courts may award property to one spouse in kind, order a property sold and the proceeds divided, order one spouse to pay the other a monetary award to equalize the division, or a combination of all three.

## Classification of Property {#classification-of-property}

### Marital Property

Marital property is all property acquired by either or both spouses **during the marriage**, other than by gift or inheritance from a third party. The key is acquisition during the marriage — it does not matter whose name is on the account, the deed, or the title.

Common examples of marital property:
- Income earned by either spouse during the marriage
- Bank and investment accounts funded with marital earnings
- The marital home (if purchased during the marriage)
- Retirement account contributions made during the marriage
- Vehicles purchased during the marriage
- Household furnishings and personal property acquired during the marriage
- Businesses or business interests built during the marriage
- Increase in value of marital property during the marriage

**Joint debt** incurred during the marriage for marital purposes is also marital — both the assets and the liabilities accumulated during the marriage are subject to equitable distribution.

### Separate Property

Separate property belongs exclusively to one spouse and is not divided:

- Property owned before the marriage
- Property received as a **gift** from a third party (not the other spouse) during the marriage
- Property received as an **inheritance** during the marriage
- Property acquired after the date of separation using separate funds
- Increases in value of separate property, as long as that increase is not attributable to marital effort or marital funds

The burden of proving property is separate falls on the spouse claiming it. Without documentation — purchase receipts, account statements, inheritance paperwork — establishing separate property can be difficult, particularly for assets that have existed for many years.

### Hybrid (Part Marital, Part Separate) Property

Hybrid property has both a separate component and a marital component. This situation arises when:

- A home purchased before marriage has been paid down with marital income during the marriage. The equity that existed on the date of marriage may be separate; the equity built through marital mortgage payments may be marital.
- A retirement account existed before the marriage and continued to receive contributions during the marriage. Pre-marital contributions and their earnings may be separate; contributions made during the marriage may be marital.
- Separate funds were used to improve or pay for otherwise marital property.

The **coverture fraction** is a common method for apportioning hybrid retirement accounts: the marital portion is the fraction of the benefit earned during the marriage relative to the total benefit earned over the entire employment.

### The Commingling Problem

Separate property can lose its separate character through **commingling** — mixing it with marital funds to the point where it can no longer be traced. Classic examples:

- An inheritance deposited into a joint checking account that is regularly used for household expenses. After months or years of transactions, tracing the original inheritance through the account activity may be impossible.
- Separate funds used to purchase an asset that is later titled jointly.
- Separate funds deposited into a marital investment account and then actively traded.

Virginia courts follow the **tracing rule**: separate property retains its character if it can be traced with sufficient specificity. When tracing is impossible, the property is presumed marital. Careful record-keeping — keeping inherited funds in a dedicated separate account, documenting the source of major purchases — is the only reliable protection for separate property.

## When Property Is Valued

The **date of valuation** is a critical and often contested issue in Virginia equitable distribution cases. Virginia Code § 20-107.3(A) requires courts to use the **date of the evidentiary hearing** as the valuation date, unless the court finds another date would be more equitable under the circumstances.

### Why the valuation date matters

The value of assets fluctuates. A stock portfolio worth $500,000 on the date of separation may be worth $650,000 or $350,000 by the time of trial a year or two later. The marital home may have appreciated or declined. A business may have grown or contracted.

Whoever benefits from using a particular date will advocate for that date. If a marital asset has appreciated since separation, the spouse who did not contribute to that appreciation may prefer the earlier date; the spouse who managed the asset through that period may prefer the later date.

### Assets where valuation date is particularly contested

**The marital home:** Real estate values change over time. In a rising market, a long delay to trial can significantly increase the home's value, which affects both the equity calculation and the ultimate division. In a falling market, the opposite occurs.

**Retirement accounts:** Investment accounts fluctuate with market conditions. Both parties should be aware that using the trial date as the valuation date means market movements between separation and trial affect how much each party receives.

**Businesses:** Business valuations are not market-price transactions — they require expert appraisers who use methodologies (income approach, asset approach, market approach) that produce different results depending on when the business is valued and which methodology is applied. In contested business valuations, the parties' expert witnesses often produce dramatically different numbers, and the court must choose or split the difference.

**Defined benefit pensions:** Pension values are calculated based on the benefit accrued as of a specific date. The coverture fraction methodology is used to determine the marital share.

### Active versus passive appreciation

Virginia law distinguishes between **active appreciation** (resulting from marital effort or marital funds — treated as marital) and **passive appreciation** (resulting from market forces with no marital contribution — treated as separate). This distinction can significantly affect how appreciation in separate property is classified.

For example: If one spouse owned a vacation home before marriage, and that home appreciated in value during the marriage solely due to general real estate market trends, the appreciation may be separate property. If marital funds were used to renovate it, or if either spouse actively managed it as a rental property during the marriage, a portion of the appreciation may be marital.

## Factors Courts Use to Divide Property

Virginia Code § 20-107.3(E) lists the factors courts must consider when deciding how to divide marital property. Unlike some states, Virginia does not start from an equal 50/50 presumption — the court weighs the factors and divides equitably:

**1. The monetary and non-monetary contributions of each party to the well-being of the family**

Both financial contributions (income, paying bills, growing investments) and non-financial contributions (homemaking, childcare, supporting the other spouse's career, managing the household) are considered. A spouse who left the workforce to raise children is not penalized for that choice — their non-monetary contributions are explicitly recognized.

**2. The contributions, monetary and non-monetary, of each party in the acquisition and care of the marital property**

Who found the investment opportunity? Who managed the rental property? Who negotiated the business deal? Who maintained the home? Active contributions to specific assets are weighed in dividing those assets.

**3. The duration of the marriage**

Longer marriages generally produce outcomes closer to an equal division. In short marriages, courts are more likely to return each spouse closer to their pre-marital position.

**4. The ages and physical and mental condition of the parties**

A younger, healthier spouse has more ability to rebuild financially than an older spouse who has been out of the workforce for decades. Courts consider each party's capacity to generate future income and accumulate new assets.

**5. The circumstances and factors that contributed to the dissolution of the marriage, including any ground for divorce under Virginia Code § 20-91**

**Fault matters in property division.** A spouse who committed adultery, wasted marital assets in furtherance of an affair, or engaged in other misconduct may receive a smaller share of the marital estate. This is not automatic — courts must find that the fault conduct had economic consequences — but it is a real factor.

**6. How and when specific items of marital property were acquired**

Property acquired early in the marriage, purchased with joint savings, or built through joint effort over decades is weighted differently than property acquired shortly before separation or through one spouse's independent effort.

**7. The debts and liabilities of each spouse, the basis for such debts and liabilities, and the property which may serve as security for such debts or liabilities**

Both sides of the balance sheet — assets and debts — are considered. A spouse who ran up significant debt for personal expenditures that did not benefit the marriage may bear a disproportionate share of those obligations.

**8. The liquid or non-liquid character of all marital property**

A $500,000 brokerage account and $500,000 of equity in an illiquid closely-held business are not equivalent. Courts consider how easily property can be converted to cash, which affects what is practical to award to each party.

**9. The tax consequences to each party**

This is one of the most commonly overlooked factors and one of the most financially significant. Courts are required to consider the tax consequences of the proposed division — but only do so meaningfully when the parties put tax evidence before them. (See the dedicated section below.)

**10. The use or expenditure of marital property by either party for a non-marital separate purpose or the dissipation of such funds, when such was done in anticipation of divorce or after the last separation**

Dissipation of marital assets — spending marital money on an affair partner, gambling away savings, making unilateral large gifts to family members — is one of the most impactful factors in property division. Courts can reconstruct what the estate would have been but for the dissipation and award the dissipating spouse a smaller share accordingly.

**11. Any other factors necessary to do equity and right justice between the parties**

The catch-all factor allows courts to consider circumstances not enumerated above.

## The Marital Home

For most couples, the marital home is the largest single marital asset. Its disposition is often the most emotionally charged decision in the divorce.

### Option 1: One Spouse Keeps the Home

The spouse who keeps the home must typically:
- **Refinance the mortgage** to remove the other spouse from personal liability on the loan. Lenders will not simply remove a borrower's name from an existing mortgage — a new loan in only the keeping spouse's name is required.
- **Pay a buyout** to the other spouse equal to their share of the equity. Equity is the current market value minus the outstanding mortgage balance and costs of sale.
- **Qualify financially.** A spouse who cannot qualify for a mortgage on their own income cannot keep the home unless they can pay cash for the other's equity interest.

**The equity calculation:**
- Market value (established by appraisal or agreement)
- Minus: outstanding mortgage balance
- Minus: estimated costs of sale (real estate commission, transfer taxes, closing costs — typically 6-8% of value)
- Equals: net equity
- Each spouse's share of net equity depends on the equitable distribution factors

**When keeping the home makes sense:**
- Children are in school and stability of location is important to their wellbeing
- The keeping spouse can qualify for the refinanced mortgage
- The keeping spouse genuinely wants to remain in the home long-term
- The home has appreciated significantly and keeping it is a sound financial investment

**When keeping the home does not make sense:**
- The keeping spouse cannot actually afford the mortgage, taxes, insurance, and maintenance on one income
- The home requires significant deferred maintenance that will further strain the keeping spouse's finances
- The keeping spouse is emotionally attached to the home in a way that is clouding financial judgment — a house is an asset, not a refuge

Courts have seen many cases where a spouse fought desperately to keep the home, refinanced at a worse rate on a single income, then was forced into foreclosure two years later. Judges sometimes push back on home-keeping requests precisely because they know the financial reality often does not support it.

### Option 2: The Home Is Sold

When the parties cannot agree on who keeps the home, or when neither can afford it independently, the home is sold and the net proceeds are divided.

**Deferred sale** — particularly in cases with minor children — is sometimes ordered. The court may allow the custodial parent to remain in the home with the children until a trigger event (youngest child turns 18, custodial parent remarries, custodial parent voluntarily moves out), at which point the home is sold and proceeds divided. This arrangement is called a **tenancy in common** with a deferred sale and requires careful drafting to address who pays the mortgage, taxes, maintenance, and insurance in the interim, and how those costs affect the eventual equity split.

**Court-ordered sale** — When a party refuses to cooperate with a sale ordered by the court (refuses to sign listing agreements, interferes with showings, refuses to sign the deed at closing), courts can hold the non-complying party in contempt and in some cases appoint a **Special Commissioner** to execute the deed on that party's behalf.

### Option 3: One Spouse Retains the Home Without Refinancing (Risky)

Sometimes parties agree that one spouse keeps the home but the mortgage remains in both names while the keeping spouse makes payments. This is highly risky for the departing spouse:

- They remain personally liable on the mortgage
- Non-payment by the keeping spouse will damage their credit
- Their debt-to-income ratio includes the mortgage, affecting their ability to obtain new financing
- If the keeping spouse stops paying and the home goes into foreclosure, both parties' credit is destroyed

Lenders are not bound by divorce decrees. A lender who holds a mortgage signed by both spouses can pursue both spouses for deficiency after foreclosure, regardless of what the divorce decree says about who was responsible for the mortgage.

**If you are the departing spouse, do not agree to an arrangement where the mortgage remains in your name unless you have a clear timeline and mechanism for refinancing — and you have recourse if it does not happen.**

## Retirement Accounts and Pensions

Retirement assets accumulated during the marriage are marital property subject to equitable distribution. They are among the most valuable and most complex assets in many divorces.

### Defined contribution accounts (401k, 403b, IRA, TSP)

The **marital portion** of a defined contribution account is the contributions made during the marriage, plus investment earnings on those contributions. Pre-marital contributions and their earnings are separate property.

Division of an employer-sponsored retirement account (401k, 403b, TSP) requires a **Qualified Domestic Relations Order (QDRO)** — a separate court order directed to the plan administrator that authorizes division of the account. A QDRO is not part of the divorce decree; it must be drafted separately, approved by the plan administrator, and submitted after the divorce is final.

**IRAs** do not require a QDRO — they are divided through a **transfer incident to divorce** under a court order or written separation agreement. The transfer must be structured correctly to avoid triggering a taxable distribution.

**Critical point:** A QDRO that is never drafted, drafted incorrectly, or never submitted to the plan administrator means the division of the retirement account never actually happens. Attorneys who handle divorces involving retirement accounts should ensure the QDRO is prepared, approved, and submitted promptly after the divorce is finalized. Years of delay can cause significant complications.

### Defined benefit pensions

A defined benefit pension pays a monthly benefit at retirement based on years of service and salary. The marital portion is the portion of the benefit earned during the marriage — calculated using the coverture fraction.

Division options for pensions include:
- **Deferred distribution:** The non-employee spouse receives their share of the benefit when the employee actually retires, paid by the pension plan directly. This requires a QDRO and means the non-employee spouse waits until the employee retires.
- **Present value offset:** The present value of the pension's marital portion is calculated by an actuary, and the employee spouse keeps the entire pension while the non-employee spouse receives other marital assets of equivalent present value. This avoids the deferred distribution structure but requires an accurate actuarial valuation.

Military pensions are subject to the **Uniformed Services Former Spouses' Protection Act (USFSPA)**, which has its own rules: the non-military spouse is entitled to a share of disposable retired pay, with direct payment from DFAS available if the marriage overlapped with at least 10 years of creditable service (the 10/10 rule).

## Businesses and Professional Practices

A business or professional practice (law firm, medical practice, accounting firm) built during the marriage is marital property. Valuing it is among the most contested issues in Virginia divorce litigation.

### Valuation methodologies

**Income approach:** Values the business based on its capacity to generate income. An appraiser projects future earnings, applies a capitalization rate or discount rate, and arrives at a present value.

**Asset approach:** Values the business based on its net assets — what it owns minus what it owes.

**Market approach:** Values the business by comparing it to similar businesses that have recently sold.

Different methodologies often produce dramatically different values. Opposing expert witnesses routinely disagree by millions of dollars in high-asset cases. The court must weigh the methodologies and the experts' credibility.

### Goodwill: Enterprise vs. Personal

Virginia courts distinguish between **enterprise goodwill** (the value of the business that would survive if the owner-spouse left — customer relationships, brand, systems, location) and **personal goodwill** (value attributable solely to the owner-spouse's personal reputation, relationships, and skills that would not survive their departure).

Enterprise goodwill is marital property subject to division. Personal goodwill is not — it belongs to the individual, cannot be divided, and will not survive a sale if the owner-spouse is not retained.

The distinction between enterprise and personal goodwill is one of the most litigated issues in business valuation divorces. A solo physician's practice may have almost entirely personal goodwill; a regional construction company with multiple managers and an established client base may have substantial enterprise goodwill.

## Tax Consequences of Property Division

Virginia Code § 20-107.3(E)(9) requires courts to consider the tax consequences of property division. In practice, parties and their attorneys must proactively present tax evidence — the court will not perform independent tax analysis.

### Why tax consequences matter: a $500,000 example

Suppose the marital estate consists of two assets each worth $500,000:
- The marital home (purchased for $200,000, now worth $500,000)
- A taxable brokerage account (original basis $50,000, now worth $500,000)

An equal division — one spouse takes the home, the other takes the brokerage account — is not actually equal after taxes:

**The home:** The keeping spouse can exclude up to $250,000 of capital gains ($500,000 for a couple) from sale of a primary residence under IRC § 121, if they have lived there for 2 of the last 5 years. If the keeping spouse sells for $500,000 with a $200,000 basis, $300,000 of gain is realized — $250,000 is excluded, leaving $50,000 of taxable gain. Tax cost: roughly $7,500–$15,000 (depending on rate).

**The brokerage account:** The receiving spouse has a $50,000 basis in a $500,000 account — $450,000 of embedded capital gain. If they liquidate, they owe capital gains tax on $450,000. At a combined federal/state rate of 27-30% (including the 3.8% net investment income tax for higher earners), the tax cost could exceed $120,000.

The spouse who received the "equal" brokerage account actually received an asset worth $120,000+ less after taxes. **Equal asset values do not mean equal after-tax values.**

### Common tax issues in property division

**Capital gains on investment accounts:** The embedded gain (unrealized appreciation) in taxable investment accounts represents a future tax liability. When dividing accounts, spouses should try to ensure each receives a proportionate share of embedded gain — or the division should account for the difference in after-tax value.

**Retirement account taxation:** Traditional 401k and IRA distributions are taxed as ordinary income when withdrawn. Roth accounts are distributed tax-free. A $500,000 traditional IRA and a $500,000 Roth IRA are not equivalent — the traditional IRA carries a substantial future tax liability. A rough adjustment for the present value of the tax liability should inform how these are divided.

**The home exclusion and timing:** A spouse who is leaving the marital home and has not lived there for 2 of the last 5 years is at risk of losing the IRC § 121 exclusion if the sale is delayed. If the divorce takes two years and the leaving spouse has been gone for more than three years before the home is sold, they may lose the exclusion on their share of the gain. **The divorce decree or separation agreement should address this specifically** — often the settlement includes language preserving the departing spouse's right to the exclusion.

**Interspousal transfers:** Property transferred between spouses incident to divorce is **not a taxable event** under IRC § 1041. The receiving spouse takes the property at the transferring spouse's original basis. This means the embedded gain does not disappear — it transfers. The receiving spouse will owe tax on the full gain when they eventually sell, even though they did not own the asset during the appreciation period. This is why basis matters in any property division involving appreciated assets.

**Deferred compensation:** Stock options, restricted stock units (RSUs), and deferred compensation plans that were earned during the marriage but not yet paid or vested are marital property to the extent they were earned during the marriage. Their value and tax treatment are complex and require careful attention.

**The qualified opportunity zone and depreciation recapture:** For couples with real estate holdings, depreciation recapture (taxed at 25% federal rate, not the preferential capital gains rate) and any deferred gain in opportunity zone investments require specific tax analysis.

**Practical advice:** Before finalizing any property division involving significant assets, have a CPA or tax attorney model the after-tax value of each proposed division. The negotiating leverage that comes from understanding the actual after-tax outcomes often more than pays for the professional fees.

## Division of Debt

Virginia courts divide both assets and debts. The same equitable distribution principles apply to marital debt as to marital property.

### Marital vs. separate debt

**Marital debt** is debt incurred during the marriage for marital purposes — the mortgage, car loans, credit cards used for household expenses, medical bills, home improvement loans.

**Separate debt** is debt incurred before the marriage, or during the marriage for entirely personal and non-marital purposes, by one spouse.

The classification of debt follows similar principles to the classification of property: when was it incurred, by whom, and for what purpose?

### How courts divide marital debt

Courts generally assign debt to the spouse who received the corresponding benefit, who is better positioned to pay it, or who can be held most directly accountable for it:

- The mortgage is typically assigned to whoever keeps the home
- Vehicle debt goes with the vehicle
- Joint credit card debt may be divided in proportion to who incurred what charges, or in proportion to income
- Student loans are typically treated as separate debt of the spouse who incurred them, though loans taken during the marriage for joint benefit may have a marital component

### The third-party problem

**A critical limitation:** Divorce court orders dividing debt are binding between the spouses, but they do not bind creditors. A creditor who holds a joint obligation can still pursue either spouse regardless of what the divorce decree says.

If your spouse is ordered to pay a joint credit card and fails to do so, the credit card company can and will come after you. Your only recourse against your spouse is to return to court for contempt or to seek indemnification — which does nothing for your credit and requires additional litigation.

**Practical protection:**
- Pay off and close joint credit accounts before or at the time of divorce when possible
- Refinance joint debt into individual accounts
- If a joint debt must remain, the person not responsible for payment should monitor the account and have clear legal recourse if the responsible spouse fails to pay
- Indemnification clauses in the separation agreement — requiring the assigned spouse to hold the other harmless from the debt — provide a legal remedy but not protection from the creditor

## When a Spouse Has Run Up Debt Without Your Knowledge {#when-a-spouse-has-run-up-debt-without-your-knowledge}

Financial deception — a spouse secretly opening credit accounts, running up charges, taking out loans, or incurring obligations without the other spouse's knowledge — is more common than most people realize and can have severe consequences for the uninvolved spouse.

### How this happens

**Secret credit cards:** A spouse opens a credit card in their own name only — or sometimes in a joint name through forgery — and runs up balances the other spouse never sees.

**Credit cards opened in the other spouse's name:** A spouse uses the other's Social Security number, date of birth, and personal information to open accounts in the other spouse's name without their consent. This is identity theft.

**Home equity lines of credit (HELOCs):** A spouse takes out a HELOC against the marital home without the other spouse's knowledge. Depending on how the home is titled and the lender's practices, this may require a forged signature on the loan documents.

**Business debts:** In a closely-held business, a spouse-owner may take on business debt that the other spouse does not know about, which then becomes a marital liability or affects the business's value in equitable distribution.

**Tax liabilities:** A spouse files tax returns (jointly or separately) that underreport income, claim improper deductions, or otherwise create tax liability that the other spouse is unaware of.

### Who is legally liable

Under Virginia law, spouses are generally **not liable for each other's individual debts** — a creditor cannot pursue one spouse for a debt that is solely in the other spouse's name, unless the debt was for necessaries (food, medical care, housing) under the doctrine of necessities.

**However**, if the debt is in the innocent spouse's name — whether through identity theft, forgery, or unauthorized use — the innocent spouse is the primary obligor to the creditor. The creditor does not care about the divorce.

### Consequences for the innocent spouse

**Damaged credit:** Accounts opened fraudulently in the innocent spouse's name, or joint accounts that have been maxed out without their knowledge, appear on their credit report and damage their score — often discovered only when they apply for new credit during or after the divorce.

**Collection actions:** Creditors may pursue the innocent spouse for debts in their name regardless of who incurred the charges.

**Tax liability:** If spouses have been filing joint tax returns and one spouse underreported income or claimed fraudulent deductions, the IRS can pursue both spouses for the full tax, penalties, and interest — unless the innocent spouse qualifies for **Innocent Spouse Relief** under IRC § 6015 (see below).

**Liens on property:** Creditors with judgments against either spouse can place liens on marital real property.

### What Virginia courts can do

Virginia Code § 20-107.3 gives courts authority to assign debt and to adjust the property division to account for a spouse's unauthorized debt:

**Debt assignment:** A court can assign a debt incurred by one spouse without the other's knowledge entirely to the incurring spouse, directing that spouse to indemnify the innocent spouse.

**Property division adjustment:** If a spouse has secretly incurred marital debt, the court can treat that debt as a dissipation of marital assets — effectively deducting the incurred debt from the incurring spouse's share of the marital estate.

**Monetary award:** The court can award the innocent spouse an additional monetary amount from the marital estate to compensate for marital property dissipated through secret debt.

**What courts cannot do:** Virginia courts cannot compel creditors to release the innocent spouse from liability on a joint debt, cannot eliminate a credit bureau entry, and cannot undo identity theft — those remedies require separate action.

### Remedies for the innocent spouse

**In the divorce proceeding:**
- Present evidence of the secret debt to the court
- Demand full financial disclosure through discovery — bank statements, credit reports, tax returns, loan applications
- Pull your own complete credit report from all three bureaus immediately — you may discover accounts you did not know existed
- Request that the court treat the secretly incurred debt as a dissipation of marital assets
- Seek indemnification provisions in the separation agreement or court order

**Outside the divorce:**
- **Dispute fraudulent accounts** with the credit bureaus if the accounts were opened without your knowledge or consent. File a fraud alert or credit freeze.
- **File a police report** if a spouse opened accounts in your name or forged your signature — this is identity theft and forgery, both of which are crimes in Virginia (Virginia Code §§ 18.2-186.3 and 18.2-172)
- **Notify creditors** of the fraud in writing; the Fair Credit Reporting Act provides mechanisms to dispute unauthorized accounts
- **Seek Innocent Spouse Relief from the IRS** (Form 8857) if you were unaware of tax underreporting on joint returns

### IRS Innocent Spouse Relief

If your spouse filed fraudulent or incorrect joint tax returns — underreporting income, claiming deductions you did not know about, or failing to report income-producing assets — and the IRS assesses a deficiency, you may qualify for **Innocent Spouse Relief** under IRC § 6015.

There are three forms of relief:

**Basic Innocent Spouse Relief (§ 6015(b)):** Full relief from the liability if you can show:
- A joint return was filed
- There is an understatement of tax attributable to erroneous items of the other spouse
- When you signed the return, you did not know and had no reason to know of the understatement
- It would be inequitable to hold you liable

**Separation of Liability Relief (§ 6015(c)):** Available to divorced or legally separated spouses, or spouses who have not lived together for the past 12 months. Allocates the deficiency between the spouses based on whose items caused the understatement.

**Equitable Relief (§ 6015(f)):** A catch-all for situations where full relief or separation of liability relief is not available, but it would be inequitable to hold the requesting spouse liable. This includes situations where the deficiency arose from the other spouse's unreported income that you did not know about.

**Innocent Spouse Relief requests must be filed within 2 years of the IRS beginning collection activity** (for basic and separation of liability relief; equitable relief has different rules). Time limits are strictly enforced. If you discover joint tax liability tied to your spouse's fraudulent conduct, consult a tax attorney promptly.

## Dissipation of Marital Assets

**Dissipation** is the use or destruction of marital assets for a non-marital purpose after the marriage has begun to break down — or sometimes in anticipation of divorce. Virginia Code § 20-107.3(E)(10) specifically directs courts to consider dissipation.

### What counts as dissipation

- Spending marital funds on an affair partner (gifts, travel, housing, restaurants)
- Gambling losses from marital funds
- Excessive alcohol or drug expenditures that depleted marital accounts
- Gifts to third parties (family members, friends) that were not mutual decisions
- Deliberate destruction of marital property
- Selling marital assets for below-market value to a third party
- Running up credit card debt for personal indulgences after separation begins
- Withdrawing retirement funds and spending them (triggering taxes and penalties in addition to depleting the asset)

### What does not count as dissipation

Not every expenditure one spouse dislikes is dissipation. Courts distinguish between:
- **Dissipation:** Expenditures that deplete the marital estate for personal or non-marital purposes after the breakdown of the marriage
- **Normal spending:** Ordinary living expenses, reasonable legal fees, business expenses, and costs incurred in the maintenance of the marital standard of living

Courts generally look for the timing (did the spending coincide with the breakdown of the marriage?), the amount (was it proportionate to the couple's normal expenditures?), and the purpose (did it benefit the marriage or only one spouse?).

### How courts address dissipation

Courts can:
- **Reconstruct the estate:** Add back the dissipated amount to the marital estate for purposes of division, then award the dissipating spouse a smaller share
- **Award a monetary judgment:** Order the dissipating spouse to pay the innocent spouse their share of the dissipated amount from any remaining marital property
- **Adjust support:** In some cases, economic misconduct that dissipates the marital estate can also affect spousal support

**Evidence matters:** Proving dissipation requires documentation — bank statements, credit card records, communications about the expenditures. A forensic accountant can trace the flow of funds and identify patterns that ordinary analysis misses.

## Hiding Assets

Asset concealment during divorce is common, often sophisticated, and can be discovered with proper professional assistance.

### Common methods of hiding assets

- Deferring income, bonuses, or commissions until after the divorce is finalized (by arrangement with an employer)
- Overpaying the IRS or state taxes to generate a refund after the divorce
- Creating fictitious debts to family members or business associates
- Underreporting business income or inflating business expenses
- Transferring cash to accounts the other spouse does not know about
- Purchasing cash-value life insurance with marital funds
- Purchasing cryptocurrency without disclosure
- Transferring marital assets to trusts for children or other family members
- Undervaluing business interests on financial disclosure forms

### Discovering hidden assets

- **Forensic accountants:** Trace the flow of funds through bank and investment accounts, identify unusual transactions, compare lifestyle expenses to reported income, and analyze business records for irregularities
- **Discovery tools:** Interrogatories about all financial accounts, subpoenas to financial institutions, requests for loan applications (which contain independent financial disclosures), tax return requests going back several years
- **Lifestyle analysis:** If reported income does not explain the observable lifestyle (cars, vacations, restaurants, clothing), a forensic accountant can quantify the discrepancy
- **Social media:** Purchases and experiences often appear on social media — a spouse who claims poverty while posting vacation photos creates credibility problems
- **Business records:** Privately-held businesses offer many opportunities to hide income and assets; forensic accountants specializing in business valuation can examine these records in detail

### Consequences of hiding assets

A spouse caught hiding assets faces:
- **Adverse inference:** Courts can instruct themselves to assume the hidden assets were more valuable than any subsequently disclosed value
- **Sanctions:** Courts can award a larger share of the marital estate to the innocent spouse as a sanction for concealment
- **Contempt:** Failure to comply with discovery obligations or financial disclosure requirements is contempt of court
- **Perjury:** A spouse who lies under oath about assets — in interrogatory responses, depositions, or testimony — has committed perjury, a felony in Virginia (Virginia Code § 18.2-434)
- **Fraud on the court:** If a divorce was finalized based on fraudulent financial disclosure, the innocent spouse may be able to have the final decree reopened and the property division reconsidered

## Practical Considerations

### Gather financial records before you separate

The single most valuable thing you can do before initiating a divorce is to compile a complete picture of the marital estate:

- Bank and investment account statements (at least 3-5 years)
- Retirement account statements
- Tax returns (at least 3-5 years)
- Mortgage statements and property records
- Vehicle titles
- Life insurance policies with cash value
- Business financial records if applicable
- Credit card and loan statements
- Any documentation of separate property (inheritance records, pre-marital account statements)

Once separation begins and litigation starts, access to some of these records may become more difficult — particularly if records were on shared accounts your spouse controls. Copy and preserve what you can.

### Understand the whole estate before dividing any of it

A common mistake is to negotiate specific assets in isolation without understanding the full picture. The tax treatment of an asset, its liquidity, its embedded gain or loss, and its relationship to other marital assets all affect its real value. Dividing assets piecemeal — without a complete accounting — often leads to unequal outcomes that neither party fully understood when they agreed.

### Work with professionals

Complex property division — particularly involving retirement accounts, businesses, real estate, stock options, or significant investment portfolios — requires more than a family law attorney. Consider engaging:

- A **CPA or tax attorney** to model the after-tax consequences of proposed divisions
- A **forensic accountant** if you suspect hidden assets or complex financial structures
- A **business valuator** (certified in business valuation, or CBV) if a business interest is at stake
- A **QDRO specialist** to ensure retirement account division is properly implemented

The cost of these professionals is typically far less than the value of the mistakes they help you avoid.
