# Types of Divorce in Virginia

Not all divorces are the same. Virginia law recognizes distinct categories of divorce that differ in scope, process, cost, and what courts can order. Understanding which type applies to your situation — and what judges typically do in each — is essential to setting realistic expectations before you enter the process.

## Divorce from the Bond of Matrimony

**Virginia Code § 20-91**

A **divorce from the bond of matrimony** — also called an **absolute divorce** — is a full, final dissolution of the marriage. Once granted, both parties are legally single and free to remarry.

This is what most people mean when they say "divorce." It is the end state of the divorce process in Virginia, regardless of whether the case was contested or uncontested, fault or no-fault.

An absolute divorce can address and resolve:
- Division of marital property and debts (equitable distribution)
- Spousal support (alimony)
- Child custody and visitation
- Child support
- Restoration of a former name

**The final decree of divorce** is the court order that grants an absolute divorce. Once entered, the marriage is over. The decree also incorporates any separation agreement or court rulings on the issues above.

## Divorce from Bed and Board

**Virginia Code § 20-95**

A **divorce from bed and board** (*a mensa et thoro* — Latin for "from table and bed") is a partial, legal separation that does not fully dissolve the marriage. The parties remain legally married but are authorized to live separately.

### What it can and cannot do

**A divorce from bed and board CAN:**
- Establish the right to live separately without legal consequence
- Award one spouse exclusive use of the marital home
- Establish temporary custody and visitation arrangements
- Order spousal support
- Divide use of marital property during the separation

**A divorce from bed and board CANNOT:**
- Dissolve the marriage — neither party can remarry
- Permanently divide marital assets in the same way an absolute divorce can
- Grant a final, permanent resolution of all marital issues

### Grounds required

Unlike no-fault absolute divorce, a divorce from bed and board requires **fault-based grounds**:
- Cruelty or reasonable apprehension of bodily harm
- Willful desertion or abandonment

### When it is used today

Divorce from bed and board is rarely sought. Most attorneys will advise pursuing an absolute divorce directly. However, it may be appropriate in limited circumstances:

- A spouse needs to remain legally married to retain health insurance coverage under the other spouse's policy (COBRA conversion is triggered by absolute divorce)
- A spouse relies on military benefits (e.g., the 20/20/20 rule for commissary and healthcare access) that require the marriage to remain legally intact
- Religious beliefs prohibit a full dissolution of the marriage
- A spouse needs immediate court-ordered protection and separation but is not yet eligible to file for absolute divorce

**Practical note:** A divorce from bed and board can later be converted to an absolute divorce once the grounds and separation requirements for absolute divorce are met.

## Uncontested Divorce

An **uncontested divorce** is one where both spouses have reached agreement on all issues before the court finalizes the divorce. There are no disputed matters for the judge to decide — the court is simply asked to review and approve what the parties have already resolved.

### How an Uncontested Divorce Works

The typical path for an uncontested divorce in Virginia:

1. **Separation agreement** — The parties (usually through their attorneys, a mediator, or sometimes on their own) negotiate and sign a comprehensive **Property Settlement Agreement (PSA)** covering property division, spousal support, custody, visitation, and child support.

2. **Corroboration witness** — Virginia requires that the plaintiff's testimony be corroborated by at least one witness who can confirm the date of separation, the fact of continuous separation, and that the plaintiff has been a Virginia resident for at least six months. This witness is often a family member or close friend.

3. **Filing** — The plaintiff files a Complaint for Divorce, along with the signed separation agreement and other required documents.

4. **Proving up** — Many Virginia circuit courts allow uncontested divorces to be finalized on the papers alone — without either party appearing in court — through a process sometimes called a "paper divorce" or "affidavit divorce." The plaintiff and corroboration witness submit sworn affidavits; a commissioner in chancery or the judge reviews the record and recommends or grants the divorce.

5. **Final decree** — The judge enters the Final Decree of Divorce, which incorporates the separation agreement. The agreement becomes a court order and is enforceable as such.

### What Courts Typically Do: Uncontested {#what-courts-typically-do-uncontested}

In an uncontested divorce, the court's role is largely ministerial — it reviews the paperwork rather than making independent decisions. However, there are limits:

**Property and support between spouses:**
Courts almost universally approve whatever the parties agreed to for property division and spousal support, as long as both parties signed voluntarily and the agreement is not unconscionable. Judges rarely second-guess negotiated agreements between adults represented by counsel.

**Child custody and visitation:**
Courts apply more scrutiny here. Even in uncontested cases, the judge must independently determine that the proposed custody and visitation arrangement is in the **best interests of the child**. In practice, if the parenting plan is reasonable and both parents are in agreement, courts approve it. But a plan that appears harmful to the child's welfare will not be rubber-stamped.

**Child support:**
Virginia child support guidelines carry a presumption of correctness. If the parties agree to a support amount that deviates from the guidelines, the court requires written findings explaining why the deviation serves the child's best interests. Courts are more likely to approve upward deviations (more support than guidelines require) than downward deviations.

**Bottom line for uncontested cases:** If you have a valid, comprehensive agreement and have met the procedural requirements, courts in Virginia generally approve the divorce with minimal intervention. The process is designed to be efficient for cooperative parties.

## Contested Divorce

A **contested divorce** is one where the spouses cannot agree on one or more significant issues — property division, spousal support, custody, support, or the grounds for divorce itself. The unresolved issues are submitted to the court for decision.

Contested divorces are substantially more expensive, more time-consuming, and more emotionally demanding than uncontested ones. They frequently involve discovery, depositions, expert witnesses, multiple hearings, and sometimes a multi-day trial.

### How a Contested Divorce Works

1. **Filing and service** — One spouse files a Complaint for Divorce; the other is served and files an Answer (and potentially a Counterclaim asserting their own grounds or claims).

2. **Pendente lite motions** — Early in the case, either party can file motions for temporary orders: temporary custody, temporary support, temporary exclusive use of the marital home, and injunctions against dissipating marital assets. These orders govern the parties during the pendency of the divorce.

3. **Discovery** — Both sides exchange financial information and documents. This typically includes: tax returns, pay stubs, bank and investment account statements, retirement account statements, business records, credit card statements, and real estate documents. Discovery may also include depositions of the parties and third-party witnesses, subpoenas to financial institutions, and interrogatories.

4. **Motions to compel** — If a party fails to cooperate with discovery, the other party can seek a court order compelling compliance. Sanctions for non-compliance can include adverse evidentiary inferences, fee awards, or in extreme cases, dismissal of claims.

5. **Guardian ad litem** — In contested custody cases, the court may appoint a Guardian ad Litem (GAL) — an attorney appointed to represent the children's interests independently. The GAL investigates and reports to the court, and their recommendations carry significant weight.

6. **Custody evaluations** — Courts may also order a psychological evaluation or custody evaluation by a mental health professional, particularly in high-conflict cases. These reports often significantly influence the court's custody ruling.

7. **Mediation** — Virginia courts routinely refer contested cases to mediation before trial. Many issues are resolved in mediation even in cases that seemed hopelessly contentious. Mediation is confidential and non-binding unless an agreement is reached.

8. **Trial** — If issues remain unresolved after mediation, the case proceeds to a hearing or trial before the judge. Witnesses testify, documents are admitted into evidence, and attorneys argue. The judge then issues rulings — sometimes from the bench, sometimes in a written opinion weeks later.

9. **Final decree** — The judge enters the Final Decree of Divorce incorporating all rulings.

### What Courts Typically Rule: Contested {#what-courts-typically-rule-contested}

**Property Division:**
Virginia courts divide marital property under the **equitable distribution** standard — fair, but not necessarily equal. In practice, long-term marriages with relatively equal contributions tend to result in roughly 50/50 splits of major assets. Courts deviate from an equal split when the facts support it:

- A spouse who wasted, dissipated, or hid marital assets may receive less
- A spouse who made significantly greater non-monetary contributions (e.g., homemaking while the other built a career) may receive more
- Separate property traceable to pre-marital assets or inheritance is excluded from division, though commingling can convert it to marital property
- Fault is a factor courts can consider, but Virginia courts rarely use it to dramatically skew property division

**Spousal Support:**
Virginia courts have broad discretion. Key patterns in what courts typically do:

- *Short marriages (under 5 years):* Courts are unlikely to award long-term spousal support; if awarded at all, it tends to be rehabilitative and time-limited
- *Medium marriages (5–15 years):* Courts may award support for a period proportional to the marriage length, often roughly half the length of the marriage, particularly if there is a significant income disparity
- *Long marriages (15+ years):* Courts are more likely to award extended or indefinite support, especially where one spouse left the workforce or has limited earning capacity due to age or health
- *Adultery by the recipient spouse:* Courts are barred from awarding alimony to a spouse who committed adultery, absent manifest injustice
- *Standard of living:* Courts consider the marital standard of living and try to avoid drastic lifestyle disparities where one spouse can afford support

**Child Custody:**
The standard is always the **best interests of the child** under Virginia Code § 20-124.3. Courts consider sixteen specific statutory factors. In practice:

- Courts strongly favor arrangements that keep both parents meaningfully involved in the child's life — true sole physical custody awards are less common than they used to be
- **Joint legal custody** (shared decision-making) is the norm in contested cases unless there is a documented history of domestic violence, abuse, substance abuse, or an inability of the parties to communicate
- **Physical custody** arrangements vary widely. Courts look at each parent's availability, stability, relationship with the child, work schedule, proximity to the child's school, and the child's established routine
- A parent who is more likely to support the child's relationship with the other parent is viewed more favorably
- Relocation requests — where one parent wants to move away with the children — are decided on the best interests standard, but courts often scrutinize them closely and may deny relocation if it would significantly disrupt the child's relationship with the other parent
- Children's preferences are considered, but are not determinative; older teenagers' preferences carry more weight

**Child Support:**
Courts follow the **Virginia Child Support Guidelines** (Virginia Code § 20-108.2), which produce a presumed support amount based on both parents' incomes, the custody arrangement, and specific expenses (childcare, health insurance). Deviation from guidelines requires written justification. In practice, courts follow the guidelines in the vast majority of cases.

## Simplified (Affidavit) Divorce

Virginia does not have a formal "simplified divorce" procedure by statute, but most Virginia circuit courts have developed streamlined processes for uncontested no-fault divorces that are effectively simplified in practice.

Where available, this process allows the divorce to be finalized without either party appearing in court. The plaintiff (and corroboration witness) submit sworn affidavits. The court reviews the record, and if everything is in order, a commissioner in chancery issues a report recommending the divorce, which the judge then approves by entering the Final Decree.

Requirements for this process vary by jurisdiction, but generally require:
- A signed, notarized separation agreement
- No disputed issues
- The required separation period has been met
- Properly completed affidavits from the plaintiff and at least one corroborating witness

**This process is often faster and less expensive** than any court appearance — some courts can finalize the paperwork in a matter of weeks once all documents are submitted correctly.

## High-Conflict Divorce

A **high-conflict divorce** is not a distinct legal category, but a practical reality in some cases — one that significantly alters the trajectory of the proceedings and what outcomes to expect.

High-conflict divorces typically involve one or more of:
- Domestic violence or abuse (physical, emotional, or financial)
- Allegations of substance abuse or mental health issues affecting parenting
- Hidden assets or financial fraud
- Severe parental alienation
- Repeated violations of court orders
- Ongoing litigation after the divorce is finalized (modification proceedings, contempt motions)

### What courts typically do in high-conflict cases

**Protective orders:** If domestic violence is established or credibly alleged, courts can issue emergency protective orders, preliminary protective orders, and permanent protective orders under Virginia Code § 16.1-253.1. A protective order can remove a party from the marital home, restrict contact, and affect temporary custody arrangements.

**Guardian ad litem:** Courts almost always appoint a GAL in contested custody cases with abuse allegations or high parental conflict. The GAL's investigation and report heavily influence custody outcomes.

**Custody evaluations:** Courts may order a forensic custody evaluation by a licensed psychologist. These evaluations are thorough and expensive, and their recommendations are taken seriously by judges.

**Supervised visitation:** Where a parent poses a credible risk to the child's safety, courts can order that visitation occur only in a supervised setting — either through a professional visitation center or with a designated supervisor present.

**Contempt:** Courts take violations of their orders seriously. A party who repeatedly violates court orders — on support, custody exchanges, or discovery — faces contempt findings, fines, and in serious cases, incarceration.

**Dissipation of assets:** If one party destroys, hides, or wastes marital assets during the divorce, courts can — and do — adjust property division to account for the dissipation. The dissipating party may receive less of the remaining marital estate.

## Collaborative Divorce

**Collaborative divorce** is a voluntary process in which both spouses and their attorneys commit in writing to resolving all issues outside of court, without litigation. The parties work with a team that may include financial neutrals and mental health professionals to reach a mutually acceptable resolution.

Key features:
- Both attorneys sign a **participation agreement** — if the case goes to court, both attorneys must withdraw and the parties must hire new counsel
- The process is confidential
- It is typically faster and less expensive than contested litigation
- It requires good-faith participation by both parties

Virginia does not have specific collaborative divorce legislation, but collaborative practice is well-established in the state and recognized by Virginia's courts and bar.

**Collaborative divorce is not appropriate** where there is a significant power imbalance, domestic violence, hidden assets, or a spouse who is not genuinely committed to good-faith negotiation.

## Choosing the Right Path

| Situation | Likely Best Path |
|-----------|-----------------|
| Both spouses agree on everything | Uncontested / affidavit divorce |
| Generally cooperative, some issues unresolved | Mediation leading to uncontested divorce |
| Committed to cooperation, want structured process | Collaborative divorce |
| Significant disagreements, but willing to negotiate | Negotiated settlement with attorneys |
| Deep conflict, cannot communicate, complex assets | Contested divorce with full litigation |
| Domestic violence, hidden assets, urgent safety concerns | Contested with emergency motions; protective orders |
| Need legal separation without full divorce | Divorce from bed and board |

The path you choose — or are forced into — will shape your experience, your costs, and your outcomes. Most cases that begin as contested eventually settle before trial. Settlements give both parties control over the outcome; trials give that control to a judge who does not know your family and must make decisions based on a limited record.

If there is any realistic prospect of reaching agreement, most experienced family law attorneys will advise pursuing that path first.
