# The Divorce Process in Virginia

Understanding what actually happens during a Virginia divorce — not just the steps on paper, but what each court date looks like, how the schedule gets set, and what judges expect — helps you prepare and avoid costly surprises. This guide walks through the process from the first filing to the final decree.

## Filing the Complaint

The divorce process begins when one spouse — the **plaintiff** — files a **Complaint for Divorce** (also called a Bill of Complaint) with the Circuit Court clerk in the appropriate jurisdiction. The filing spouse pays the filing fee (typically $86–$100 in most Virginia jurisdictions, plus service fees).

**What the Complaint contains:**
- The plaintiff's name and the defendant's name
- The date and place of marriage
- Names and ages of any minor children
- The grounds for divorce being asserted (no-fault separation, or a fault ground)
- The relief requested (divorce, custody, support, property division, etc.)

**What gets filed with it:**
- A Civil Case Information Sheet
- If a separation agreement exists and the case is uncontested, the signed agreement is typically attached
- Any motions for emergency or pendente lite relief if immediate court intervention is needed

Once filed, the clerk assigns the case a docket number and the case is formally open.

---

## Service of Process

After filing, the defendant must be formally served with the divorce papers. This is not optional — the court cannot proceed without it.

### Methods of service

**Sheriff's service:** The most common method. The plaintiff pays a fee and the sheriff's office in the defendant's jurisdiction personally delivers the papers. Service is complete when the defendant is handed the documents.

**Private process server:** An alternative to sheriff's service, often faster. Private servers can be more persistent and flexible in scheduling.

**Acceptance of service:** In cooperative cases, the defendant signs and notarizes an **Acceptance of Service**, waiving formal service. This is the fastest and least contentious method and is common in uncontested cases.

**Service by publication:** Used when the defendant's location is genuinely unknown. Notice is published in a local newspaper for a set period. This method severely limits the court's ability to issue orders against the defendant — the court can grant the divorce but generally cannot order support or divide property against an absent, unlocated party.

**Out-of-state service:** If the defendant lives in another state, personal service in that state is required. The defendant has **60 days** to respond (rather than 21 days for in-state service).

### What happens after service

Once served, the defendant has a limited time to respond. The clock starts on the date of service.

---

## The Defendant's Response

After being served, the defendant has:
- **21 days** to file an Answer if served in Virginia
- **60 days** to file an Answer if served outside Virginia

### The Answer

The Answer is the defendant's formal response to the Complaint. The defendant admits or denies each allegation and may raise defenses. The Answer may also include a **Counterclaim** — the defendant's own affirmative claims against the plaintiff (for example, asserting different grounds for divorce, or seeking support or property division on different terms).

### If the defendant does not respond

If the defendant fails to file an Answer within the deadline, the plaintiff can move for a **default**. A default does not automatically grant the divorce — the plaintiff must still prove their case — but it means the defendant has given up the right to contest the proceedings. The court may proceed and enter orders without further input from the defaulting party.

Default is significant: a defendant who ignores a divorce Complaint can have property division, support, and custody decided entirely without them.

## How Court Dates Are Scheduled

This is one of the least-understood aspects of Virginia divorce litigation. There is no single scheduling system — it varies by jurisdiction, by judge, and by the complexity of the case. Here is how it generally works:

### Initial scheduling

After the defendant responds (or defaults), the case moves toward scheduling. In many Virginia circuit courts:

- The **plaintiff's attorney** (or the plaintiff, if pro se) takes responsibility for contacting the court to schedule hearings
- Some courts issue a **scheduling order** on their own initiative, setting deadlines for discovery and a trial date
- Other courts are more passive and schedule dates only when the parties request them

**Docket types:** Most circuit courts have separate dockets for different types of hearings. Understanding which docket applies matters:

- **Motions docket** — Short hearings on discrete legal issues (motions to compel, motions to dismiss, etc.). Usually 15–30 minutes, scheduled weeks to a few months out.
- **Pendente lite docket** — Emergency or temporary relief hearings. May be scheduled on shorter notice, sometimes within days if a true emergency is shown.
- **Domestic relations docket / Family docket** — Full evidentiary hearings on contested custody, support, and property issues. Often scheduled months in advance due to docket congestion.
- **Trial docket** — Multi-day contested divorce trials. In busy jurisdictions (Fairfax, Arlington, Prince William, Chesterfield), trial dates can be **6 to 18 months** from the time of filing, and sometimes longer.

### Continuances

Court dates in divorce cases frequently get continued (postponed). Common reasons include:
- Settlement negotiations still in progress
- Discovery not yet complete
- Parties requesting more time for mediation
- Judicial reassignment or scheduling conflicts

Each continuance pushes the case further back on the docket. In high-volume jurisdictions, getting a new trial date after a continuance can mean waiting several more months.

### Local rules matter

Every Virginia circuit court has its own local rules that govern scheduling, page limits on briefs, exhibit procedures, and other procedural details. What is standard practice in Fairfax Circuit Court may differ from Roanoke or Virginia Beach. Knowing the local rules and the preferences of the assigned judge is part of what experienced local family law attorneys bring to a case.

## Pendente Lite Hearing

*Pendente lite* is Latin for "while the suit is pending." A pendente lite hearing addresses **temporary orders** that govern the parties while the divorce case is ongoing. These are not final — they last only until the case is resolved.

### When it is needed

Not every divorce case requires a pendente lite hearing. In cooperative cases, the parties agree to temporary arrangements without court intervention. A pendente lite hearing becomes necessary when:

- One spouse needs immediate financial support and the other refuses to pay
- Custody of children cannot be agreed upon and the parties need a temporary schedule
- There is a dispute over who lives in the marital home during the case
- One spouse is dissipating or hiding marital assets
- An emergency protective order is needed due to domestic violence

### How to get a pendente lite hearing

Either party files a **Motion for Pendente Lite Relief** (sometimes called a Motion for Temporary Relief) specifying what they are asking the court to order. In genuine emergencies — imminent domestic violence, a parent about to flee with children — courts can schedule an emergency hearing within days. For non-emergency temporary relief, the hearing is typically scheduled a few weeks to a couple of months out, depending on the court's docket.

### What the hearing looks like

A pendente lite hearing is a **short evidentiary hearing**, typically 30 minutes to 2 hours. Each side presents evidence on the temporary issues:

- Testimony from each party (usually brief)
- Sworn financial statements (required in most jurisdictions)
- Pay stubs, bank statements, and expense documentation
- Evidence relevant to custody (the children's schedules, each parent's availability, any safety concerns)

Because of time constraints, pendente lite hearings are not mini-trials. The judge makes temporary decisions based on limited evidence. **These rulings are not a preview of the final outcome** — they are designed to maintain the status quo and prevent immediate harm, not to resolve the underlying issues permanently.

### What judges typically order at pendente lite

**Support:** Courts calculate temporary spousal support using one of the available formulas (many Virginia courts use the Fairfax formula or a similar method as a starting point) and may order temporary child support at or near the guidelines amount. The goal is to provide sufficient funds to maintain both households during the case, not to punish either party.

**Custody:** Courts typically order a temporary parenting schedule that keeps both parents involved unless there is a credible safety concern. Courts are reluctant to make dramatic custody changes at the pendente lite stage without strong evidence — temporary rulings tend toward stability. Joint legal custody is commonly ordered as the temporary arrangement.

**Marital home:** Courts often order one party to temporarily vacate the marital home, particularly when the parties cannot coexist under the same roof or when children need stability of location. Courts may also issue injunctions against selling, mortgaging, or encumbering marital property.

**Injunctions against dissipation:** If one spouse is spending down marital assets, hiding money, or running up debt, the court can enjoin further dissipation and freeze accounts.

## Scheduling Conference

Some Virginia circuit courts hold a **scheduling conference** — a short administrative hearing (often 15–30 minutes) at which the judge or a judicial assistant meets with the attorneys to:

- Set deadlines for discovery (interrogatories, depositions, expert witnesses)
- Set a mediation deadline
- Set a pre-trial conference date
- Set a trial date (or a trial scheduling window)

Not all courts hold scheduling conferences — some courts accomplish this by written order, and some leave scheduling entirely to the parties. Where they do occur, the scheduling conference is usually not an opportunity to argue the merits of the case. It is purely administrative.

**What to bring:** Attorneys (or pro se parties) should come prepared with their calendar and a realistic sense of how long discovery will take and how complex the trial will be. Agreeing to an unrealistic timeline at the scheduling conference creates problems later.

## Discovery Period

Discovery is the formal process by which each side gathers information and evidence before trial. In a contested Virginia divorce, discovery typically includes:

### Interrogatories

Written questions that the other party must answer under oath within 21 days. In divorce cases, interrogatories commonly cover:
- Employment history and current income
- All assets, accounts, and debts
- The history and value of specific marital property
- Insurance policies
- Business interests
- Any fault conduct being alleged

### Requests for Production of Documents

Written requests for specific documents. Common in divorce cases:
- Tax returns (typically 3–5 years)
- Bank and investment account statements (typically 2–5 years)
- Retirement account statements
- Pay stubs and employment records
- Credit card statements
- Business financial records
- Appraisals and valuations
- Loan applications (which contain financial disclosures that can reveal hidden assets)
- Text messages, emails, or other communications relevant to fault grounds

### Depositions

Oral testimony taken under oath before a court reporter, outside of court. Either party can be deposed, as can third-party witnesses. Depositions are expensive (court reporter fees, preparation time) but valuable for:
- Locking in testimony before trial so witnesses cannot change their story
- Assessing credibility and what the other side will say at trial
- Discovering information not apparent from documents

### Subpoenas

Subpoenas can compel third parties — banks, employers, social media platforms, cell phone carriers — to produce records. Subpoenas are often used when one party is suspected of hiding assets or when financial records are needed from sources the other party controls.

### Expert witnesses

Complex financial issues (business valuation, pension valuation, tax implications of property division) and custody disputes often require expert witnesses:
- **Forensic accountants** to value businesses or uncover hidden assets
- **Actuaries or financial planners** to value defined benefit pensions
- **Real estate appraisers** to value the marital home and other real property
- **Custody evaluators** or **psychologists** in contested custody cases

Expert witnesses must typically be disclosed by a deadline set in the scheduling order, with reports provided to the other side in advance of trial.

### Motions to Compel

If a party fails to respond to discovery requests, provides incomplete or evasive responses, or refuses to appear for a deposition, the other party can file a **Motion to Compel**. The court will hold a hearing and, if discovery was improperly withheld, order compliance. Courts may also award attorney's fees against the non-complying party. In serious cases of willful non-compliance, courts can impose sanctions including adverse evidentiary inferences — essentially telling the jury or themselves to assume the withheld information was unfavorable to the non-complying party.

## Motions Hearings

Throughout the divorce case, the parties may file various motions that require the court's attention before trial. Common motions in Virginia divorce cases:

**Motion to Compel** — To force compliance with discovery (see above).

**Motion for Sanctions** — Seeking penalties for a party's misconduct during litigation (discovery abuse, violation of court orders, filing frivolous motions).

**Motion in Limine** — A pre-trial motion asking the court to exclude certain evidence or limit testimony at trial. For example, a party might seek to exclude evidence of conduct that is time-barred or legally irrelevant.

**Motion for Summary Judgment** — Rare in divorce cases, but occasionally used to resolve discrete legal issues where there is no genuine dispute of fact (e.g., whether a particular asset is marital or separate property based on undisputed documentation).

**Motion to Reconsider** — A request to revisit a prior ruling, typically based on new evidence or a legal argument not previously raised.

### What a motions hearing looks like

Motions hearings are typically scheduled for 15–30 minutes, though complex motions may get longer slots. The hearing is conducted in the judge's courtroom. Each attorney argues their position; the judge may ask questions. Testimony is rarely taken at motions hearings — they are argument-based, not evidence-based (with the exception of pendente lite hearings, which are evidentiary).

Judges often rule from the bench at motions hearings, but may also take the motion "under advisement" and issue a written ruling days or weeks later.

## Mediation

Virginia courts routinely order contested divorce cases to mediation before trial. Even without a court order, many parties voluntarily attempt mediation as a less expensive alternative to trial.

### What mediation is

Mediation is a confidential, voluntary process in which a neutral third party — the mediator — helps the parties communicate and explore settlement options. The mediator does not decide anything; they facilitate negotiation. Nothing said in mediation can be used against a party in court if mediation fails.

Mediators in Virginia divorce cases are often attorneys or retired judges with family law experience, though mental health professionals also serve as mediators in custody-focused disputes.

### How it is scheduled

When a court orders mediation, the scheduling order typically sets a **mediation deadline** — a date by which mediation must occur. The parties (or their attorneys) are responsible for selecting a mediator and scheduling the session. Private mediators charge by the hour; rates vary widely but $200–$400/hour is common, split between the parties.

### What a mediation session looks like

Mediation sessions typically last a half day to a full day, though complex cases may require multiple sessions. The mediator usually begins with the parties together, then often separates them into different rooms and shuttles between them (called "caucus" mediation). The mediator conveys offers, explains legal realities, and helps each party evaluate their position honestly.

Attorneys typically attend with their clients, though in some lower-conflict cases parties mediate without attorneys (attorney-assisted mediation is generally recommended in complex cases).

### What happens if mediation succeeds

If the parties reach agreement, the mediator drafts a Memorandum of Understanding or the attorneys draft a formal Property Settlement Agreement. Once signed and notarized, the agreement is submitted to the court and incorporated into the final decree.

### What happens if mediation fails

If mediation is unsuccessful, the case proceeds toward trial. Mediation failure is not a mark against either party — courts expect that not all cases will settle.

In practice, a significant percentage of contested Virginia divorce cases settle at or shortly after mediation, even cases that seemed deeply contentious. The prospect of trial — its cost, unpredictability, and emotional toll — often motivates compromise that earlier negotiations could not achieve.

## Pre-Trial Conference

A **pre-trial conference** is a hearing held shortly before trial — typically days to a few weeks beforehand — at which the judge meets with the attorneys to:

- Confirm the trial date and estimated duration
- Identify what issues remain contested and what (if anything) has been resolved
- Address any outstanding motions or evidentiary disputes
- Discuss logistics: number of witnesses, exhibits, whether any witnesses will testify by video
- Determine whether the parties have complied with all pre-trial filing requirements (witness lists, exhibit lists, proposed findings of fact, trial briefs)

Pre-trial conferences can also prompt last-minute settlement discussions. When attorneys state on the record what each side's evidence will show, the remaining gaps often become clearer — and more likely to be bridged.

### Pre-trial filing requirements

Most Virginia circuit courts require the parties to file certain documents in advance of trial. Common requirements include:

- **Witness list** — Names of all witnesses the party intends to call at trial
- **Exhibit list** — A numbered list of all documents and items the party intends to introduce into evidence
- **Trial brief** — A written legal argument explaining the party's position on the contested issues and the applicable law
- **Proposed findings of fact and conclusions of law** — In some courts and for some issues, the judge may ask parties to submit proposed written rulings for the judge to consider

Failure to include a witness or exhibit on the pre-trial list can result in the court excluding that witness or evidence at trial. Compliance with pre-trial filing requirements is not optional.

## Trial

If the parties cannot resolve their disputes through negotiation or mediation, the case proceeds to **trial**. A contested Virginia divorce trial is a civil bench trial — there is no jury. The judge decides all issues.

### How trial is scheduled

Trial dates in Virginia circuit courts are set in the scheduling order, typically many months in advance. In high-volume jurisdictions:

- **Northern Virginia (Fairfax, Arlington, Loudoun, Prince William):** Trial dates can be 12–18 months from filing, and the docket is competitive. Cases that continue off a trial date may wait 6 or more months for a new one.
- **Richmond area (Henrico, Chesterfield, Richmond City):** Typically 6–12 months from filing.
- **Hampton Roads (Virginia Beach, Norfolk, Chesapeake):** Similar to Richmond.
- **Smaller jurisdictions:** Trial dates can sometimes be obtained in 3–6 months.

Trial dates are **not guaranteed**. Courts may bump a scheduled trial if an older case takes longer than expected or a more urgent matter arises. Cases that have been pending longer generally have priority.

### What to expect at trial

**Before court begins:** Attorneys typically arrive 30–60 minutes early to organize exhibits, confer with witnesses, and address any last-minute issues with opposing counsel. Clients should arrive on time, dressed professionally.

**Opening statements:** Each attorney briefly outlines what the evidence will show. Opening statements in bench trials are often shorter than in jury trials — judges are experienced lawyers who do not need the same level of context setting.

**Plaintiff's case:** The plaintiff presents evidence first. This typically means:
- The plaintiff testifies (direct examination by their attorney, then cross-examination by the opposing attorney)
- Other witnesses called by the plaintiff testify
- Documents and exhibits are admitted into evidence through witness testimony

**Defendant's case:** After the plaintiff rests, the defendant presents their evidence in the same format.

**Rebuttal:** The plaintiff may offer limited rebuttal evidence to respond to issues raised in the defendant's case.

**Closing arguments:** Each attorney summarizes the evidence and argues how the law should be applied to the facts. The plaintiff typically argues first, then the defendant, then the plaintiff has a brief rebuttal opportunity.

**The ruling:** The judge may rule from the bench immediately after closing arguments, or may take the case "under advisement" and issue a written ruling later. Bench decisions in divorce cases are frequently taken under advisement — particularly for complex property or support issues — and written opinions can take days to several weeks.

### What judges focus on at trial

**Credibility:** Judges observe witnesses closely. Inconsistencies between a witness's testimony and their prior written statements, deposition testimony, or documentary evidence significantly damage credibility. A witness who is caught in a lie — even on a minor point — may have their entire testimony discounted.

**Documentary evidence:** In financial disputes, documents speak loudly. Bank records, tax returns, account statements, and business records that contradict a party's testimony are powerful evidence. Judges are experienced at reading financial documents.

**The applicable legal standards:** Judges apply the statutory factors for each issue — the 16 factors for child custody under Virginia Code § 20-124.3, the factors for equitable distribution under § 20-107.3, the factors for spousal support under § 20-107.1. Attorneys who organize their evidence and argument around those specific factors are more effective than those who present generalized narratives.

**Proportionality:** Judges have seen many divorce cases. They are not easily shocked by infidelity, conflict, or financial misbehavior — but they notice when a party presents evidence that is disproportionate to the issues at stake, or when a party spends significant trial time on grievances that do not legally affect the outcome.

## Commissioner in Chancery

Virginia has a distinctive procedure not found in most other states: referral to a **Commissioner in Chancery**.

A Commissioner in Chancery is a licensed Virginia attorney appointed by the circuit court to take evidence and make recommendations on divorce cases. The court may refer an entire uncontested divorce, specific contested issues, or the entire contested case to a commissioner rather than conducting a trial directly before the judge.

### How commissioner proceedings work

When a case is referred to a commissioner:
- The commissioner schedules a hearing (similar to a trial, but before the commissioner rather than the judge)
- Evidence is presented, witnesses testify, and exhibits are admitted — same process as trial
- The commissioner prepares a written **Report** containing findings of fact and recommendations on the referred issues
- The Report is filed with the court
- Either party may file **Exceptions** to the commissioner's Report — specific objections to findings or recommendations
- The judge then reviews the Report and any Exceptions, holds a hearing if needed, and either adopts, modifies, or rejects the commissioner's recommendations by entering a court order

### Why commissioners matter

Commissioner proceedings are extremely common for **uncontested divorces** in Virginia. The "paper divorce" or affidavit divorce described elsewhere involves submitting affidavits to a commissioner, who reviews them and issues a Report recommending the divorce, which the judge then approves.

In contested cases, referral to a commissioner can relieve docket pressure and allow more scheduling flexibility than a trial before the judge. However, commissioner proceedings add a layer to the process — the Report, the Exceptions period, and the judge's review — which can extend the timeline.

## Final Decree of Divorce

The **Final Decree of Divorce** is the court order that officially ends the marriage and resolves all outstanding issues. It is the goal of the entire process.

The Final Decree:
- Grants the divorce from the bond of matrimony
- Incorporates and approves any separation agreement (which then becomes an enforceable court order)
- Contains the court's rulings on all contested issues (property division, support, custody, visitation, child support) if the case was contested
- May restore a former name if requested
- Sets out each party's ongoing obligations (support payments, transfer of property, retirement account division via QDRO, etc.)

### After the Final Decree is entered

The divorce is final when the judge signs the decree. However, several steps often follow:

- **Deed transfers:** If the marital home or other real property is changing ownership, new deeds must be drafted and recorded with the clerk of court in the jurisdiction where the property is located.
- **QDRO:** If retirement accounts are being divided, a **Qualified Domestic Relations Order** (QDRO) must be prepared and submitted to the retirement plan administrator. A QDRO is separate from the divorce decree and requires its own drafting and approval process.
- **Beneficiary updates:** Both parties should update beneficiary designations on life insurance policies, retirement accounts, and other financial accounts.
- **Name change:** If a name restoration was granted, the Final Decree serves as legal documentation for changing identification (driver's license, passport, Social Security records).
- **Insurance:** Both parties should review and update health, auto, and life insurance coverage, as divorce typically triggers a qualifying life event.

## Post-Decree Matters

The Final Decree does not always end the relationship between the parties and the court, particularly when children are involved.

### Modification

Court orders for child custody, visitation, and child support can be modified if there is a **material change in circumstances** since the last order. Spousal support orders may also be modifiable, depending on how they were structured.

Common triggers for post-decree modification proceedings:
- A significant change in either parent's income (child support modification)
- A parent seeking to relocate with the children
- A significant change in a child's needs or circumstances
- A change in the custodial arrangement that has evolved organically but needs court ratification
- A change in either spouse's financial situation affecting spousal support

### Contempt

If one party fails to comply with the Final Decree — fails to pay support, interferes with visitation, fails to transfer property — the other party can file a **Motion for Rule to Show Cause** (contempt motion). The court holds a hearing; if contempt is found, sanctions can include fines, attorney's fees, and in serious cases, incarceration.

### Appeals

A party who believes the trial court made an error of law can appeal the Final Decree (or any appealable order) to the **Court of Appeals of Virginia**. Appeals must be filed within **30 days** of the entry of the order being appealed. Appeals are based on the record from the trial court — no new evidence is presented. Appeals are expensive, time-consuming, and succeed only when a clear legal error is shown; appellate courts give significant deference to trial court findings of fact.

## Uncontested Divorce: The Shortened Path

For couples who have resolved all issues through a separation agreement, the Virginia divorce process is dramatically shorter:

1. Both parties sign and notarize the Property Settlement Agreement
2. The required separation period is completed (6 months or 1 year)
3. The plaintiff files the Complaint and supporting documents with the circuit court
4. The defendant accepts service (or is served by the sheriff)
5. The plaintiff and a corroboration witness complete sworn affidavits
6. The matter is submitted to a Commissioner in Chancery or reviewed directly by the court
7. The commissioner issues a Report recommending the divorce
8. The judge signs the Final Decree

In many Virginia jurisdictions, this entire process — from filing to Final Decree — takes **4 to 12 weeks** once the separation period has been completed and the paperwork is properly prepared. There is typically no court appearance required.

## How Long Does It All Take?

The timeline of a Virginia divorce depends almost entirely on whether the case is contested and how complex the issues are.

| Case Type | Typical Duration from Filing |
|-----------|------------------------------|
| Uncontested, no children, separation agreement in place | 6–12 weeks after filing (assuming separation period complete) |
| Uncontested, with children | 8–16 weeks after filing |
| Contested, limited issues (e.g., only support) | 6–12 months |
| Contested, multiple issues (property, custody, support) | 12–24 months |
| High-conflict, complex assets, custody dispute | 18–36 months or more |

These are estimates. Docket congestion, attorney availability, the complexity of financial issues, and the willingness of the parties to compromise all affect the timeline. Cases in Northern Virginia consistently take longer than comparable cases in less populated jurisdictions due to heavy court volume.

**The most reliable way to shorten the process** is to reach a negotiated agreement — even a partial one — that narrows the issues the court must decide.
