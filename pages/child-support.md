# Child Support in Virginia

Child support is one of the most concrete and consequential financial issues in a Virginia divorce or separation. Unlike spousal support, where courts exercise broad discretion, Virginia child support is primarily calculated by a **statutory formula** — and the consequences for failing to pay are severe, reaching far beyond the courtroom into employment, finances, licenses, and federal programs.

## The Legal Foundation

Virginia child support is governed by **Virginia Code §§ 20-108.1 and 20-108.2**, which establish the guidelines formula that courts must apply. The guidelines create a presumed support amount based on the parents' incomes and the custody arrangement. Courts are required to follow the guidelines unless they make written findings explaining why the guidelines amount would be unjust or inappropriate in the particular case.

Child support exists for the benefit of the child — not the custodial parent. This distinction matters legally: a parent cannot waive a child's right to support on the child's behalf, agreements between parents to forgo child support are unenforceable without court approval, and support paid must be used for the child's benefit.

## How Child Support Is Calculated

Virginia uses an **income shares model**, meaning both parents' incomes are considered and support is calculated based on what both parents together would have spent on the child had the family remained intact — then apportioned based on each parent's share of combined income.

### The basic calculation

**Step 1 — Determine each parent's gross monthly income.**

**Step 2 — Add the incomes together** to get the combined gross monthly income.

**Step 3 — Look up the base child support obligation** on the Virginia Schedule of Monthly Basic Child Support Obligations (Virginia Code § 20-108.2). The schedule is a table that lists the presumed monthly support amount based on combined income and number of children.

**Step 4 — Add qualifying expenses** to the base amount:
- Health insurance premiums for the child
- Work-related childcare costs

**Step 5 — Calculate each parent's proportional share** of the combined income. A parent who earns 60% of the combined income is responsible for 60% of the total obligation.

**Step 6 — Determine who pays whom.** The non-custodial parent (or in shared custody, the higher-earning parent) typically pays their share to the other parent.

### A simplified example

Parent A earns $5,000/month gross. Parent B earns $3,000/month gross.
- Combined income: $8,000/month
- The guideline schedule for two children at $8,000 combined income: approximately $1,600/month (base obligation)
- Parent A's share: 62.5% × $1,600 = $1,000
- Parent B's share: 37.5% × $1,600 = $600
- If Parent B is the custodial parent, Parent A pays Parent B approximately $1,000/month (before adding healthcare and childcare expenses)

The Virginia Department of Social Services provides a child support calculator online. Attorneys use the official guidelines worksheet at every stage of a case.

## Income: What Counts and What Doesn't

### What counts as gross income

Virginia Code § 20-108.2 defines gross income broadly to include **all income from all sources**, regardless of whether it is regularly occurring:

- Wages, salaries, commissions, and tips
- Self-employment income (after allowable business expenses)
- Bonuses (typically averaged over recent years)
- Overtime (courts examine whether overtime is voluntary or expected)
- Rental income
- Interest and dividends
- Pension and retirement benefits
- Social Security benefits (including disability)
- Workers' compensation benefits
- Unemployment compensation
- Severance pay
- Trust and annuity income
- Gifts and inheritances that are recurring or substantial
- Alimony received from a prior relationship (but not from the current case)

### Imputed income: earning capacity

If a parent is voluntarily unemployed or underemployed — working below their capacity without a valid reason — Virginia courts may **impute income** based on what that parent could earn, rather than what they actually earn.

Factors courts consider when imputing income:
- The parent's education, training, and work history
- The local job market and available opportunities in the parent's field
- The parent's health and any legitimate limitations on employment
- Childcare obligations that may limit work hours (courts are sensitive to the practical realities of single parenting)

A parent cannot escape child support obligations by quitting a job, refusing available work, or deliberately staying underemployed. Courts look behind income claims when there is reason to doubt them. Self-employed parents who run income through a business are particularly scrutinized — courts examine business expenses that provide personal benefit and may adjust income accordingly.

### What does not count

- Child support received for children from prior relationships
- Public assistance (TANF, SNAP, Medicaid)
- Means-tested benefit programs
- Payments received on behalf of a child (SSI, VA benefits for the child)

## Custody Arrangement and the Guidelines

The physical custody arrangement has a direct and significant effect on the child support calculation.

### Sole or primary physical custody

When one parent has primary physical custody (fewer than 90 days per year with the other parent), the standard guidelines schedule applies directly. The non-custodial parent pays the custodial parent their proportional share of the base obligation plus expenses.

### Shared physical custody

When each parent has **90 or more days per year** of parenting time, Virginia uses an **adjusted shared custody formula** under Virginia Code § 20-108.2. This formula accounts for the fact that each parent bears direct expenses during their own parenting time and results in a lower transfer payment than the sole custody formula.

The shared custody calculation:
1. Computes each parent's guideline obligation as if the other were the custodial parent
2. Multiplies each obligation by an adjustment factor based on the actual number of days with each parent
3. The parent with the higher adjusted obligation pays the difference to the other parent

The practical effect: as a parent's parenting time increases toward 50/50, the child support obligation decreases — but does not reach zero even at equal parenting time if there is an income disparity, because the higher-earning parent still provides a greater share of the child's total financial support.

### Manipulating parenting time for support purposes

Courts are alert to parents who seek additional parenting time not because of a genuine desire for more time with the child, but to reduce their child support obligation. Judges view this pattern negatively. The appropriate reason to seek parenting time is the child's relationship with the parent — not the financial calculation.

## Deviating from the Guidelines

The guidelines create a presumed amount, but courts may deviate — upward or downward — when the guideline amount would be **unjust or inappropriate** given the specific circumstances. Any deviation requires **written findings** by the court.

Factors Virginia courts consider when deviating:

- Actual monetary support provided by a parent for the child outside of the guideline amount
- The child's independent financial resources
- The child's standard of living had the family remained intact
- Extraordinary medical or educational expenses not captured in the base calculation
- Independent income of the child
- The parents' debts and obligations
- Travel expenses for parenting time (particularly in long-distance cases)
- Support obligations to other children
- Any other relevant factor

Deviations are not uncommon in high-income cases (where the guideline amount may exceed what is actually needed for the child's support) or in cases with unusual expenses. Courts document the deviation and the reasoning in the order.

## Health Insurance and Medical Expenses

### Health insurance premiums

The cost of the child's health insurance premium is added to the base guideline amount and shared between the parents proportionally. If Parent A pays $300/month to cover the child on their employer-sponsored plan, $300 is added to the support calculation and each parent bears their proportional share.

Courts strongly prefer that children be covered by health insurance at all times. The order will specify which parent is responsible for maintaining coverage and require notification if coverage lapses or changes.

### Uninsured medical expenses

Medical expenses not covered by insurance — copays, deductibles, orthodontia, therapy, glasses, prescriptions — are typically shared between the parents in proportion to their incomes. The order establishes the mechanism: one parent pays the provider, then seeks reimbursement from the other parent for their proportional share within a specified time period (often 30 days of submitting documentation).

Disputes over which medical expenses qualify (particularly elective or alternative treatments) are common in high-conflict cases. The parenting order or property settlement agreement should define the scope of covered expenses and the reimbursement process clearly to minimize conflict.

### Catastrophic or extraordinary medical expenses

Extraordinary medical expenses for a child with chronic illness, disability, or significant medical needs may warrant a deviation from the standard guidelines or a separate provision in the support order. Courts have discretion to address these expenses in ways that reflect the actual financial burden on the family.

## Childcare Costs

Work-related childcare expenses — daycare, after-school care, summer camp used as childcare — are added to the base support obligation and shared proportionally between the parents, similar to health insurance premiums.

Only childcare costs that are **work-related** qualify. Enrichment activities or childcare arrangements that are not tied to a parent's employment or job search are not included in the guideline calculation (though they may be addressed separately in the parenting agreement).

When one parent's work schedule or the child's age changes and childcare costs drop or increase substantially, this can trigger a modification of the child support order.

## Duration of Child Support

Child support in Virginia continues until:

- The child turns **18**, or
- The child turns **19** while still **enrolled in high school** and living in the home of the parent receiving support (in which case support continues until the child graduates or turns 19, whichever comes first), or
- The child **graduates from high school** before turning 18 (support ends at graduation), or
- The child **dies**, **marries**, or is **legally emancipated** before age 18

**Post-secondary education:** Unlike some states, Virginia does not require parents to pay child support for a child's college education. Support ends when the child reaches the statutory age or graduates from high school. Parents may voluntarily agree to contribute to college expenses in a separation agreement, but courts cannot order it.

**Children with disabilities:** For a child who is severely mentally or physically disabled and cannot be self-supporting, courts may extend child support beyond age 18 or 19 under Virginia Code § 20-124.2. This requires specific findings about the child's incapacity.

## Modification of Child Support

Child support orders are not permanent. Either parent may seek modification when circumstances change materially.

### The standard for modification

Virginia Code § 20-108.1 requires that a party seeking modification show a **material change in circumstances** since the last order. Additionally, Virginia presumes that a change is material if it would result in a **change in the guideline amount of at least 25 percent** and has existed for at least three months.

Common material changes:
- A significant increase or decrease in either parent's income (raise, job loss, promotion, disability)
- A change in the custody arrangement affecting the number of parenting days
- A substantial change in childcare costs
- A substantial change in the child's health insurance costs
- The child's emancipation or reaching the age at which support terminates
- A parent becoming voluntarily unemployed or underemployed

### Retroactivity of modifications

Critically, modifications are only effective from the date the motion to modify is **filed** — not from the date the change in circumstances occurred. A parent who loses their job but waits six months to file for modification has six months of accrued arrears at the old rate that cannot be retroactively reduced. Existing arrears are never modifiable.

**This is one of the most consequential timing rules in child support law.** If your income drops significantly, file for modification immediately. Do not wait.

## The Federal Connection: Title IV-D and DCSE

Child support enforcement in Virginia is deeply intertwined with **federal law and federal funding** in ways that most people do not realize. The federal government plays a direct role in how Virginia collects, enforces, and reports child support.

### Title IV-D of the Social Security Act

**Title IV-D** of the Social Security Act (42 U.S.C. § 651 et seq.) is the federal statutory framework that created the national child support enforcement system. Under Title IV-D, states receive substantial federal funding to operate child support programs in exchange for meeting federal requirements for enforcement, data reporting, and interstate cooperation.

Virginia receives hundreds of millions of dollars annually in federal matching funds tied to Title IV-D compliance. This federal funding directly shapes how aggressively Virginia pursues non-paying parents.

### Virginia Division of Child Support Enforcement (DCSE)

The **Division of Child Support Enforcement (DCSE)**, part of the Virginia Department of Social Services, is Virginia's Title IV-D agency. DCSE provides:

- **Case setup and order establishment** — For families receiving public assistance, DCSE automatically opens a child support case. Other parents can apply voluntarily.
- **Income withholding** — DCSE sends income withholding orders directly to employers, routing support payments through the state disbursement unit
- **Payment processing and disbursement** — All payments processed through DCSE are recorded and tracked, creating an official record of every payment and any arrears
- **Enforcement actions** — DCSE has independent authority to pursue a wide range of enforcement tools (described below) without requiring the custodial parent to return to court
- **Location services** — DCSE has access to federal and state databases to locate non-paying parents
- **Interstate enforcement** — DCSE coordinates with other states' IV-D agencies to enforce orders across state lines

### Federal data sharing

Because of the Title IV-D framework, child support information flows through federal systems:

- **Federal Case Registry (FCR):** Every child support order in Virginia is reported to the federal registry, which matches parents across state lines
- **National Directory of New Hires:** Employers nationwide report all new hires to a federal database. When a non-paying parent gets a new job — even in another state — DCSE is notified and can immediately issue an income withholding order to the new employer
- **Department of State:** DCSE reports parents with child support arrears over $2,500 to the U.S. Department of State, which can deny, revoke, or limit passports
- **Credit bureaus:** Child support arrears over a threshold amount are reported to all three major credit bureaus
- **IRS and tax intercept:** DCSE participates in the federal and state tax refund intercept programs (see below)

### Do you have to use DCSE?

Parents who reach private agreements and have reliable payment histories sometimes choose to manage child support payments privately. However, DCSE services are available to any parent with a Virginia child support order, regardless of income, and are free.

The primary advantage of going through DCSE is the **official payment record** — every payment is documented, disputes about payment history are resolved by the record, and DCSE handles enforcement automatically when payments stop. The disadvantage is that DCSE cases are subject to federal reporting and the enforcement machinery is less flexible than private arrangements.

## How Support Is Collected and Enforced

### Income withholding orders

Virginia law requires that child support orders include an **income withholding order (IWO)** — unless both parties agree otherwise in writing. The IWO is sent to the paying parent's employer, who withholds the support amount from each paycheck and remits it to the state disbursement unit, which then disburses it to the custodial parent.

Income withholding is the most reliable and efficient collection method. It removes the burden from the custodial parent of chasing payments and creates an automatic, documented record. If the paying parent changes jobs, a new IWO can be issued to the new employer — often within days, through the National Directory of New Hires.

Self-employed parents cannot be subject to employer withholding. For these parents, alternative enforcement tools (bank levies, tax intercepts, contempt) are more commonly needed.

### State and federal tax refund intercept

If a parent is behind on child support, DCSE and the federal government can intercept state and federal tax refunds and apply them to arrears. The **Federal Tax Refund Offset Program** and **Virginia Tax Refund Intercept Program** are among the most effective enforcement tools for parents who receive meaningful tax refunds.

For cases involving families on public assistance, the threshold for intercept is $150 in arrears. For non-public-assistance cases, the threshold is $500 in arrears for federal intercept and lower for state intercept.

### Bank levies and liens

DCSE can place a **lien on a non-paying parent's bank accounts** and levy the account to satisfy arrears — without a separate court order. This tool can be used against checking and savings accounts, investment accounts, and other financial assets.

A **lien** can also be placed on real property owned by a parent with significant arrears, which must be satisfied before the property can be sold or refinanced.

## Consequences for Non-Payment

The consequences for failing to pay child support in Virginia are extensive and escalating. Virginia has one of the more aggressive enforcement frameworks in the country, driven in large part by the federal requirements of Title IV-D.

### Contempt of court

Failure to pay court-ordered child support is **contempt of court**. Virginia Code § 16.1-278.16 authorizes courts to hold a non-paying parent in contempt, which can result in:

- Fines
- Payment of the custodial parent's attorney's fees
- A purge amount — a lump sum the non-paying parent must pay to "purge" the contempt
- **Incarceration** until the purge amount is paid or the contempt is otherwise resolved

Civil contempt in child support cases is not punitive — it is coercive. The parent holds "the keys to the jail": they are released when they comply. But incarceration is a real and regularly used consequence in Virginia for willful non-payment, particularly for parents who have the ability to pay but refuse.

Criminal non-support under Virginia Code § 20-61 can result in **felony charges** when a parent willfully fails to provide child support and the arrears or duration of non-payment meet the statutory threshold. A felony conviction for non-support carries potential prison time and a permanent criminal record.

### Driver's license suspension

Virginia Code § 63.2-1937 authorizes DCSE to certify non-paying parents to the **Department of Motor Vehicles for license suspension** when they are at least 90 days delinquent or owe $5,000 or more in arrears. The DMV then suspends the driver's license.

A suspended driver's license creates a cascading problem: it affects the ability to commute to work, maintain employment, and — in some occupations — hold professional licenses that require a valid driver's license. The license suspension remains in place until the parent enters a payment plan with DCSE or pays the arrears.

### Professional and occupational license suspension

Under Virginia Code § 63.2-1940, DCSE can certify non-paying parents to **licensing boards** for suspension of professional and occupational licenses. This covers:

- Law licenses (Virginia State Bar)
- Medical and nursing licenses
- Real estate licenses
- Contractor licenses
- Hunting and fishing licenses
- Commercial driver's licenses

The potential for loss of a professional license — and with it, the ability to earn the income needed to pay support — creates a powerful incentive for compliance, and also illustrates why addressing child support arrears early and proactively is far better than allowing them to compound.

### Passport denial and revocation

Under federal law (**42 U.S.C. § 652(k)**), the U.S. Department of State will deny, revoke, or restrict the passport of any parent who owes more than **$2,500 in child support arrears**. Virginia reports qualifying obligors to the federal Office of Child Support Services, which transmits the list to the State Department.

This can effectively prevent international travel for business or personal reasons — and in some cases, prevent a parent from returning to the United States if their passport is revoked while they are abroad.

### Credit reporting

DCSE reports child support arrears to the three major credit bureaus (Equifax, Experian, TransUnion) when a parent owes a threshold amount. This creates a negative entry on the credit report that affects:

- The ability to obtain a mortgage or refinance a home
- Auto loan approvals and interest rates
- Credit card applications
- Rental housing applications (many landlords run credit checks)
- Employment background checks in some fields

The credit reporting follows the parent and can take years to resolve even after arrears are paid, as the history of delinquency remains on the credit report.

### Federal prosecution: CSRA

For parents who cross state lines to avoid child support, the **Child Support Recovery Act (CSRA)** and the **Deadbeat Parents Punishment Act** provide federal criminal jurisdiction. A parent who willfully fails to pay child support for a child in another state is subject to **federal prosecution** when:

- Arrears exceed $5,000, or
- The parent travels to another state to avoid payment, or
- Non-payment has continued for more than one year

Federal child support crimes are prosecuted in U.S. District Court and can result in federal imprisonment.

### Seizure of assets

Beyond bank levies, DCSE can pursue:

- **Seizure of lottery winnings** — Virginia intercepts lottery prizes to satisfy child support arrears
- **Seizure of workers' compensation benefits** — A portion of workers' comp payments can be redirected to child support
- **Attachment of unemployment benefits** — Up to 50% of unemployment compensation can be withheld for child support
- **Attachment of federal payments** — Federal contractor payments, federal employee wages, and federal retirement benefits can all be reached through the federal income withholding framework

### The compounding problem of arrears

Unpaid child support accumulates as **arrears** — a legal debt that grows over time. Virginia Code § 20-78.2 provides that child support arrears accrue interest at the judgment rate (currently **6% per annum** for most judgments). This means a parent who falls significantly behind does not simply owe the missed payments — they owe the missed payments plus years of compound interest.

Critically, **arrears cannot be retroactively reduced or discharged**. Unlike other civil debts, child support arrears in Virginia:
- Cannot be eliminated in bankruptcy (they are specifically excepted from discharge under the Bankruptcy Code, 11 U.S.C. § 523(a)(5))
- Cannot be waived by the custodial parent without court approval
- Continue to accrue interest while unpaid
- Follow the non-paying parent indefinitely — there is effectively no statute of limitations on collection of child support arrears once reduced to judgment

This permanence is by design: child support arrears represent money that should have gone to support a child and did not. The law treats non-payment seriously and provides no easy escape.

## Interstate Child Support

When parents live in different states, child support jurisdiction and enforcement are governed by the **Uniform Interstate Family Support Act (UIFSA)**, which Virginia has adopted (Virginia Code §§ 20-88.32 et seq.).

### Which state has jurisdiction

The state that issued the original child support order retains **continuing exclusive jurisdiction** over that order as long as the child or at least one parent remains in that state. Other states must register and enforce the order but cannot modify it.

If the child and both parents all move out of the state that issued the original order, any one of the new states of residence can seek transfer of jurisdiction to modify the order.

### Registering an out-of-state order

A custodial parent who moves to Virginia with an out-of-state child support order can register that order with a Virginia court. Once registered, Virginia courts can enforce it using all of Virginia's enforcement tools — income withholding, contempt, license suspension, and the rest.

DCSE handles interstate enforcement for Virginia IV-D cases through coordination with the other state's IV-D agency, making it possible to pursue a non-paying parent who lives in another state without the custodial parent needing to hire an attorney in that state.

## Taxes and Child Support

Child support is treated neutrally for federal and Virginia income tax purposes:

- **Child support payments are not deductible** by the paying parent
- **Child support received is not taxable income** to the receiving parent
- Child support is entirely separate from the **dependency exemption** and the **child tax credit**, which are allocated by the parenting plan or court order — typically to the custodial parent unless the order specifies otherwise

### Dependency exemption and child tax credit

The federal child tax credit and dependency exemption default to the custodial parent. However, the custodial parent can sign **IRS Form 8332** to release the exemption to the non-custodial parent for a specific year or for multiple years. This is a valuable negotiating point in settlement — particularly when the non-custodial parent is in a higher tax bracket and gains more benefit from the exemption.

Any agreement on how to allocate the dependency exemption and child tax credit should be incorporated into the parenting plan or separation agreement to avoid disputes.

### Tax refund intercept and the Earned Income Tax Credit

If a non-paying parent's federal tax refund is intercepted for child support arrears, this includes any Earned Income Tax Credit (EITC) in the refund. The intercept is applied to arrears before any portion is disbursed to the non-paying parent.
