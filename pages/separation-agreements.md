# Separation Agreements in Virginia

A separation agreement — formally called a **Property Settlement Agreement (PSA)** or **Marital Settlement Agreement (MSA)** — is a legally binding contract between spouses that resolves the financial, parental, and logistical issues of their divorce outside of court. A well-drafted agreement is one of the most powerful tools in a Virginia divorce: it gives both parties control over the outcome, reduces litigation costs, and produces a result tailored to their specific circumstances rather than a judge's generalized assessment.

Understanding what goes into an agreement, what happens after it is signed, and the realistic timeline from separation to a final signed agreement is essential for anyone navigating this process.

## What a Separation Agreement Is

A separation agreement is a **private contract** — not a court order — that the parties negotiate and sign voluntarily. It is enforceable as a contract under Virginia contract law independent of the divorce proceeding. Once signed, it binds both parties to its terms.

When a Virginia court incorporates the agreement into the final divorce decree, it becomes a **court order** as well as a contract. At that point it is enforceable through contempt proceedings in addition to contract remedies — giving it the full weight of a judicial order.

### What a separation agreement is not

- It is not a divorce. The parties remain legally married until the court enters the Final Decree of Divorce, regardless of whether they have signed an agreement.
- It is not automatically a court order. It becomes a court order only when incorporated into the final divorce decree.
- It is not a substitute for legal advice. Each party should have independent legal counsel review the agreement before signing. An agreement signed without counsel is not automatically invalid, but the risks of unfavorable terms, missed issues, and later unenforceability are substantially higher.

### Merger vs. incorporation

When a separation agreement is incorporated into the divorce decree, the parties can choose whether it **merges** into the decree or is **incorporated but not merged**:

- **Merger:** The agreement loses its independent existence as a contract and becomes purely a court order. It can be modified by the court under the standards for modifying court orders.
- **Incorporation without merger:** The agreement retains its independent existence as both a contract and a court order. The parties can enforce it either as a contract (breach of contract action) or through the court (contempt). Provisions may be more difficult for either party to modify unilaterally.

The distinction matters most for support provisions, which survive as independent contracts if not merged. Standard practice in Virginia is to incorporate the agreement without merger for most provisions, preserving both enforcement mechanisms.

## What Is Included in a Separation Agreement {#what-is-included}

A comprehensive Virginia separation agreement addresses every aspect of the parties' financial and parental relationship. Missing provisions create gaps that become disputes later. The following are the core categories.

### 1. Real Property

**The marital home and all other real estate** must be addressed:

- Which spouse keeps the property (if any), or that it will be sold
- If one spouse keeps it: the deadline and mechanism for the other spouse to be removed from the deed (quitclaim deed executed at or before closing), and the deadline and mechanism for removing the other spouse from the mortgage (refinancing within a specified period, typically 90–180 days after the divorce is final)
- What happens if the keeping spouse fails to refinance within the deadline (right of the departing spouse to list the property for sale, or other remedy)
- If sold: the listing timeline, how the listing price and agent are selected, how price reductions are decided, what happens if the parties cannot agree, how closing costs are split, and how net proceeds are divided
- Deferred sale provisions (if applicable): who lives in the home, who pays the mortgage, taxes, insurance, and maintenance, how those payments affect the eventual equity split, and what triggers the eventual sale
- Rental properties: who manages them, how income is split, whether they are sold or transferred
- Any property located outside Virginia: may require additional state-specific instruments

### 2. Personal Property

**Tangible personal property** — furniture, vehicles, artwork, jewelry, collections, tools, electronics, and household goods — should be addressed:

- A schedule of who keeps specific items of significant value
- A general provision for the division of household goods already physically separated
- Vehicles: who keeps each vehicle, how the title is transferred, who assumes any associated loan, deadline for title transfer
- High-value items that require appraisal (art, jewelry, collectibles, wine collections)
- Sentimental property and family heirlooms that may have limited market value but significant personal importance

Courts frequently spend significant time on personal property disputes that could have been resolved in the agreement. The more specifically the agreement addresses personal property, the fewer post-agreement disputes arise.

### 3. Financial Accounts

**All bank and investment accounts** must be accounted for:

- Checking and savings accounts: which spouse keeps which account, deadline for removing the other spouse's name or closing joint accounts, how any remaining balances are divided
- Investment accounts (taxable brokerage accounts): who keeps each account, how the account is split if divided (in-kind transfer of shares vs. liquidation and cash distribution), deadline for completing the transfer
- Health savings accounts (HSAs) and flexible spending accounts (FSAs)
- Cryptocurrency holdings: identification, valuation methodology, and transfer mechanism (cryptocurrency transfers require specific wallet information and should be handled carefully)
- Any other financial accounts

**A note on joint accounts:** Joint accounts should generally be separated as soon as possible after the agreement is signed and ideally before. Each party having access to joint accounts during the separation period creates ongoing risk of unauthorized withdrawals.

### 4. Retirement Accounts and Pensions

Retirement assets require detailed treatment:

- **401(k), 403(b), TSP accounts:** Which portion (if any) of each account is divided, whether a Qualified Domestic Relations Order (QDRO) will be used, the coverture fraction or dollar amount to be transferred, who is responsible for preparing the QDRO and bearing the cost of the plan administrator's review fee, and the deadline for submission
- **IRAs and Roth IRAs:** How the marital portion is calculated, the mechanism for transfer (direct transfer incident to divorce under IRC § 408(d)(6)), and who bears the transfer costs
- **Defined benefit pensions:** Whether divided by deferred distribution (QDRO required) or present value offset against other assets, the coverture fraction, survivor benefit elections, and who prepares the QDRO
- **Military retirement:** USFSPA elections, direct payment from DFAS vs. payment from the service member directly, survivor benefit plan (SBP) elections and who pays the premium
- **Stock options, RSUs, and deferred compensation:** How unvested awards are handled, vesting schedules, and the tax treatment of transfers

**The QDRO problem:** One of the most common post-agreement failures is a QDRO that is never drafted, drafted incorrectly, or never submitted. The agreement should specify which party is responsible for preparing the QDRO, set a deadline (typically 60–90 days after the divorce is final), require cooperation from both parties, and include a remedy if the QDRO is not completed (e.g., the plan participant bears the cost of any delay in the alternate payee's benefit).

### 5. Debts and Liabilities

Every known debt must be addressed:

- **Mortgage:** Assigned to the spouse keeping the property; indemnification provision protecting the departing spouse from liability if the keeping spouse fails to pay
- **Vehicle loans:** Assigned with the vehicle; indemnification
- **Credit card debt:** Which party pays which balance, deadline for payoff or transfer, indemnification against the other party's assigned debt
- **Student loans:** Generally treated as the separate debt of the borrower, but any loans taken during the marriage that benefited both parties may have a marital component
- **Medical debt:** How incurred, when, and for whom
- **Personal loans:** From family members, friends, or lenders
- **Tax liabilities:** Any existing IRS or state tax assessments, audit risk, who bears the cost of any additional tax liability arising from joint returns
- **Business debts:** Particularly for sole proprietors or partners in closely-held businesses

**The indemnification clause** is critical for every debt assigned to one spouse that is also in the other's name. The indemnification provision requires the assigned spouse to hold the other harmless from the debt — and to reimburse the other if the creditor successfully pursues them. This does not bind the creditor, but it provides a contractual and court-order remedy against the non-paying spouse.

### 6. Spousal Support

The agreement must make an explicit decision on spousal support — one of three choices:

- **Spousal support award:** Amount, duration, termination events, modifiability, escalation provisions, life insurance requirements, and whether support is taxable/deductible (governed by the date of execution relative to January 1, 2019)
- **Waiver of spousal support:** One or both parties explicitly waive any claim to spousal support, present and future. A waiver must be clear and unequivocal to be effective.
- **Reservation of spousal support:** The court retains jurisdiction to award support in the future if circumstances change, without awarding any support currently. If neither a waiver nor a reservation is included, the court loses jurisdiction to award support after the divorce is final.

**The reservation option is critically important for the receiving spouse.** A spouse who signs an agreement waiving support without understanding that they are permanently relinquishing that right — regardless of future circumstances — can face devastating financial consequences if their health deteriorates, they lose employment, or other changed circumstances arise.

### 7. Child Custody and Visitation

If the parties have minor children, the agreement must include a comprehensive parenting plan:

- **Legal custody:** Joint or sole; how joint legal custody decisions are made; what happens when joint legal custody parents disagree (tiebreaker provisions, mediation requirements before court)
- **Physical custody:** Primary, shared, or split; the regular parenting schedule (school year and summer)
- **Holiday and school break schedule:** Specific provisions for each major holiday (Thanksgiving, Christmas Eve, Christmas Day, New Year's Eve/Day, spring break, school breaks, three-day weekends), how holidays rotate year to year, and which parent has priority in which years
- **Summer schedule:** Duration of each parent's summer parenting time, notice requirements for vacation planning, coordination with the school-year schedule
- **Transportation:** Who drives, who pays for transportation, where exchanges occur, what happens when a parent is late to an exchange
- **Communication:** How parents communicate with each other about the child, how each parent communicates with the child during the other's parenting time (video calls, phone calls, frequency, timing)
- **Notification requirements:** How far in advance each parent must notify the other of travel with the child, medical appointments, school events, and changes in residence
- **Relocation provisions:** Required notice period, consent requirements, what happens if consent is withheld
- **Right of first refusal:** Whether a parent with the child must offer the other parent the opportunity to care for the child before using a third-party caregiver, and if so, for what duration of absence
- **Dispute resolution:** Mediation requirements before returning to court for modification, which mediator or how one is selected, who pays mediation costs

### 8. Child Support

- The current guideline calculation, using both parents' current incomes
- The agreed monthly amount (which may be at, above, or below guidelines with written justification)
- How health insurance premiums for the child are handled
- How uninsured medical, dental, and vision expenses are split
- Work-related childcare costs and how they are shared
- Any extraordinary expenses (private school tuition, special needs expenses, extracurricular activities) and how they are addressed
- Income withholding order (IWO) designation — whether support is paid through the state system or directly
- The mechanism for adjustment if one parent's income changes significantly, or if childcare costs change
- Post-secondary education provisions (voluntary only — courts cannot order college support in Virginia)

### 9. Health Insurance and COBRA

- Which parent covers the children on their employer health plan, and how long
- What happens if the covering parent loses coverage
- The other spouse's health insurance: if they have been covered as a dependent on the other's employer plan, **COBRA** continuation coverage is available for up to 36 months following a divorce (qualifying life event). The agreement should address who pays the COBRA premium (typically the departing spouse, but negotiable)
- Notification: the employer spouse must notify the health plan administrator within 30 days of the divorce, triggering the COBRA election period

### 10. Life Insurance

- Existing policies: who keeps which policy, how beneficiary designations are changed, whether either party must maintain coverage for the other's benefit (particularly if support is being paid)
- Required coverage: if support is ordered or agreed, requiring the payor to maintain a life insurance policy naming the recipient as beneficiary, with a minimum death benefit tied to the remaining support obligation; who pays the premium; how the recipient verifies coverage annually
- Children: naming the children as contingent beneficiaries, or a trust for their benefit

### 11. Taxes

- Which years' joint returns have been filed, and which remain to be filed
- Agreement on how to file for any pending tax year (joint or separate) and how any refund or liability is split
- Dependency exemptions and child tax credits: who claims the children in which years, whether Form 8332 will be signed, and the process for exchanging signed forms annually
- Responsibility for any tax liability arising from an audit of a prior joint return
- IRS Innocent Spouse provisions: if one party has been hiding income or taking improper deductions, protective provisions for the other party
- Liability for taxes triggered by the property division itself (e.g., distribution of retirement funds, capital gains from asset sales)

### 12. Business Interests

- Valuation of any closely-held business, professional practice, or partnership interest
- Which spouse retains the business interest
- The mechanism for any buyout of the other spouse's marital share (lump sum, installments, offset against other assets)
- Non-disparagement and non-interference provisions preventing the departing spouse from interfering with the business or its customer relationships
- Non-compete provisions (if appropriate and enforceable under Virginia law)
- Handling of business debts and personal guarantees

### 13. Estate Planning and Beneficiary Designations

The agreement typically addresses the obligation to update:
- Wills and trusts (divorce itself revokes bequests to a former spouse under Virginia Code § 64.2-412, but agreements often make this explicit)
- Beneficiary designations on retirement accounts, life insurance, and annuities
- Powers of attorney and healthcare directives

### 14. General Provisions

Every separation agreement includes boilerplate provisions that are not merely formalities — they have real legal significance:

- **Entire agreement clause:** The written agreement is the entire agreement; no side deals or oral promises are enforceable
- **Modification clause:** Requires any modification to be in writing and signed by both parties
- **Voluntary execution:** Each party affirms they signed freely, without duress or undue influence, with full understanding of the terms
- **Independent legal advice:** Each party affirms they had the opportunity to consult with independent counsel (even if they chose not to)
- **Full disclosure:** Each party represents they have fully and accurately disclosed all assets and liabilities
- **Governing law:** Virginia law governs the agreement
- **Severability:** If one provision is found unenforceable, the rest of the agreement remains in effect
- **Indemnification (global):** Each party agrees to indemnify and hold the other harmless from any breach of their obligations under the agreement
- **Attorney's fees:** In the event of a breach, the breaching party pays the other's attorney's fees and costs incurred in enforcement

## What Is Not Included in a Separation Agreement {#what-is-not-included}

Not everything the parties might want to address belongs in a separation agreement — and some provisions that parties try to include are unenforceable.

### Provisions that cannot be included

**Child custody and support waivers:** Parents cannot permanently waive a child's right to support in a separation agreement. Courts retain jurisdiction to modify child support and custody regardless of what a private agreement says, because these issues belong to the child, not the parents. An agreement that purports to permanently fix child support at zero, or to waive custody rights irrevocably, will not be enforced.

**Provisions that violate public policy:** Courts will not enforce provisions that are unconscionable, illegal, or contrary to Virginia public policy — for example, provisions penalizing a spouse for seeking court modification of custody, or requiring a parent to forfeit custody rights under specified circumstances.

**Provisions regulating future conduct unrelated to the divorce:** Post-divorce lifestyle provisions — who the parties may date, where they may live unrelated to the children, what they may say about each other publicly — are generally not enforceable through the court's contempt power even if included in the agreement.

**Provisions about third parties:** The agreement binds only the parties. It cannot bind creditors, employers, or other third parties to its terms.

### Provisions that are included but often misunderstood

**Non-disparagement clauses:** Provisions prohibiting the parties from saying negative things about each other are included in many agreements, particularly those with children. They are enforceable as contract terms — a violation can support a contempt motion or breach of contract claim — but they are difficult to police in practice.

**Social media provisions:** Increasingly, agreements include provisions about what each party may post about the other or the divorce on social media. These are enforceable as contractual obligations but do not prevent third parties from posting.

**Mediation-before-litigation clauses:** Requiring the parties to mediate any disputes before returning to court is enforceable and a good practice — it reduces unnecessary litigation over minor disputes.

## How the Agreement Is Executed {#how-the-agreement-is-executed}

A Virginia separation agreement must be:

1. **In writing** — oral agreements are not enforceable as separation agreements
2. **Signed by both parties** — each party's signature must appear on the agreement
3. **Acknowledged before a notary public** — each party must sign in the presence of a notary, who acknowledges that the party appeared before them and signed the document

Without notarization, the agreement cannot be incorporated into a Virginia divorce decree. A signed but unnotarized agreement may be enforceable as a contract but cannot serve as the basis for an uncontested divorce on the papers.

**Each party should sign a separate acknowledgment** before a notary — typically the parties sign in separate locations, with their own attorneys or a notary available. Joint signings are possible but less common.

A copy of the fully executed agreement should be retained by each party and their attorneys immediately upon signing.

## Property Distribution After the Agreement Is Signed {#property-distribution-after-signing}

Signing the agreement does not automatically transfer property — it creates the **legal obligation** to transfer. The actual transfer of each category of property follows on its own timeline, which the agreement should define.

### Financial accounts: days to weeks

Bank accounts, investment accounts, and similar financial assets are typically transferred within days to a few weeks of signing. The process:

- Joint bank accounts: one party opens a new individual account, the balance is split per the agreement, and the joint account is closed
- Investment accounts: the broker or custodian (Fidelity, Vanguard, Schwab, etc.) requires a copy of the signed agreement or a court order to process a transfer. A direct transfer of shares (in-kind transfer) is generally preferable to liquidation, as it avoids triggering capital gains. Most brokers complete in-kind transfers within 5–10 business days after receiving proper documentation.
- Retirement accounts: transfers **cannot occur** until the divorce is final and a QDRO or court order is in place. The QDRO must be submitted to the plan administrator after the divorce decree is entered (not before). Plan administrators have their own review periods — anywhere from a few weeks to several months depending on the plan.

### Vehicles: days to a few weeks

Vehicle title transfers require:
- Signing the existing title over to the receiving party (seller signs the back of the title), or
- Executing a separate bill of sale
- The receiving party then applies for a new title at the Virginia DMV

**Loan assumption:** If there is an outstanding loan on the vehicle, the receiving spouse must be approved by the lender to assume the loan, or the loan must be refinanced into the receiving spouse's name. Lenders are not automatically bound by the agreement — they must consent to any assumption. If the loan cannot be refinanced or assumed, the vehicle may need to be sold.

The agreement should set a deadline — typically 30–60 days — for vehicle title transfers to be completed.

### Real property: 30–90 days typically, sometimes longer

Real property transfers are the most complex and often take the longest.

**If one spouse keeps the home:**
- A **quitclaim deed** is prepared transferring the departing spouse's interest to the keeping spouse. The deed must be signed, notarized, and recorded with the clerk of the circuit court in the county or city where the property is located. Recording fees and transfer taxes apply.
- The **mortgage refinance** must be completed to remove the departing spouse from personal liability. This requires the keeping spouse to qualify for a new loan individually, obtain an appraisal, and complete a normal loan underwriting process. Depending on the lender and market conditions, refinancing typically takes 30–60 days from application to closing.
- The agreement should set a deadline for refinancing — typically 90–180 days after the divorce is final — and specify what happens if the deadline is missed (the home is listed for sale, or the departing spouse has other remedies).
- The quitclaim deed is typically delivered and recorded at or shortly after closing on the refinance. Signing it before the refinance is completed creates risk for the departing spouse: they lose their interest in the property but may still be on the mortgage.

**If the home is sold:**
- The listing and sale process follows normal real estate timelines: listing preparation (1–4 weeks), marketing (varies widely by market), accepted offer, due diligence and financing contingencies (typically 30–45 days), and closing.
- In a cooperative sale, this process typically runs 2–4 months from the decision to list to receiving proceeds at closing.
- Net proceeds are distributed to the parties at closing per the agreement.

**Deferred sale:**
- The home remains in both parties' names (as tenants in common) until the trigger event. Each party has a continuing ownership interest.
- Detailed provisions should govern who is responsible for the mortgage, property taxes, homeowner's insurance, and maintenance during the deferred period, and how expenses affect the equity division at eventual sale.
- Both parties must cooperate in the sale when triggered — the agreement should have provisions for what happens if one party refuses, including appointment of a Special Commissioner.

**Rental properties and investment real estate:**
- Transferred similarly to the marital home through quitclaim deed and, if applicable, refinancing
- If sold, the sale proceeds and timeline follow the agreement's provisions
- Tenant issues (existing leases, security deposits, management responsibilities) must be addressed in the agreement

### Business interests: varies widely (weeks to months)

Transferring or buying out a business interest may require:
- A formal business valuation if the parties did not agree on value
- Execution of a stock transfer agreement, LLC membership interest assignment, or partnership interest assignment
- Approval of other owners or partners (if the business has governing documents requiring consent to transfers)
- Lender consent if the business has loans with change-of-ownership provisions
- Regulatory notice or filing in some licensed industries

Lump-sum buyouts are paid per the timeline specified in the agreement. Installment buyouts require ongoing compliance and should include default provisions.

### Retirement accounts: 60 days to 6+ months after divorce is final

Retirement account transfers cannot be initiated until the divorce decree is entered. After entry:

1. The QDRO (or IRA transfer order) must be drafted by an attorney who specializes in QDROs
2. The draft QDRO is typically submitted to the plan administrator for **pre-approval review** before being submitted to the court. Most plan administrators have model QDRO language they prefer.
3. The approved QDRO is then submitted to the court for signature by the judge
4. The signed QDRO is submitted to the plan administrator for processing
5. The plan administrator transfers the alternate payee's share into a new account in their name

**Total timeline:** Typically 3–6 months from the date the divorce is final for well-managed cases; up to a year or longer for complex pension plans, governmental plans, or cases where the QDRO requires revision and re-submission.

**The consequence of delay:** Until the QDRO is processed, the alternate payee has no legal right to the retirement account funds. If the account participant dies, takes loans, or withdraws funds in the interim, the alternate payee may have limited recourse. Prompt preparation and submission of QDROs is one of the most important post-divorce tasks.

## Incorporating the Agreement into the Final Decree {#incorporating-into-the-final-decree}

The separation agreement is submitted to the court along with the divorce pleadings and affidavits. The court reviews it for:

- Voluntariness of execution
- Whether custody and support provisions are in the best interests of the children
- Whether the agreement is unconscionable on its face

Courts rarely refuse to incorporate a separation agreement between competent adults who had the opportunity to consult with counsel. However, the court is not simply a rubber stamp — in uncontested divorces, the judge (or commissioner in chancery) reviews the agreement and the supporting affidavits before signing the Final Decree.

Once the Final Decree is entered incorporating the agreement, it is both a contract and a court order. Violations can be pursued through contempt proceedings as well as breach of contract claims.

## Typical Timelines {#typical-timelines}

There is no single timeline — cases vary enormously. The following reflects realistic ranges based on how Virginia divorces typically proceed.

### From separation to signed agreement

**Uncontested, cooperative cases with no significant contested issues:**
- Parties who are in basic agreement and engage experienced attorneys promptly can sometimes reach a fully signed agreement within **4–8 weeks** of retaining counsel, assuming there are no complex assets requiring appraisal or valuation
- Cases involving a home sale, business valuation, or pension actuarial analysis add weeks to months to the negotiation timeline
- Cases where both parties have reviewed and understand the financial picture before retaining counsel move faster than those beginning discovery from scratch

**Moderately contested cases:**
- When parties begin far apart on key issues, a realistic timeline to a signed agreement is **3–9 months**, often following mediation
- Business valuations, competing appraisals, and discovery disputes can extend this timeline significantly

**Highly contested cases:**
- Cases that proceed through full discovery and multiple hearings before a mediated or negotiated settlement may take **12–24 months** or longer before an agreement is signed
- Some cases settle on the courthouse steps before trial — after 18 months or more of litigation

### From signed agreement to final divorce decree

Once a separation agreement is signed, the divorce can be filed (if it has not been already) and the applicable separation period must be completed:

**6-month track** (no minor children + signed agreement):
- Once the 6-month separation period is complete, the uncontested divorce can be filed on the papers
- From filing to final decree: typically **4–10 weeks**, depending on the court's docket and processing time

**1-year track** (any case not qualifying for 6-month):
- From the date of separation: the full year must pass before the divorce can be finalized
- From filing to final decree after the year is complete: typically **4–10 weeks**

**Total timeline examples:**

| Situation | Typical Total Time from Separation to Final Decree |
|-----------|---------------------------------------------------|
| Cooperative, no children, quick agreement | 6–8 months (dominated by the 6-month waiting period) |
| Cooperative, minor children, quick agreement | 12–16 months (dominated by the 1-year waiting period) |
| Moderate negotiation required, no children | 8–14 months |
| Moderate negotiation required, minor children | 14–24 months |
| Contested, eventually settled at mediation | 18–36 months |
| Fully litigated through trial | 24–48 months or more |

The waiting period cannot be shortened. Even if the parties sign an agreement on the day they separate, the 6-month or 1-year separation period still runs from the date of separation to the date the divorce can be finalized.

### What accelerates the timeline

- Both parties engaging attorneys early and cooperatively
- Full financial disclosure at the outset (both parties providing complete financial records immediately)
- A realistic and similar understanding of asset values (appraisals not needed or not disputed)
- Willingness to make reasonable concessions
- A written record of separation (lease agreements, utility bills, USPS change-of-address records) that makes the separation date clear and uncontested
- Prompt preparation of QDROs and deed transfers after signing

### What extends the timeline

- Incomplete financial disclosure, requiring discovery
- Disputed asset values requiring competing appraisals or business valuations
- Contested separation date (requiring evidence and potentially a hearing)
- Unresponsive or uncooperative counsel
- One party's unwillingness to negotiate in good faith
- Complex assets (military pensions, deferred compensation plans, multi-state real estate)
- Changes in circumstances mid-negotiation (job loss, discovery of hidden assets, new domestic violence allegations)
- Delays in obtaining QDRO pre-approval from plan administrators

## Negotiating the Agreement {#negotiating-the-agreement}

### Approaches to negotiation

**Attorney-to-attorney negotiation:** Each party retains counsel; the attorneys negotiate on their clients' behalf, exchanging draft agreement language, financial proposals, and counter-proposals. Most Virginia separations are resolved through this process.

**Mediation:** A neutral mediator helps the parties communicate and work toward agreement. Mediation can occur at any stage — before attorneys are retained, during attorney-to-attorney negotiation that has stalled, or as a last resort before trial. Agreements reached in mediation are then drafted formally by the attorneys.

**Collaborative divorce:** Both parties and their attorneys commit contractually to resolving all issues without court intervention. If the process breaks down, both attorneys must withdraw. Collaborative divorce can produce durable agreements but requires genuine good-faith participation.

**Kitchen table agreements:** Parties who are cooperative and trust each other sometimes negotiate informally and then have attorneys review and formalize the result. This works in simple cases; it creates significant risk in cases with complex assets, significant income disparity, or one party who is more financially sophisticated than the other.

**DIY agreements:** Parties who attempt to draft their own separation agreements without counsel risk missing critical provisions, agreeing to unenforceable terms, waiving rights they did not know they had, and creating ambiguities that produce litigation later. The savings in attorney's fees are often consumed many times over in subsequent disputes about what the agreement means or whether it is enforceable.

### The role of independent counsel

Virginia does not require each party to have independent counsel to execute a valid separation agreement. However:

- An agreement is more likely to be challenged as the product of duress, undue influence, or lack of understanding if one party was unrepresented
- Significant income and asset disparities between the parties increase the risk that a court will scrutinize an unrepresented party's knowing and voluntary agreement to unfavorable terms
- An attorney can identify provisions that may be unenforceable, incomplete, or disadvantageous in ways that are not apparent to a non-lawyer

Even if a party cannot afford full representation, a **limited scope representation** (unbundled legal services) arrangement — where an attorney reviews the agreement before signing without representing the client throughout the negotiation — provides meaningful protection at a fraction of the cost of full representation.

## Modifying a Separation Agreement {#modifying-a-separation-agreement}

Once signed and incorporated into the divorce decree, the agreement can be modified only in limited circumstances.

### Property division provisions

Property division provisions that have been incorporated into the decree are generally **not modifiable** by the court. The parties must comply with what they agreed. If a party fails to comply — fails to execute a deed, refuses to refinance, does not complete a QDRO — the remedy is contempt or a breach of contract action, not modification of the property division.

Provisions that have not yet been performed (e.g., a deed not yet transferred, a QDRO not yet processed) can sometimes be addressed by the court through enforcement mechanisms.

### Support provisions

Spousal and child support provisions can be modified upon a material change in circumstances, unless the agreement explicitly makes them non-modifiable.

**Non-modifiable spousal support:** If the agreement says support is non-modifiable, courts enforce that provision. A party who agreed to non-modifiable support cannot later seek to reduce it because of changed circumstances, absent fraud or misrepresentation in the original agreement.

**Child support:** Always modifiable by the court on a material change of circumstances, regardless of what the agreement says. The children's right to adequate support is not something the parents can waive by contract.

### Custody provisions

Custody and visitation provisions are always subject to modification upon a material change of circumstances, regardless of what the agreement says. Courts retain jurisdiction over children's welfare and cannot be contractually divested of it.

## When Agreements Fall Apart {#when-agreements-fall-apart}

Not every negotiation produces a signed agreement. Common reasons negotiations fail:

**Valuation disputes:** The parties cannot agree on the value of the business, the home, or retirement accounts, and neither is willing to accept the other's number or split the difference.

**Hidden assets discovered mid-negotiation:** One party discovers during financial disclosure that the other has been hiding accounts, understating business income, or misrepresenting asset values. Trust collapses and litigation becomes necessary to compel full disclosure.

**Support impasse:** The parties are too far apart on spousal support amount or duration, and neither is willing to accept the other's position.

**Custody breakdown:** Parents who cannot agree on a parenting schedule after extended negotiation often end up in contested custody litigation. This is the most emotionally damaging failure point and typically the most expensive.

**Changed circumstances:** A significant change during negotiation — one party loses their job, a major asset changes value dramatically, a domestic violence incident occurs — can derail an agreement that was close to final.

**Bad faith:** A party who uses the negotiation process to run out the clock (while depleting assets, establishing a different status quo, or waiting out the other party financially) may never intend to reach an agreement. When good-faith efforts at negotiation have failed, proceeding to litigation is appropriate.

When negotiations fail after a genuine attempt at resolution, the parties typically proceed to discovery and the contested divorce process. In Virginia, even cases that go to trial often settle shortly before or during trial — sometimes on the courthouse steps — when the reality of the process and its costs become concrete.

## Protecting Yourself in the Agreement {#protecting-yourself-in-the-agreement}

**Insist on full financial disclosure before signing.** Both parties should exchange complete financial documentation — tax returns, account statements, retirement statements, business records — before finalizing any agreement. Signing an agreement based on incomplete or inaccurate financial information may be voidable, but the burden of proving it and reopening the matter is on the party who was misled.

**Understand every provision before you sign.** An agreement is a permanent, binding legal document. If you do not understand a provision, ask your attorney to explain it. If you do not have an attorney, retain one before signing. "I didn't know what I was signing" is a difficult argument to make after the fact, particularly when both parties sign an acknowledgment that they reviewed the agreement and signed voluntarily.

**Pay attention to deadlines.** The agreement creates obligations with timelines. A keeping spouse who fails to refinance by the agreement's deadline is in breach. A spouse who fails to remove the other from a joint account by the agreed deadline is in breach. Monitor compliance with your own obligations and document the other party's compliance with theirs.

**Protect yourself on joint obligations.** If the other spouse is responsible for paying a joint debt, monitor that debt. Set up account alerts if possible. The creditor's ability to pursue you does not disappear because the divorce agreement assigns the debt to your spouse.

**Complete the QDRO.** Do not allow retirement account transfers to languish. The alternate payee is not protected until the QDRO is processed. Follow up regularly until you receive written confirmation from the plan administrator that the transfer is complete.

**Update your estate planning.** Immediately after the divorce is final: update your will, revoke prior powers of attorney, update healthcare directives, and change beneficiary designations on life insurance, retirement accounts, and annuities. Virginia law revokes bequests to a former spouse automatically, but beneficiary designations on retirement accounts and insurance policies are governed by federal law and are **not automatically revoked by divorce** — they must be updated manually. A former spouse named as beneficiary on a 401(k) may legally receive those funds even after divorce if the designation is not changed.
