# Spousal Support (Alimony) in Virginia

Spousal support — commonly called alimony — is one of the most discretionary and unpredictable areas of Virginia divorce law. Unlike child support, there is no formula. Courts apply a list of statutory factors to the specific facts of each case, and outcomes can vary significantly between judges and jurisdictions. Understanding what courts actually look at, what behaviors affect an award, and what can terminate or modify support after it is ordered is essential for both the spouse who may pay and the spouse who may receive.

## The Legal Foundation

Spousal support in Virginia is governed by **Virginia Code § 20-107.1**. The statute does not establish a formula. Instead, it directs courts to consider a list of factors and exercise equitable discretion in determining whether support should be awarded, the amount, and the duration.

Courts are not required to award spousal support. Whether it is appropriate depends on the specific facts of the case — most significantly the income disparity between the spouses, the length of the marriage, and each spouse's ability to be self-supporting.

A court may award support **pendente lite** (temporarily, during the divorce proceedings) and then enter a final support award as part of the divorce decree. The two are independent determinations: a temporary support order does not bind the court's final decision, though it often sets a practical baseline.

## Types of Spousal Support

Virginia courts can award several distinct forms of spousal support, depending on the circumstances of the case.

### Temporary Support (Pendente Lite)

Awarded during the pendency of the divorce to maintain both parties' financial stability while the case is litigated. Temporary support is calculated using a formula in many Virginia jurisdictions (see the pendente lite section below) and ends when the final decree is entered.

### Rehabilitative Support

Time-limited support intended to give the receiving spouse time and resources to become financially self-sufficient — through education, job training, gaining work experience, or reestablishing a career. Rehabilitative support has a defined end date or a termination event (e.g., completion of a degree program).

This is the most commonly awarded form of support in short to medium-length marriages where the receiving spouse has marketable skills or the realistic ability to become self-supporting with time.

### Transitional Support

A shorter-term bridge payment to help a spouse transition from the marital standard of living to an independent financial life. Often used in shorter marriages or cases where the receiving spouse has skills but needs time to find appropriate employment.

### Permanent or Indefinite Support

Ongoing support without a defined end date, typically awarded in long marriages where:
- One spouse has been out of the workforce for many years
- Age or health makes re-entering the workforce impractical
- The income disparity is so significant that self-sufficiency is not a realistic goal

"Permanent" support is a misnomer — it is indefinite, not truly permanent. It remains subject to modification and termination upon changed circumstances.

### Lump-Sum Support

A one-time payment rather than periodic payments. Lump-sum support is non-modifiable once paid and terminates automatically — there is nothing to modify or terminate. It can be attractive to paying spouses who want certainty and to receiving spouses who want security without ongoing financial dependence. It may also have different tax treatment depending on how it is structured.

## How Spousal Support Is Calculated

Virginia has **no statutory formula** for final spousal support. Courts exercise broad discretion. However, several practical frameworks influence what courts typically award.

### The Fairfax Formula (and similar approaches)

While not binding law, many Virginia practitioners and courts use the **Fairfax formula** (or similar local approaches) as a starting point for pendente lite support and sometimes as a reference point for final awards:

**Gross income approach:**
> Monthly support ≈ 28% of the payor's gross monthly income minus 58% of the payee's gross monthly income

If this calculation produces a negative number, no support is paid. This formula is most commonly applied at the pendente lite stage, not as a final determination.

Some Virginia jurisdictions use the **Williamson formula** or other variations. These formulas are tools for initial estimation — not binding legal standards. At the final stage, the statutory factors govern.

### What "gross income" means for support purposes

Gross income for spousal support purposes is similar to — but not identical to — gross income for child support:

- Wages, salary, bonuses, and commissions
- Self-employment income
- Investment income (interest, dividends, capital gains distributions)
- Rental income
- Pension and retirement income
- Social Security benefits
- Disability income
- Any other recurring source of funds available to the party

**Earning capacity vs. actual income:** As with child support, courts may impute income to a voluntarily unemployed or underemployed spouse. A spouse who quits a well-paying job shortly before or after separation to reduce support obligations will face scrutiny. Courts look at prior work history, education, skills, and local market conditions to determine what a spouse could earn.

### The realistic-needs analysis

Beyond formulas, courts look at what the receiving spouse actually needs to maintain something approximating the marital standard of living, and what the paying spouse can realistically afford after paying their own reasonable expenses. Support cannot exceed the paying spouse's ability to pay — an award that leaves the paying spouse unable to cover their own basic needs will not stand.

Attorneys in final support hearings typically prepare detailed **monthly expense statements** for both parties — showing their actual or projected monthly expenditures — alongside income documentation. The gap between the receiving spouse's income and their needs, and the paying spouse's income versus their own expenses, frames the court's analysis.

## The Statutory Factors

Virginia Code § 20-107.1(E) lists the factors courts must consider. All factors are weighed together; no single factor controls. Courts are required to make findings on each factor in a contested case.

### 1. The Obligations, Needs, and Financial Resources of the Parties

The starting point is a clear-eyed assessment of each party's current financial picture: income, assets, debts, monthly expenses, and reasonably foreseeable financial obligations. A spouse with significant separate property, retirement accounts, or income-producing assets will receive less (or no) support. A spouse with no independent income and no savings has a greater demonstrated need.

### 2. The Standard of Living Established During the Marriage

Courts consider the lifestyle the couple maintained during the marriage — housing, travel, dining, clothing, charitable giving, leisure activities. Support is not meant to provide an identical lifestyle, but courts try to prevent a dramatic and unjust disparity where one spouse lives lavishly on the marital income while the other cannot meet basic needs.

The marital standard of living matters most in long marriages where one spouse has sacrificed career advancement to support the household or raise children, and where that spouse's ability to independently recreate the marital lifestyle is severely limited.

### 3. The Duration of the Marriage

Marriage length is the single most influential factor in determining both the amount and duration of support. Virginia courts do not have a fixed ratio, but patterns are observable:

- **Short marriages (under 5 years):** Support is rarely awarded, and when it is, the duration is very short. Courts focus on returning each party to their approximate pre-marital financial position.
- **Medium marriages (5–15 years):** Support is more likely, particularly if there is a significant income disparity or one spouse left the workforce. Duration is typically proportional to the marriage length — often ranging from one-third to one-half of the marriage.
- **Long marriages (15–20+ years):** Support is frequently awarded. Indefinite support becomes more likely, particularly if one spouse is older, has been out of the workforce for many years, or has limited ability to become self-supporting.

Marriage length is measured from the date of the marriage ceremony to the date of separation — not to the date of the final divorce decree.

### 4. The Age and Physical and Mental Condition of the Parties

A 30-year-old with a college degree who was married for four years is expected to re-enter the workforce. A 58-year-old who left a career 25 years ago to raise children and support the other spouse's career is not. Courts are realistic about the impact of age on employability and earning capacity.

Physical or mental health conditions that limit a spouse's ability to work — whether the paying spouse or the receiving spouse — are considered. A chronic illness that limits work capacity may increase the need for support; a similar condition in the paying spouse reduces their ability to pay.

### 5. The Circumstances and Factors That Contributed to the Dissolution of the Marriage

Fault — adultery, desertion, cruelty — directly affects spousal support in Virginia. (See the dedicated Fault section below.)

### 6. How and When Specific Marital Property Was Acquired

Property division and support are related — a receiving spouse who obtains significant marital assets in the divorce has greater resources and thus less need for ongoing support. Courts consider the overall financial settlement in the context of the support determination.

### 7. The Earning Capacity, Including the Skills, Education, and Training of the Parties

This factor drives the court's assessment of each party's ability to be self-supporting. Courts look at:

- Current income and employment
- Educational background and degrees
- Work history and transferable skills
- Gaps in employment and the effect on current marketability
- The local job market for the spouse's skills and experience
- Whether additional education or training would materially improve earning capacity, and at what cost and time commitment

A spouse who gave up a professional career to raise children has both a greater claim to support and a more complex employability analysis than a spouse who maintained continuous employment throughout the marriage.

### 8. The Opportunity for, Including the Time and Costs of, Necessary Education or Training

If the receiving spouse could become self-supporting with additional education or job training, courts consider:
- What education or training is realistic given the spouse's age, background, and circumstances
- How long it would take and what it would cost
- Whether the employment prospects after completion would justify the investment
- Whether the receiving spouse is genuinely committed to becoming self-supporting

This factor often supports a **rehabilitative support** award — a fixed period of support tied to the time reasonably needed for the receiving spouse to complete a degree, certification, or training program and find employment.

### 9. The Decisions Regarding Employment, Career, Education, and Parenting Arrangements Made by the Parties During the Marriage, and Their Effect on Present and Future Earning Potential

This factor expressly recognizes the trade-offs couples make during a marriage — one spouse reducing work hours to care for children, declining a promotion to stay in the area for the other spouse's job, supporting the other through graduate or professional school while sacrificing their own career development.

A spouse who made those sacrifices has a stronger claim to support. A spouse who made unilateral decisions to reduce their own income without the other's involvement has a weaker claim.

### 10. The Property Interests of the Parties, Both Real and Personal, Including Both Marital and Separate Property

The receiving spouse's total assets — marital property they receive in the divorce, separate property they bring, inheritance, and other wealth — reduce their need for support. A receiving spouse with a $1 million brokerage account has demonstrably different needs than one with no assets.

### 11. The Provisions Made with Regard to the Marital Property Under Virginia Code § 20-107.3

How the marital estate is divided affects support. A generous property settlement — receiving a larger share of retirement accounts, taking the house with its equity — reduces the receiving spouse's financial need. Courts view the support determination and the property division as interconnected parts of the overall financial resolution.

### 12. The Tax Consequences to Each Party

Since the 2017 Tax Cuts and Jobs Act, **spousal support paid under agreements or orders executed after December 31, 2018 is neither deductible by the payor nor taxable to the recipient** for federal income tax purposes. This is a fundamental change from prior law and affects the economics of spousal support for both parties.

For divorces finalized before 2019 (or agreements executed before January 1, 2019), the old rules — deductible by the payor, taxable to the recipient — continue to apply for that agreement. Modification of a pre-2019 agreement does not automatically change its tax treatment unless the parties elect otherwise.

Courts must consider these tax consequences in setting the amount. What matters is the **after-tax value** of the support award to both parties, not just the nominal amount.

### 13. The Expectation of Retirement Benefits, Including Social Security

Approaching retirement has significant implications for both parties. A paying spouse who is nearing retirement age will see their income drop — a factor that may limit the duration of support or provide grounds for later modification. A receiving spouse's eventual Social Security eligibility (including derivative benefits based on the former spouse's record) is considered part of their future financial picture.

Virginia courts consider the timing of retirement and its effect on both parties' financial situations when setting support in cases involving spouses who are within 10–15 years of typical retirement age.

## How Duration Is Determined

Duration is as important as amount, and Virginia courts have broad discretion on both. No statutory formula exists. The following factors most heavily influence duration:

### Marriage Length

The primary anchor for duration. Rough patterns Virginia practitioners observe:
- Under 5 years: 1–2 years of support, if any
- 5–10 years: 2–5 years of support in many cases
- 10–20 years: Support duration often ranges from one-third to one-half the length of the marriage
- 20+ years: Indefinite support becomes common, particularly where one spouse cannot realistically become self-supporting

These are generalizations, not rules. A 12-year marriage where both spouses maintained careers throughout may result in no support. A 10-year marriage where one spouse sacrificed a professional career to raise three children may result in longer, substantial support.

### The Receiving Spouse's Path to Self-Sufficiency

If the receiving spouse can realistically become self-supporting with a defined period of support, courts often set a termination date tied to that timeline — for example, the expected date of completion of a degree program, plus a reasonable period to find employment. Courts expect good-faith efforts to become self-supporting and may reduce or terminate rehabilitative support if the receiving spouse fails to pursue the plan the support was designed to enable.

### Age and Health

A receiving spouse who is 55 with health limitations faces a fundamentally different employment horizon than one who is 38 and healthy. Courts are realistic about the practical employment prospects for older spouses who have been out of the workforce for decades. This reality pushes toward longer or indefinite awards for older receiving spouses in long marriages.

### Defined Termination Events vs. Open-Ended Awards

Courts may structure support with:
- A fixed end date (e.g., "support terminates on January 1, 2030")
- A defined termination event (e.g., "support terminates when recipient completes a four-year degree program")
- Periodic review (e.g., "support shall be reviewed in three years for modification based on changed circumstances")
- No defined end date (indefinite support, subject to modification or termination upon changed circumstances)

**Reservation of jurisdiction** is an alternative to an immediate support award: the court can reserve jurisdiction to award support in the future if the receiving spouse's circumstances change, without awarding support immediately. This is useful in cases where the receiving spouse is currently employed and doing reasonably well but may face changed circumstances (e.g., a health condition that could worsen, approaching retirement age).

## Fault and Spousal Support

Fault plays a direct and consequential role in Virginia spousal support law — far more so than in most states.

### Adultery: The Absolute Bar

Under Virginia Code § 20-107.1(B), **a court shall not award spousal support to a spouse who has committed adultery**, unless the court finds that denial of support would constitute a **manifest injustice** based on:
- The respective degrees of fault during the marriage, and
- The relative economic circumstances of the parties

This is a high bar. Courts rarely find manifest injustice. A spouse who committed adultery and seeks spousal support faces a strong legal presumption against any award.

**The practical consequence:** If the spouse who would otherwise receive support (typically the lower-earning spouse) committed adultery, they may walk away from a long marriage with no support at all, regardless of the income disparity or their financial need.

**Proving adultery:** The burden is on the spouse asserting the adultery defense. The standard is clear and convincing evidence — higher than a preponderance but lower than beyond a reasonable doubt. Direct proof of sexual intercourse is not required; evidence of opportunity and disposition is typically sufficient.

**Condonation:** If the innocent spouse knew about the adultery and resumed the marital relationship afterward (had sexual intercourse after learning of the adultery), the adultery is "condoned" and cannot be used as a defense to support. This is why the sequence of events — when the innocent spouse learned of the adultery and what happened afterward — matters.

### Other Fault Grounds

Other fault grounds — cruelty, desertion, constructive desertion — are not absolute bars to support, but they are factors courts may consider. A spouse who deserted the marriage may receive less support than they would have otherwise. A spouse who was the victim of cruelty may receive more.

Courts have discretion to weigh how the circumstances contributing to the breakdown of the marriage affect the equities of the support determination.

## Post-Separation Behaviors That Impact Spousal Support

What happens between separation and the final decree — and even after the final decree — can significantly affect whether support is awarded, how much, and for how long.

### Adultery After Separation

**This is one of the most important and counterintuitive points in Virginia spousal support law:** adultery committed **after separation** can still bar a spousal support claim, even if the marriage had effectively ended.

Virginia courts have held that the adultery bar under § 20-107.1(B) applies to adultery that occurs at any time before the final divorce decree — including during the separation period. The parties remain legally married until the divorce is final. Sexual intercourse with a third party during the separation period is legally adultery.

**The practical consequence:** A spouse who is separated and begins a new romantic relationship before the divorce is final may lose their right to spousal support entirely if that relationship involves sexual intercourse. Given that divorce cases can take one to three years to resolve, this creates significant legal exposure for spouses who start new relationships during the separation period.

This is not merely theoretical — it is litigated regularly in Virginia courts, and private investigators hired to document post-separation conduct are a feature of contested Virginia divorce cases.

### Cohabitation During the Separation Period

A spouse who moves in with a new romantic partner during the separation period may face:
- Reduced spousal support (the court may impute income from the cohabiting partner or find that the receiving spouse's needs are reduced)
- Arguments that the relationship involves adultery, triggering the adultery bar
- Arguments that the receiving spouse has achieved financial stability through the cohabiting relationship

### Voluntary Unemployment or Income Reduction

A spouse who voluntarily reduces their income after separation — quits a job, reduces hours, declines a promotion, retires early — in order to reduce their support obligation or increase their claim to support will face judicial scrutiny. Courts impute income based on earning capacity when voluntary unemployment or underemployment is found.

Conversely, a receiving spouse who fails to take reasonable steps to find employment or increase their income after separation may see support reduced on the theory that their need could have been reduced through reasonable effort.

### Dissipation of Assets During the Separation Period

A paying spouse who dissipates marital assets during the separation period — spending down savings, running up debt on a new partner, destroying property — weakens their claim that they cannot afford support and exposes themselves to both an adverse property division and a higher support award.

A receiving spouse who dissipates marital assets may weaken their equitable claim to support.

### Litigation Conduct and Credibility

How each party conducts themselves in litigation affects their credibility with the judge. A spouse who:
- Provides inconsistent financial information
- Attempts to hide income or assets
- Files frivolous motions or takes extreme positions
- Treats the other spouse with open contempt in court filings and hearings

...damages their credibility on every issue the court must decide, including support. Judges have long memories over the course of a case. First impressions formed at pendente lite hearings can color the court's view of a party throughout the litigation.

### Post-Separation Retirement or Job Loss

A paying spouse who genuinely retires or loses their job during the separation period may have strong grounds for reducing their support obligation. The key distinction courts make is between:
- **Voluntary** retirement or job change (may be treated as dissipation or voluntary income reduction)
- **Involuntary** retirement or job loss (genuine changed circumstances that can support modification)

A paying spouse who retires at 65 after a full career has likely made a non-voluntary, permanent life change that courts will recognize. A paying spouse who "retires" at 52 shortly after separation begins will face skepticism.

### New Living Arrangements and Expenses

A paying spouse who takes on a new expensive lifestyle — new home, new car, expensive travel — during the separation period cannot easily claim inability to pay support based on those self-imposed expenses. Courts distinguish between reasonable living expenses and expenses that serve to reduce apparent ability to pay.

### Intercepting or Controlling Joint Finances

In many separating households, one spouse controls joint bank accounts, credit cards, or financial resources. A paying spouse who cuts off the other spouse's access to joint accounts — leaving them without funds for basic living expenses — during the separation creates both a contempt risk and an extremely unfavorable impression at the pendente lite hearing.

Courts expect spouses to maintain reasonable financial arrangements for both parties during the separation period, absent a specific court order otherwise.

## Pendente Lite Support

Temporary support ordered during the pendency of the divorce case is governed by the same legal framework as final support, but courts apply it more quickly and with less evidentiary development.

### How pendente lite hearings work

At a pendente lite hearing, each party submits:
- A sworn financial statement (income and expense disclosure)
- Proof of income (pay stubs, tax returns, W-2s)
- Documentation of major expenses

The hearing is typically 30 minutes to 2 hours. There is limited time for extensive testimony. Judges make decisions based on the financial documents and brief testimony.

### The role of formulas

Many Virginia circuit courts apply a formula at the pendente lite stage because they lack the time for a full statutory factor analysis. The Fairfax formula (28% of payor's gross minus 58% of payee's gross) or similar approaches are common starting points. Judges may adjust based on specific documented needs or circumstances.

### The pendente lite award's influence on the final case

Temporary support orders are not supposed to bind the final determination, but they have significant practical influence:
- They establish a baseline that the parties adapt their finances around
- They create a reference point that attorneys use in settlement negotiations
- They affect credibility — a spouse who has been paying temporary support for a year without issue is in a better position at the final hearing than one who has been in and out of court for contempt

## Modification of Spousal Support

Final spousal support orders can be modified if there is a **material change in circumstances** since the last order. Virginia Code § 20-109 governs modification.

### What constitutes a material change

- A significant involuntary decrease in the payor's income (job loss, reduction in force, disability)
- A significant voluntary increase in the payor's income that was not anticipated at the time of the original order
- A significant change in the recipient's income or financial circumstances
- A change in the recipient's need (health improvement, successful completion of education and re-employment)
- Retirement by the paying spouse at a normal retirement age
- A change in the cost of living, when the order was based on specific economic assumptions

### What does not constitute a material change

- Normal fluctuations in income from year to year
- The paying spouse's mere desire to pay less
- The receiving spouse's preference for more support
- Changes the parties knew were likely at the time of the original order (a planned retirement, for example, that was already contemplated)

### Non-modifiable support

The parties can agree in a separation agreement to make support **non-modifiable** — fixing the amount and duration against any future change in circumstances. Courts will enforce non-modifiable support provisions. A paying spouse who agrees to non-modifiable support takes on the risk that their circumstances will change; a receiving spouse accepts certainty in exchange for the possibility that support might have been modified upward. Non-modifiable support provisions should be entered into deliberately and with full understanding of the trade-offs.

## Termination of Spousal Support

Spousal support automatically terminates upon:

**Death of either party:** Support ends when the paying spouse or the receiving spouse dies. Courts sometimes order life insurance to secure support obligations — requiring the payor to maintain a policy naming the recipient as beneficiary in an amount sufficient to cover the remaining support obligation if the payor dies first. This provision should be included in any support agreement or order when appropriate.

**Remarriage of the recipient:** Under Virginia Code § 20-109(A), spousal support terminates automatically upon the recipient's remarriage. A recipient who remarries must notify the payor immediately. If support payments continue after remarriage, the payor is entitled to recover overpaid amounts.

**Cohabitation:** Virginia Code § 20-109(A) provides that support terminates if the recipient **cohabits with another person in a marriage-like relationship** for one year or more. Cohabitation does not require the parties to be legally married — it requires a relationship of permanence and mutual support consistent with marriage. The payor bears the burden of proving cohabitation.

**A defined end date:** If the order includes a specific termination date or event, support ends when that date or event occurs.

**Court order:** A court can terminate support upon modification proceedings.

## Cohabitation and the Resumption of Spousal Support

The cohabitation termination provision is one of the most litigated issues in post-decree spousal support law.

### What cohabitation means

Virginia courts look for evidence of a relationship that resembles marriage in its permanence, mutual support, and shared domestic life:
- Living together in the same residence
- Holding themselves out publicly as a couple
- Sharing finances, household expenses, and financial obligations
- Sexual relationship (though this alone is not sufficient)
- The duration and consistency of the living arrangement

Occasional overnight stays or a dating relationship does not constitute cohabitation. A permanent shared household with financial integration does.

### The one-year period

Virginia requires cohabitation for **one year or more** before support automatically terminates. The one-year period does not need to be continuous in some courts' interpretations — a pattern of cohabitation that totals one year may qualify.

### Resumption of support after cohabitation ends

**Can support resume if cohabitation ends before one year?** Virginia Code § 20-109(A) provides that if cohabitation ends before the one-year period is reached, and the recipient is not yet re-cohabiting with the same or another person, support may resume — but the payor is not required to pay the amounts that accrued during the period of cohabitation.

Once cohabitation has continued for one year and support has terminated, courts have generally held that support does not resume even if cohabitation subsequently ends.

### Investigating cohabitation

Paying spouses who suspect cohabitation often hire private investigators to document the living arrangements. Evidence used to prove cohabitation includes:
- Surveillance of the recipient's residence showing the third party's regular presence
- Photographs and video of the third party at the residence
- Mail and delivery records at the recipient's address
- Utility and lease records showing both names
- Social media posts showing a shared domestic life
- Financial records showing merged finances
- Testimony of neighbors, friends, or family members

## Enforcement of Spousal Support Orders

A recipient who is not receiving court-ordered support has several enforcement options.

### Income withholding

Like child support, spousal support orders may include income withholding — directing the paying spouse's employer to deduct support payments from their paycheck and remit them to the recipient. This is the most reliable enforcement mechanism for wage earners.

### Contempt of court

A paying spouse who willfully fails to pay court-ordered support is in contempt. The recipient can file a **Motion for Rule to Show Cause** requiring the payor to appear and explain the non-payment. Contempt can result in:
- Fines
- Payment of the recipient's attorney's fees
- A lump-sum purge amount to cure the contempt
- Incarceration until the arrearage is paid

### Judgment and execution

Past-due spousal support is a judgment debt. The recipient can obtain a judgment for arrears and then execute on the payor's assets — bank accounts, investment accounts, real property — through the same mechanisms available for any civil judgment.

### Spousal support and bankruptcy

Unlike child support, spousal support is treated differently in bankruptcy depending on its nature and timing. Under the Bankruptcy Abuse Prevention and Consumer Protection Act of 2005 (BAPCPA):

- **Domestic support obligations** (DSOs) — which include alimony in many cases — are **non-dischargeable** in both Chapter 7 and Chapter 13 bankruptcy
- Arrears that qualify as a DSO survive bankruptcy and remain enforceable
- Some property settlement obligations that are labeled "alimony" but are really property equalization payments may be dischargeable in Chapter 13 bankruptcy if they are not found to be true support obligations

The classification of a payment as "support" versus "property division" in the divorce decree and settlement agreement has significant bankruptcy implications. This is another area where careful drafting of the separation agreement matters enormously.

## Tax Treatment of Spousal Support

The tax treatment of spousal support was fundamentally changed by the **Tax Cuts and Jobs Act of 2017 (TCJA)**.

### Orders and agreements executed after December 31, 2018

For divorces finalized and agreements executed after this date:
- **Spousal support is not deductible** by the payor
- **Spousal support is not taxable income** to the recipient

This eliminates what was previously a significant tax advantage for higher-earning payors — who historically deducted support at their marginal rate and shifted the tax burden to the lower-earning recipient. Under current law, neither party receives a tax benefit or incurs additional tax liability from the support payments themselves.

### Orders and agreements executed before January 1, 2019

Pre-TCJA rules continue to apply:
- Support is **deductible** by the payor (above the line)
- Support is **taxable income** to the recipient

These pre-2019 agreements retain their tax treatment indefinitely unless both parties agree in writing to apply the new rules.

### After-tax value in negotiations

Because support is no longer tax-advantaged for the payor, the **nominal amount of support is also the after-tax cost to the payor**. A payor in the 37% marginal bracket who pays $3,000/month in support now bears the full $3,000 after tax — whereas before 2019, the effective after-tax cost would have been approximately $1,890. This change significantly shifted the economics of support negotiations.

Recipients should also note that support received is tax-free — they keep the full payment. This should inform reasonable expectations about the amount of support a court will award given the paying spouse's ability to pay.

### Lump-sum support and taxes

A lump-sum payment designated as spousal support (rather than property division) is also no longer deductible or taxable under post-2018 law. The distinction between a lump-sum support payment and a property equalization payment remains relevant for other reasons (bankruptcy treatment, enforceability), but not for income tax purposes under current law.

## Negotiating Spousal Support

Most spousal support is resolved through negotiated agreements rather than judicial determinations. The parties have considerably more flexibility in structuring support through agreement than courts have in ordering it.

### What parties can negotiate

- **Amount** — not limited by any formula, can be set at any figure the parties agree to
- **Duration** — can be shorter or longer than what a court might order
- **Modifiability** — parties can agree that support is non-modifiable, partially modifiable (amount only, not duration), or fully modifiable
- **Termination events** — beyond the statutory defaults (death, remarriage, cohabitation), parties can agree to additional termination events (recipient reaching a certain income level, sale of a specified asset, completion of a degree)
- **Escalation clauses** — support that increases over time (e.g., tied to the cost of living or a percentage of any income increase)
- **Reimbursement provisions** — one party reimburses the other for a specific expense (e.g., health insurance premiums) as a form of support
- **Lump-sum versus periodic payments** — a lump sum payment avoids the ongoing relationship and eliminates future modification risk

### The reservation strategy

Where support is not clearly warranted at the time of divorce but circumstances might change (the receiving spouse's health, the paying spouse's retirement income), parties sometimes agree to **reserve spousal support** — the court retains jurisdiction to award support in the future if circumstances change, without awarding any support currently. This preserves the receiving spouse's ability to seek support later without requiring current payment.

If the parties do not reserve support in the separation agreement, the court loses jurisdiction to award it in the future after the divorce is final. **A receiving spouse who waives support without reserving it has no further ability to claim it if circumstances change.**

### Insurance provisions

When one spouse depends on the other for ongoing support, life insurance provisions are essential. If the payor dies without life insurance, the support obligation dies with them. Common provisions include:

- Requiring the payor to maintain a life insurance policy with the recipient as beneficiary
- Setting a minimum death benefit tied to the present value of remaining support obligations
- Requiring the payor to provide annual proof of coverage
- Including the recipient as a co-beneficiary on the policy with the right to pay premiums directly to the insurer if the payor fails to do so
