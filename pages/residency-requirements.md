# Residency Requirements for Divorce in Virginia

Before a Virginia court can grant a divorce, it must have the legal authority to do so. That authority — called **jurisdiction** — depends on meeting residency requirements established under Virginia Code § 20-97. Failing to satisfy these requirements means the court cannot hear the case, even if the marriage broke down in Virginia.

## The Basic Residency Rule

Virginia Code § 20-97 requires that **at least one spouse must have been a bona fide resident and domiciliary of Virginia for at least six months** immediately before the divorce complaint is filed.

Both elements — **residency** and **domicile** — must be satisfied. They are related but legally distinct:

- **Residency** means physically living in Virginia
- **Domicile** means Virginia is your permanent home — the place you intend to remain or return to

A person can be temporarily living in Virginia without being domiciled there (e.g., a student or temporary worker), and in rare cases can maintain a Virginia domicile while temporarily living elsewhere (e.g., a deployed military member). Courts look at the totality of circumstances to determine domicile.

---

## What "Domicile" Means

Domicile is a legal concept that goes beyond just having an address in Virginia. Courts consider:

- **Voter registration** — where you are registered to vote
- **Driver's license** — which state issued your license
- **Vehicle registration** — where your vehicles are registered
- **Tax filings** — which state you file income taxes in as a resident
- **Intent** — statements, actions, and life circumstances that indicate you consider Virginia your permanent home
- **Bank accounts and financial ties** to Virginia
- **Employment** in Virginia
- **Length of physical presence** in Virginia

No single factor is dispositive. A person who has lived in Virginia for years, works here, and votes here is almost certainly domiciled here even if they own property or have ties to another state. A person who recently moved to Virginia and maintains strong ties elsewhere may have difficulty establishing Virginia domicile.

**Why this matters:** If domicile is disputed, the court will hold a hearing to determine whether the residency requirement is met. Filing prematurely — before six months of bona fide residency and domicile — can result in dismissal of the case.

---

## Where to File

The divorce complaint must be filed in the **Circuit Court** of the county or city where either spouse resides. Virginia Code § 20-96 gives the filing spouse the choice of:

- The county or city where the **plaintiff** (filing spouse) resides
- The county or city where the **defendant** (other spouse) resides, if the defendant still lives in Virginia

If the defendant has left Virginia, the plaintiff must file in the jurisdiction where they reside.

**Practical tip:** Circuit court filing fees, local rules, and judicial temperament can vary by jurisdiction. If you have a legitimate choice between two jurisdictions, consulting an attorney about local practices can be worthwhile.

## Special Residency Situations

### Military Spouses

Virginia Code § 20-97 contains a specific provision for **active-duty military members and their spouses**: a person who has been a member of the armed forces and has been stationed in Virginia for at least six months is deemed to satisfy the residency requirement, even if Virginia is not their state of legal domicile.

This means:
- A service member stationed at a Virginia base (e.g., Fort Belvoir, Quantico, Naval Station Norfolk, Langley) for six or more months can file for divorce in Virginia
- The non-military spouse may also rely on this provision if the military spouse has been stationed in Virginia for the required period
- If the military member is deployed overseas, Virginia domicile established before deployment is typically preserved

**Important:** The Servicemembers Civil Relief Act (SCRA) also provides protections for active-duty military members who are served with divorce papers — including the right to request a stay of proceedings while deployed. This can extend the timeline of a case significantly.

---

### Married Outside Virginia

There is no requirement that the marriage took place in Virginia. Virginia courts can grant divorces to couples who were married in another state or country, as long as the residency requirement is met by at least one spouse at the time of filing.

## When One Spouse Moves Out of State During Divorce

This is one of the most practically significant residency questions, and the answer depends heavily on **when** the move occurs relative to the filing.

### Jurisdiction Already Established

If a divorce complaint has already been filed in Virginia and the court has accepted jurisdiction, **one spouse subsequently moving out of state does not strip Virginia of jurisdiction** over the divorce itself.

Virginia courts operate under the principle that jurisdiction, once properly established, continues through the life of the case. The moving spouse cannot defeat the Virginia divorce by relocating — the case proceeds in Virginia regardless of where either party later lives.

This means:
- The divorce will be finalized in Virginia
- Virginia law governs the division of marital property
- Spousal support will be determined under Virginia law
- The spouse who moved must continue participating in the Virginia proceedings (through counsel or by appearing when required) or risk having orders entered against them by default

---

### Jurisdiction Not Yet Established

If one spouse moves out of Virginia **before** a complaint is filed, the remaining spouse can still file in Virginia — provided **they** meet the six-month residency and domicile requirement. Only one spouse needs to qualify.

If the **filing spouse** is the one who moves, and neither spouse meets Virginia's residency requirement at the time of filing, Virginia does not have jurisdiction and the case will be dismissed. The filing spouse would need to establish residency in their new state (typically six months to one year, depending on the state) before filing there.

**Key principle:** Virginia's residency requirement is assessed at the **time of filing**, not at the time of separation or the time of the final hearing.

---

### Effect on Child Custody Jurisdiction

Moving out of state mid-divorce has its most complex consequences when **minor children** are involved. Child custody jurisdiction is governed by the **Uniform Child Custody Jurisdiction and Enforcement Act (UCCJEA)**, which Virginia has adopted under Virginia Code § 20-146.1 et seq.

Under the UCCJEA:

**Home state jurisdiction:** The court with jurisdiction over custody is generally the court in the state where the child has lived for the **six months immediately preceding** the filing of a custody action. This is the child's "home state."

**If a parent moves with the children before filing:**
- If the children have lived in the new state for six months, that state may become the home state and have custody jurisdiction
- Moving children to another state shortly before filing — particularly to gain jurisdictional advantage — is viewed very unfavorably by courts and may constitute wrongful removal

**If a parent moves without the children:**
- Virginia retains home state jurisdiction over custody as long as the children remain in Virginia
- The parent who left cannot obtain custody jurisdiction in the new state simply by moving there

**If Virginia already has custody jurisdiction:**
- Virginia retains exclusive continuing jurisdiction over custody as long as the child or at least one parent remains in Virginia and maintains a significant connection to the state
- The other parent moving away does not, by itself, shift jurisdiction

**Practical consequence:** A parent who is considering relocating out of state during a pending divorce with minor children should consult an attorney immediately. Relocating with children without court approval can constitute wrongful removal and result in serious legal consequences, including being ordered to return the children.

---

### Effect on Service of Process

When a divorce complaint is filed, the defendant must be formally served with the papers. If the defendant has moved out of state, Virginia law still requires proper service — but the method changes.

Under Virginia Code § 20-99.1:
- **Personal service** out of state is permissible and preferred — a process server or sheriff in the other state can serve the defendant
- **Service by publication** is available as a last resort when the defendant's location is unknown, but it limits the court's ability to enter orders regarding property and support (the court can grant the divorce itself, but cannot adjudicate financial claims against an absent defendant without personal jurisdiction)
- **Acceptance of service** — the out-of-state spouse can sign an acceptance of service, waiving formal service requirements, which is common in cooperative cases

The defendant's response deadline is extended when served out of state: **60 days** to respond, rather than the standard 21 days for in-state service.

---

### Effect on the Separation Period

One spouse moving out of state does not pause or reset the separation period — it continues to run. Physical separation across state lines is still separation for purposes of Virginia's no-fault divorce requirements.

However, proving the separation date and the fact of continuous separation becomes more logistically complex when spouses are in different states. Document the separation carefully: keep records of separate addresses, separate finances, and any communications that establish the date separation began.

## When Both Spouses Leave Virginia

If **both spouses** move out of Virginia before a divorce complaint is filed, Virginia courts generally cannot exercise jurisdiction — neither spouse meets the residency requirement.

In this situation:
- Each spouse must establish residency in their new state before filing there
- Most states require six months of residency; some require one year
- The state with jurisdiction will typically be the state where the filing spouse has established residency
- If both spouses are in different states, either may file in their respective state — whoever files first in a state with proper jurisdiction typically controls where the case proceeds

**Exception — Virginia property:** Even if neither spouse qualifies to file for divorce in Virginia, Virginia courts may still have jurisdiction over Virginia real estate in the context of proceedings filed in another state. Division of Virginia real property typically requires some Virginia court involvement or a deed executed voluntarily by both parties.

---

## Interstate Divorce Strategy and Competing Filings

When spouses are in different states, there is sometimes a strategic incentive to file first in the more favorable state. Different states have meaningfully different laws on:

- Property division (community property vs. equitable distribution)
- Spousal support (duration, amount, modification standards)
- Grounds for divorce and their effect on support
- Child support guidelines

**Competing jurisdiction:** If both spouses file in different states around the same time, the courts will apply rules for resolving the conflict. Generally:
- The court that first obtained proper jurisdiction tends to prevail
- Courts communicate and defer to each other under principles of comity
- For custody specifically, the UCCJEA provides a framework for resolving competing state claims

**Virginia's approach:** Virginia courts will decline jurisdiction if they determine another state is a more appropriate forum, or if another state already has jurisdiction. Conversely, Virginia courts will assert jurisdiction if the residency requirements are met, even if the other spouse has filed elsewhere — leaving the parties to litigate which court should proceed.

If you are in a situation where your spouse has filed or may file in another state, time is critical. Consult a Virginia family law attorney immediately to understand your options.
